# 33-Day Residential Construction Curriculum
### We The People Construction / Joon — from zero to running the business

This is your ground-up training course: 33 days, roughly 60-90 minutes of study each, plus a
field exercise you actually go do. Days 1-25 are the core trade + business curriculum, Days 26-27
are the Design Track, and Days 28-33 close out full-lifecycle coverage — ground-up new
construction, two-story structural, SoCal hazard/compliance overlays, the other repair verticals,
the annual maintenance product, and running a job after the sale. It is built entirely around the
real materials you already have — your scope-of-work document, your 2023 Construction Manual,
Josh Bloom's sales training videos, the whiteboard lesson photos, the client-presentation decks,
and the full `knowledge/playbooks/` and `knowledge/sales-training/` corpus. Nothing here is
invented; where a technique or number is stated as "the company way," it's pulled straight from
the Construction Manual, and where a figure is estimated rather than sourced from company
material, it's flagged **[EST]**.

### The sales thread

job — sourced from verified, no-hallucination transcriptions of his own training videos and
whiteboards, not paraphrased sales theory. Six trade days carry a short "🗣️ How Josh sells

## Day 1 — Anatomy of a House: the Load Path

**Why it matters:** Every sales conversation, every scope, every change order comes back to
one question: is this load-bearing, and where does the weight go? If you can't read a house's
skeleton, you can't sell a wall removal, price a beam repair, or know when you legally need an
engineer. This is the single most important day in the course.

### Learn
A house is a machine for carrying weight (gravity load) and resisting sideways push (lateral
load — wind and, in California, earthquake) down to the ground. Everything above is designed,
whether well or badly, to get both kinds of load to the foundation.

**The load path, top to bottom:** Roof sheathing (plywood/OSB) transfers roof loads to rafters
or trusses. Rafters land on a ridge board and exterior walls, or in a truss roof, trusses bear
directly on exterior walls only. Ceiling joists or the truss bottom chord tie the walls together
so the roof doesn't spread them apart — cut that tie without engineering and you get a bowed
roofline. Exterior and interior walls carry roof/floor load down; a wall is "load-bearing" if it
sits under a beam, under a run of joists that break/lap over it, or stacks over a load-bearing
wall below it — a non-load-bearing (partition) wall only holds itself up. Studs in a bearing
wall are typically 2x4 or 2x6 at 16" on center; headers over openings (doors, windows) are
sized beams that pick up the load the studs would have carried and redirect it to the studs
on either side (the "king" and "jack"/trimmer studs). Floor joists (or a slab) carry floor
load to beams or bearing walls below, which carry it to posts, which carry it to footings —
concrete pads sized to spread the point load into the soil without settling. Under a raised
(pier-and-beam) foundation you'll find mudsills bolted to a continuous concrete perimeter
footing, with a crawl space, floor joists resting on beams, and beams resting on 4x4 posts on
concrete piers. Under a slab foundation there's no crawl space — the slab *is* the floor, poured
directly on grade over a vapor barrier, and plumbing runs either under the slab or up through
the attic.

**Lateral (seismic/shear) system:** this is separate from the gravity system and is what keeps
a house from racking sideways or sliding off its foundation in a quake. Key pieces: mudsill
anchor bolts connecting the wood framing to the concrete foundation (code minimum spacing,
typically every 6 feet or per plate count), "cripple walls" — the short stub walls between the
foundation and the first floor in a raised house — which need structural plywood shear
sheathing and metal strapping because a bare cripple wall is the #1 cause of a house falling
off its foundation in an earthquake, and shear walls — walls sheathed in structural plywood
that resist racking, often required at specific locations by an engineer when you cut a big
opening for a great-room remodel.

**What fails and what inspectors look for:** sagging ridgelines (undersized rafters or a
removed ridge beam), bouncy floors (undersized or over-spanned joists), stucco cracks radiating
from window corners (settlement or missing header), a house that "walks" off a hairline crack
at the sill (no bolting/cripple wall bracing), water stains at ceiling-to-wall joints (roof or
flashing failure feeding into framing). Inspectors checking a wall-removal or addition job will
ask: where's the engineer's letter, is the new header sized per span table or stamped
calculation, are shear panels nailed per the nailing schedule (nail spacing is inspected, not
guessed), and is there a continuous load path documented from roof to footing.

**Rough LA cost anchors you'll use constantly:** a straightforward interior partition wall
removal with a new flush beam runs low-to-mid five figures once you include engineering,
permit, and finish repair; foundation bolting/cripple-wall retrofit on an average SFR runs
$3,500-$8,000 depending on perimeter length and crawl space access.

### Field Exercise
Walk a house (yours, a relative's, or a job site) from the outside in. Stand back and sketch
the roofline — where do you think the ridge beam runs, and which exterior walls look like
they're carrying it? Then go inside and find every wall that has a header over a door or
opening wider than 3 feet — press on the drywall/casing above the opening; if you can feel a
solid beam vs. hollow space, note it. If there's a crawl space or attic access, open it (Day 2's
exercise goes deeper here) and look for the mudsill, anchor bolts, and whether the cripple wall
has plywood on it or is bare studs. Take 8-10 photos. Label each with what load you think it's
carrying and where that load goes next.

## Day 2 — How a Job Flows: Lead to Final

**Why it matters:** You are about to run a business where the product is a sequence of people
and paperwork, not just materials. If you don't know the stages cold, you can't quote a
timeline, staff a job, or explain a delay to a homeowner without sounding like you're guessing.

### Learn
Every job — from a $2,000 water heater swap to a $150,000 addition — moves through the same
skeleton, just with more or fewer stops.

**1. Lead.** Comes from a referral, a yard sign, a marketing campaign, or a canvasser. The lead
is worthless until it's a scheduled, confirmed appointment — track show-rate, not lead count.

**2. Appointment (the sit-down).** This is where Josh Bloom's whole system lives (Days 23-24).
The salesperson builds rapport, diagnoses the actual problem (not just the stated want),
measures, and presents a scoped, priced proposal — often same-visit. A good appointment
answers: what exactly is being done, in what order, with what materials, and what it costs.

**3. Measure / site verification.** Sometimes folded into the appointment, sometimes a separate
technical visit for bigger jobs (additions, room conversions) where an estimator or designer
takes exact dimensions, photographs conditions, and checks for red flags — access, existing
electrical panel capacity, roof pitch, slope for drainage — that change the price.

**4. Contract & deposit.** California law caps the down payment at 10% of the contract price or
$1,000, whichever is less, for most home improvement contracts — memorize this, it comes up in
every kitchen-table conversation about "why can't I pay half up front."

**5. Design / material selection.** For remodels: cabinet style, tile, countertop slab,
fixtures. This is also where custom cabinets (3-week lead time per the Manual) vs. semi-custom
(5 days) becomes a scheduling decision, not just a taste decision.

**6. Permit.** Drawings (if required) go to the city or county building department; plan check,
corrections, and issuance can take anywhere from same-day (over-the-counter minor permits) to
several months (room additions needing planning approval). This is usually the single biggest
timeline risk on a job and the #1 thing homeowners underestimate.

**7. Build — in trade sequence.** Demo → rough framing/structural → rough plumbing → rough
electrical → rough HVAC → insulation → drywall → the "trim-out" trades (cabinets, doors,
flooring, tile, paint) → fixtures → punch list. Trades happen in this order because each one
gets buried by the next — you cannot drywall over plumbing that hasn't been inspected.

**8. Inspections.** Tied to the build sequence: foundation/footing inspection before pour,
rough framing + rough plumbing + rough electrical (often combined) before insulation/drywall,
insulation inspection, and final inspection after everything is done and functional. An
inspector who fails a rough inspection stops the job cold until it's corrected and re-inspected
— this is why "we're waiting on inspection" is the most common excuse and also the most common
real delay.

**Smoke & CO alarms — verified at every final, on any permitted work.** CRC R314 requires
interconnected smoke alarms in every bedroom, in the hallway/area outside each group of
bedrooms, and on every level of the dwelling (including basements); CRC R315 requires
carbon-monoxide alarms outside each separate sleeping area and on every level. This isn't
scope-limited to the room you're working in — pulling *any* permit (a water heater swap, a
panel upgrade, a re-roof) puts the whole house's alarm compliance on the table at final
inspection, which makes this the single most common final-inspection correction. Many SoCal
cities accept a CSLB/permit-card self-certification form (check the permit card) in lieu of
the inspector personally testing every alarm — confirm the local jurisdiction's practice
before scheduling the final.

**9. Final walkthrough & punch list.** Homeowner and PM walk the completed job, list anything
incomplete or wrong, contractor corrects it — see Day 33 for the full punch-list methodology
(three-bucket sorting of incomplete/defective/client-requested-change, a real close-out date, and
how an open-ended list strands a retention holdback).

**10. Final payment & close-out.** Final invoice, unconditional lien waiver (the Manual calls
this out for the garage conversion job — use the statutory release forms in California Civil
Code §§8132-8138; a non-conforming waiver may not be enforceable), warranty paperwork.

**11. Maintenance hand-off.** A good close-out doesn't have to end the relationship — Day 32
covers turning the final walkthrough into the first pitch for a recurring annual maintenance
membership, and Day 33 covers the kickoff/scheduling mechanics (`sales-training/11-open-a-job.md`)
that keep every stage above running smoothly on a live job.

Where things blow up: undersized deposits collected illegally (CSLB violation), no engineer
letter when a wall is removed, material on back-order stalling the trim-out sequence, and
change orders that aren't written down (verbal "just add this" costs the contractor money and
credibility every single time).

- Re-read the "Blueprints" and "Garage Conversion" sections of the [Construction Manual](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1tFbXl62IUPVo30wiWaRbDh7B3J-7PgFR/view) (pp. 7, 33-37) — the garage conversion scope is the best example in your materials of a full job broken into demo/construction/rough-in/finish stages with a stated 4-month timeline and daily late-fee clause.

## Day 3 — Permits, Licenses & Who's Who in SoCal

**Why it matters:** Every homeowner conversation eventually hits "do we need a permit for
that?" Get it wrong and you either scare off an easy job or expose the company to stop-work
orders, fines, and liability. This is also where you learn what CSLB license you're actually
operating under and why it matters.

### Learn
**Who's who.** In the City of Los Angeles, the building department is **LADBS** (LA Department
of Building & Safety) — separate from LA County's **Department of Public Works/Building and
Safety**, which covers unincorporated county areas, and separate again from each individual
city's own building department (Santa Monica, Burbank, Glendale, Beverly Hills, Culver City,
etc. all run their own). This matters because "the permit process" is not one process — timeline,
fee schedule, and even what triggers plan-check varies city by city. Always confirm jurisdiction
by address before quoting a permit timeline. Utilities are a separate lane: DWP (LA's water and
power) for electrical service changes and solar interconnection, SoCalGas for gas line work,
and in county areas Southern California Edison/SoCal Water instead of DWP.

**When you need a permit vs. not.** Rule of thumb (always verify per jurisdiction): cosmetic
work — paint, flooring, cabinet refacing, non-structural countertop swap — generally does not
need a permit. Anything touching structure (wall removal, room addition, deck over 30" high,
patio cover attached to the house), anything touching life-safety systems (electrical panel
upgrade or rewire, gas line, water heater replacement, HVAC replacement, re-roof over a certain
scope) generally does. Garage conversions, ADUs, and pool/spa installs always require permits
and usually plan check. When in doubt, pull the permit — an unpermitted job is a liability the
company carries forever and kills a home sale down the line when a buyer's inspector finds it.
New construction and many additions also trigger **CalGreen** (Part 11 of Title 24) — a separate
mandatory green-building code layered on top of the Title 24 *energy* requirements in Days 11, 12,
18, 22, covering construction-waste diversion, low-VOC materials, and water-efficient fixtures;
see Day 30 for the full breakdown. Before quoting any job, also check the parcel for a Very High
Fire Hazard Severity Zone (VHFHSZ)/WUI flag — it changes the required construction spec (Day 30),
not just the paperwork.

**When you need a licensed engineer or architect.** Any structural change — removing/altering a
load-bearing wall, adding a room, foundation repair beyond routine bolting, a new second story —
needs a structural engineer's calculations and stamped drawings, and often a soils/geological
report (called out explicitly in the Manual's Blueprints section). An architect gets involved
for design-heavy projects or when the jurisdiction requires architectural drawings for planning
approval (setbacks, height limits, historic overlay zones — LA has many). A structural engineer
alone is enough for most straightforward wall removals and additions once a floor plan exists.
Three overlays are common enough in SoCal to check by parcel before quoting anything: a hillside
HCR/BHO designation, an HPOZ (Historic Preservation Overlay Zone — a Certificate of
Appropriateness is required for any street-visible exterior change before a permit issues), and
the Coastal Zone (may require a Coastal Development Permit) — see Day 30 for the full
hillside/HPOZ/coastal overlay sequence and what each one adds to cost and timeline.

**Licenses.** In California, any contractor performing work over $500 (labor + materials) must
hold an active **CSLB (Contractors State License Board)** license. The classification that
covers a full-service residential remodeling company like this one is the **B — General
Building Contractor** license, which allows framing, and any two or more unrelated trades on
a single job (the B license cannot be used to take a single-trade job like "just electrical" —
that requires the trade-specific C license, e.g., C-10 electrical, C-36 plumbing, C-20 HVAC —
unless it's part of a larger B-licensed project). CSLB requires the license be tied to a
"Responsible Managing Officer/Employee" (RMO/RME) who has verifiable trade experience, and
mandates a contractor's bond and, once you have employees, workers' comp insurance.

**Insurance basics.** General liability (covers property damage/injury to third parties — this
is what protects you when a sub cracks a customer's driveway), workers' compensation
(mandatory once you have employees — covers injury to your own crew), and often a separate
builder's risk policy for larger renovation/addition jobs (covers the structure itself during
construction against fire, theft, weather). Homeowners will ask for a certificate of insurance
naming them as additionally insured — the Manual's garage conversion scope explicitly commits to
this in writing.

## Day 4 — Foundations & Seismic Retrofit

**Why it matters:** Foundation and bolting work is recurring, high-margin, code-mandated
business in earthquake country — and it's a trust-builder: homeowners who see you correctly
diagnose a foundation issue believe everything else you tell them.

### Learn
**Foundation types in SoCal housing stock:** raised/pier-and-beam (common in older LA homes,
1920s-1960s) with a continuous perimeter concrete footing, mudsill, cripple wall, and floor
joists over a crawl space; and monolithic or stem-wall slab-on-grade (common in newer
construction and additions) where the concrete floor is poured directly over compacted fill and
a vapor barrier. A raised foundation is generally easier and cheaper to retrofit because you
can access it from the crawl space; a slab is far harder to modify after the fact. This day covers
existing-house retrofit and repair only — ground-up foundation types for new construction,
including hillside caisson/pier-and-grade-beam systems where the soils report calls for a deep
foundation, are covered in Day 28.

**Foundation repair (per the Manual's actual scope):** the process is to inspect the crawl
space, raise the house with jacks up to 12" as needed, cover and reinforce pillars/posts with
2x4s and corner brackets, reinforce floor joists/columns to the pillars, lower the house back
down, re-level windows and doors, then patch and paint cracks to match. This is a mechanical,
methodical process — the skill is in diagnosis (which piers are failing, why, and by how much)
more than the fix itself.

**Seismic retrofit (bolting) — this is the highest-volume "structural" job you'll sell.** The
goal is to keep the wood-framed house attached to its concrete foundation and prevent the
cripple wall from racking and collapsing during an earthquake, which is the #1 cause of
total-loss residential seismic damage in California. Per the Manual's real spec: install
**Simpson URFP-10 plates** with a ½" bolt into the foundation and 3/8" lag screw into the sill
plate every 4 feet around the house perimeter; replace or adjust failing concrete piers and 4x4
posts; install **Simpson T-128/T-66 metal straps** on all 4x4 posts; install **Simpson A-35
framing anchors every 32"** around the perimeter. Where the cripple wall itself is undersized or
unbraced, rebuild it with 2x4 blocking top and bottom and 2x4 studs every 16" on center, then
sheathe with ½" structural plywood per code, strapped every 24" between the plywood and the wood
blocking. **Critical field note straight from the Manual:** if the crawl space is less than 18"
high, you physically cannot bolt from inside — you have to remove the exterior stucco and bolt
from the outside, which means the stucco patch/repair has to be included in the bid or you'll
eat that cost. Jacking the house is a separate, not-included scope.

**Sales/scope notes worth knowing:** point income-eligible homeowners to the California
Earthquake Brace + Bolt (EBB) program — a California Residential Mitigation Program grant that
pays up to roughly $3,000 toward exactly this bolting/cripple-wall retrofit (higher supplemental
grants exist for income-qualified households). It's a legitimate closing tool and trust signal,
not a sales gimmick. Also, most standard cripple-wall/bolting retrofits qualify for a
jurisdiction's prescriptive pre-approved plan set (CEBC Appendix A / LADBS Standard Plan), which
means no stamped engineer is needed — unlike the wall-removal and addition work covered in Days
1 and 3.

**What fails and what inspectors check:** missing or under-spaced anchor bolts, bare (unbraced)
cripple walls, rotted mudsills from long-term moisture intrusion, rusted/undersized post
connectors, and piers that have settled unevenly (visible as a sloping floor or a door that
won't latch). Inspectors verify bolt spacing, plywood nailing schedule on cripple walls, and
that hardware is rated/stamped correctly — this is one of the few inspections done partly with a
tape measure on the hardware itself.

**Rough LA costs:** standard whole-house bolting/cripple-wall retrofit on an average 1,500 sq ft
SFR: roughly $3,500-$8,000. Pier/post replacement or leveling: often $150-$400 per pier
depending on access. A full slab foundation replacement (rare, catastrophic failure only) can
run into six figures — this is a "bring in a specialist and mark it up" job, not a core offering.

<a id="day-5"></a>
## Day 5 — Framing

**Why it matters:** Framing is the skeleton every other trade attaches to. If you misprice or
misunderstand a framing scope, every downstream trade (drywall, electrical, HVAC) inherits the
problem — and framing errors are the most expensive to unwind after the fact.

### Learn
**This day is written single-story-default.** For engineered second-floor systems (I-joists/TJI,
point-load stacking floor-to-floor), stair code, and two-story seismic load path, see Day 29.

**Standard wall framing** (per the Manual's "Walls: New" spec, which is the company's actual
process): before removing any existing wall, first reinforce the ceiling-to-roof framing per
engineering so nothing sags or drops when the wall comes out — this order matters and is a
common rookie mistake (demo first, brace second = a caved-in ceiling). Then: install the sole
(bottom) plate horizontally, install 2x4 studs vertically at 16" on center, install posts (4x4,
6x4, 8x4, or steel where a heavier point load needs it) at bearing locations, install the top
plate horizontally, then run electrical/plumbing through the studs (per CRC R602.6, bored holes
can be up to 40% of stud width — 60% in doubled studs — while a notch in a bearing-wall stud is
limited to 25% of width; whenever the hole or notch comes within 1-1/4 inch of the stud edge, a
16-gauge steel nail plate is required to protect the wiring/pipe). Special brackets go around the framing
at connection points, plywood sheathing goes on where the wall needs to act as a shear or fire
wall, and drywall goes up last — green board (moisture-resistant) in kitchens/baths, and
fire-rated ("red") board where code requires a fire separation (commonly at a garage-to-house
wall).

**Floor and roof framing basics:** floor joists span between beams or bearing walls and are
sized by span tables based on species, size, and spacing (typically 16" O.C.); doubling or
"sistering" a joist adds strength without full replacement when a joist is undersized or
cracked. Roof framing is either stick-built (rafters cut and set individually, ridge board at
the peak) or engineered trusses (pre-built off-site, craned or hand-set, bear only on exterior
walls and can't be modified in the field without an engineer — cutting a truss chord for a duct
run without approval is a serious, common mistake). Beams that carry point loads (a wall removal,
a roof valley) transfer that load to posts below, and posts need a continuous path to a footing
— a beam resting on drywall or an unsupported stud is a failure waiting to happen.

**Headers:** every opening in a bearing wall needs a header sized to its span and the load above
it — undersized headers show up years later as a slight sag or a door that starts sticking.

**What fails, what inspectors check:** notched/bored studs beyond code limits, missing or
undersized headers, cut truss members, joists installed crown-down (wood should be installed
with its natural upward bow, or "crown," facing up so it flattens under load, not sags),
missing hurricane/seismic clips at rafter-to-wall connections, and shear panel nailing that
doesn't match the specified schedule (nail spacing is checked with a tape measure at rough
framing inspection). Framing inspection typically happens combined with rough plumbing/electrical
before insulation goes in — nothing gets covered until it passes.

**Rough LA costs:** simple interior partition wall framing runs a few hundred to low
thousand-dollar range for materials/labor alone (excluding drywall/finish); a structural wall
removal with an engineered flush beam is typically $8,000-$20,000+ once you include engineering,
permit, header, posts/footings, and finish repair on both sides.

<a id="day-6"></a>
## Day 6 — Roofing: Shingle, Tile, Flat

**Why it matters:** Roofing is one of the highest-frequency, most weather-driven sales in SoCal
(post-storm season spikes), and it's a trust category — homeowners can't see their own roof, so
your credibility on what's actually wrong up there is the entire sale.

### Learn
**Three roof systems you'll sell constantly.** *Composition shingle* (asphalt shingle) —
the most common pitched-roof system in LA, rated 30 or 50-year, installed over 30 lb tar paper
with new edge metal and metal flashing at every pipe and vent penetration; the Manual's older
training note caps overlay at up to 3 shingle layers, but California code (CRC R908.3) allows a
maximum of **two** layers of asphalt shingles — you may overlay only if the roof currently has a
single layer, and once two layers exist, a full tear-off to the deck is mandatory. Treat the
Manual's 3-layer figure as a history note, not practice: a third layer fails re-roof inspection.
On a parcel in a Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zone, the roofing assembly itself is also
regulated by CBC Chapter 7A (Class A ignition-resistant rating, ember-resistant details) —
`knowledge/products/01-roofing-shingles.md` already flags impact-resistance for wildfire-adjacent
lots; see Day 30 for the full WUI/wildfire-hardening picture.
*Tile (clay or concrete)* — heavier, longer-lived, common on Spanish/Mediterranean
style SoCal homes; requires 2 layers of 30 lb tar paper underneath and the roof structure must
be engineered/verified to carry the extra weight, which is a common oversight when a homeowner
wants to switch from shingle to tile. *Flat roof* — common on additions, garage conversions,
and mid-century homes; the company's actual process is a tear-off to bare deck (replace
plywood as needed), 40 lb fiberglass base sheet, first hot-mop layer rolled flat, a second
torch-down hot-mop layer at ½", then fiberglass cap sheets and new metal flashing at every
penetration. An "overlay" (no tear-off) version skips the demo and uses 30 lb base sheet
instead — cheaper, faster, but doesn't fix a bad deck underneath and has a shorter effective
life.

**Roof-over vs. tear-off — how you decide:** roof-over (overlay) is faster and cheaper but
hides the condition of the deck, adds weight, and is capped by code at two layers maximum;
tear-off exposes the plywood deck so you can find and replace rot before it becomes a
structural problem, and it's mandatory once the layer limit is hit or the deck is suspect. Always
walk (or ladder-inspect) the roof yourself before quoting overlay vs. tear-off — sight-unseen
overlay quotes are how contractors eat change orders.

**Flashing is where roofs actually fail.** The shingle/tile/membrane field rarely leaks —
penetrations do: chimneys, plumbing vents, skylights, HVAC curbs, and the roof-to-wall (step
flashing) and valley intersections. New metal flashing at every pipe and vent is non-negotiable
on every re-roof per the Manual, because reusing old flashing is the #1 cause of a callback leak.

**What inspectors and a good salesperson both check:** granule loss and curling on shingles
(near end of life), moss/algae streaking (moisture retention, common on north-facing slopes),
soft spots underfoot (rotted deck), daylight visible from the attic at any point, rusted or
missing flashing, and gutters pulling away from the fascia (often a sign of long-term water
overflow damage to the fascia board itself, which is a separate line item).

**Rough LA costs:** composition shingle re-roof, tear-off: roughly $6,000-$14,000 for an
average 1,500-2,000 sq ft roof depending on pitch/access; tile roof: significantly more,
$15,000-$30,000+; flat roof (torch-down), tear-off: often priced per square (100 sq ft) — figure
$450-$650/square installed depending on layers and access.

## Day 7 — Structural Repairs: Sagging Beams & Joists

**Why it matters:** Structural repair jobs are high-trust, high-margin, and often discovered
mid-remodel (a "surprise" during a kitchen demo) — knowing how to diagnose and scope one on the
spot, without panicking the homeowner, is a skill that separates a real operator from a
handyman.

### Learn
**Reading a sag correctly.** A visible dip in a floor or roofline has one of a few causes:
an undersized beam or joist for its span (built wrong originally), long-term moisture damage
rotting a structural member, a failed or missing post/support below a beam, termite damage
(structural wood loss, not just cosmetic — see Day 31 for the actual WDO inspection,
fumigation/tenting sequencing, and repair process behind a termite-caused sag), or foundation
settlement (Day 4) pulling the whole system down at one point. The fix is different for each, so the first job is diagnosis, not
demo — use a level or a string line across the suspect area to quantify the sag (how many
inches over how many feet), and go look underneath/above if you can before committing to a
scope.

**Common fixes:**
*Sistering* — bolting or nailing a new joist or rafter alongside a failing one to add strength
without full removal; fast, less invasive, appropriate when the member is undersized but not
rotted/failed.
*Beam replacement* — required when a beam is rotted, cracked, undersized for a new load (e.g.,
you're removing the wall below it and it now has to span further), or damaged by fire/water;
this always needs temporary shoring (jacks and posts to hold the load) before the old beam
comes out, and the new beam sized by an engineer or span table.
*Post/footing repair* — if the beam itself is fine but a supporting post has rotted, shifted, or
lost its footing, you replace or re-support the post — often the actual root cause when a
"sagging beam" turns out to be a fine beam sitting on a bad post.
*Flush beam retrofit* (called out explicitly in the Manual's garage conversion scope: "frame new
load bearing posts and new flushed beam instead of removed beam") — used when you remove a
bearing wall and need a beam that sits flush with the ceiling (hidden) rather than a dropped
beam that's visible — this is the answer whenever a homeowner asks "can we open this up without
a big ugly beam showing?" and it usually costs more because of the engineering and connection
hardware required to make a flush beam work.

**Sequence on a real repair:** diagnose and quantify the sag → shore/jack the area to relieve
load safely → remove and repair/replace the failed member → re-level as needed → inspect →
close up finishes. Never remove a structural member before it's shored — this is the single most
common serious safety violation on a repair job.

**What inspectors check:** proper temporary shoring during the work (documented, not
improvised), correct beam/post sizing against a span table or engineer's stamp, correct
hardware (post caps, hurricane ties, hangers) at every connection, and — for anything beyond a
straightforward sistering job — an engineer's letter or stamped calc on file before final.

**Rough LA costs:** sistering a joist or two: often $500-$1,500 per member depending on access;
a single beam replacement with temporary shoring: $3,000-$10,000+ depending on span, access, and
whether it's flush (finished) or exposed; anything combined with a wall removal gets folded into
that project's engineering and permit cost (see Day 1/5).

## Day 8 — Waterproofing, Stucco & the Exterior Envelope

**Why it matters:** The exterior envelope's entire job is to keep water out of the framing —
fail here and you create the mold, rot, and structural jobs from Days 7 and 13. This is also
one of the highest-visibility, highest-volume exterior sales (stucco, balcony/deck waterproofing,
rain gutters).

### Learn
**Stucco — new install (per the Manual's real 3-coat process):** prepare a 6" deep trench
around the house perimeter (so the stucco can extend below grade slightly and get keyed in),
install wire mesh over the sheathing, apply the **scratch (base) coat** and let it cure **6
days**, apply the **brown (second) coat** and let it cure **4 days**, then apply the **color
coat** in the finish texture the customer picks. That's roughly a 10-day minimum cure sequence
before finish color goes on — a homeowner asking "why can't this be done in 3 days" needs to
hear this explicitly. **Re-stucco** (existing house) adds sandblasting or hydro-blasting the old
stucco off first (pressure and method depend on the city's requirements), patching cracks in the
brown coat, then the same color-coat finish. **Before blasting old coatings off a pre-1978
home, apply the Day 13 hazmat lens first:** exterior disturbance over 20 sq ft triggers EPA RRP,
and open/dry sandblasting of leaded coatings is a prohibited RRP practice — use lead-safe
methods (wet methods, HEPA-shrouded tools, ground containment) with an EPA Lead-Safe Certified
crew, and price that containment into the bid.

**Why stucco cracks and what it means:** hairline cracking is normal (stucco is cementitious and
shrinks slightly as it cures) and is cosmetic; cracks that are wide, stepped (diagonal, following
a stair-step pattern), or radiate from window/door corners usually indicate structural movement
or settlement underneath (tie this back to Day 1 and Day 4) — this is a sales-critical
distinction because it separates a $400 patch-and-paint from a foundation conversation.

**Waterproofing — balcony/deck example (per the Manual's real "Deck-O-Max" process):** remove
deteriorated plywood down to the joists, install new ¾" CDX plywood, replace scuppers (the
drain openings that let water off a flat surface), then build up a multi-layer waterproof
membrane system: diamond lath, leveling compound, fiberglass reinforcing, an acrylic skim coat,
a texture coat, and two coats of acrylic finish resin. This is a *system*, not a single product
— skipping a layer (a very common corner-cut) is why balcony waterproofing jobs fail and rot the
structure underneath within a few years.

**Rain gutters:** installed at a slight slope (roughly 3% — a fraction of an inch of drop per
foot) so water actually runs to the downspout instead of pooling; downspouts are placed roughly
every 30 feet of gutter run. Undersized or clogged gutters are a major, preventable cause of
fascia rot and foundation-adjacent water damage.

**Texcote / Cool Wall coatings:** an elastomeric coating system applied over prepped stucco
(pressure-washed, cracks patched) that adds reflectivity/thermal benefit and a fresh painted
look in one step — sold as a premium alternative to a straight repaint.

**What fails, what inspectors/estimators check:** cracked or missing weep screed at the base of
stucco walls (lets moisture trapped behind stucco drain out — missing it traps water against the
framing), caulking failure at window/door-to-stucco joints, gutters pulled away from fascia,
and — the big one — any stucco crack pattern that looks structural rather than cosmetic gets
flagged back to a Day 1/4 conversation before you quote paint.

## Day 9 — Plumbing: Repipe, Sewer, Water Heaters

**Why it matters:** Plumbing failures are emergencies — a burst galvanized pipe or a backed-up
sewer line is the fastest-closing, most urgent sale you'll ever run, and repipe/water-heater
work is recurring, code-driven revenue tied to the age of SoCal's housing stock.

### Learn
**Why repipe is such a common SoCal job:** most homes built before the 1960s-70s were plumbed
in galvanized steel, which corrodes from the inside out over decades — rust builds up inside the
pipe, restricting flow (classic symptom: low water pressure that gets worse over years) and
eventually perforates, causing leaks inside walls/slabs that go undetected until there's damage.
The fix, per the Manual, is a straight **repipe from galvanized to copper**: remove the existing
galvanized piping, install new copper (L-type, the mid-weight grade standard for residential
water lines) for each fixture, and replace the main line from the city meter to the house. On a
**raised foundation**, all new plumbing runs underneath the house through the crawl space
(accessible, easier); on a **slab foundation**, it has to run through the attic and down inside
the walls, since you can't trench a slab without major demo — this single fact changes both the
cost and the invasiveness of a repipe dramatically and is a must-know differentiator when
quoting.

**Alternative to copper:** PEX (cross-linked polyethylene) is now widely used and code-accepted
in California for repipes — flexible, faster to install, resistant to the corrosion that kills
galvanized, and generally less expensive than copper; many companies quote both and let cost/
preference decide.

**Sewer lines:** older homes often have cast iron or clay sewer laterals, which crack, root-
intrude, or corrode over decades, causing slow drains, backups, and sewage odors. A camera
inspection (running a scope down the line) diagnoses the problem before you dig or quote — this
is now standard practice and something worth offering as a low-cost diagnostic that builds trust
before a bigger sewer replacement sale. Trenchless sewer replacement (pipe bursting or lining)
is increasingly used to avoid tearing up a driveway or yard.

**Water heaters:** traditional tank water heaters (typically 40-50 gallon for a SoCal SFR) vs.
tankless (on-demand) units, which the Manual's garage conversion scope specifies as a real
example: a "9.5 GPM high efficiency" gas tankless unit. Tankless costs more upfront but takes
less space, has a longer lifespan, and never "runs out" of hot water — a strong upsell
conversation, especially on any job where the water heater has to move or be replaced anyway.

**Bathroom rough plumbing (tie-in to Day 15):** the Manual's real process for a shower is to
**hot-mop the shower pan, fill it with water, and let it sit 48 hours** to test for leaks before
any tile goes on — skipping this test is exactly how a shower leak ends up as a Day 13 mold
job two years later.

**What fails, what inspectors check:** corroded/leaking galvanized pipe, improper pipe sizing or
missing shut-offs, drain lines without correct slope (typically ¼" per foot minimum for proper
drainage), water heater installed without a required expansion tank, T&P (temperature/pressure)
relief valve, or seismic strapping (mandatory in California), and any rough plumbing that's
covered before inspection.

**Rough LA costs:** whole-house repipe (copper or PEX): roughly $6,000-$15,000 depending on
home size, foundation type, and fixture count; tank water heater swap: $1,500-$3,000; tankless
conversion: $4,000-$7,000+ including gas line upsizing if needed; sewer line replacement:
$5,000-$25,000+ depending on length, depth, and trenchless vs. open-trench method.

<a id="day-10"></a>
## Day 10 — Electrical: Rewire & Panels

**Why it matters:** Electrical is life-safety work with zero tolerance for shortcuts — it's also
one of the clearest "your house is genuinely unsafe" conversations you can have with a
homeowner, which makes it a high-trust, high-urgency sale when done honestly.

### Learn
**Why rewire is such a common SoCal job:** knob-and-tube wiring (pre-1950s) and early aluminum
branch wiring (1960s-70s) are both fire hazards by modern standards and are frequently flagged
by home insurers and inspectors, forcing a rewire before a sale, refinance, or insurance renewal.
The Manual's real rewire process: remove existing outlets, switches, fixtures, and cover
plates; install flex metal conduit in walls where required; **replace the wire up to code —
copper, #12 gauge for outlet circuits and #14 gauge for switch/lighting circuits** — running it
all the way back to the main panel; replace outlets and switches, including GFI (ground-fault)
outlets where required; install new standard cover plates; then patch, prime, and paint.

**Electrical panels:** the panel is the home's electrical "brain" — it distributes power from
the utility service into individual breaker circuits. Older homes often have 60-100 amp panels,
which is undersized for a modern household running central AC, an EV charger, an induction
range, and a kitchen full of appliances simultaneously; a panel upgrade to 200 amp service is a
common and necessary sale before any major remodel, ADU, or EV charger install, because you
can't add meaningful new load to an already-maxed panel. Panel work always requires a permit and
inspection and often requires the utility (DWP or Edison) to physically disconnect and
reconnect service — this adds scheduling lead time that has to be set in the homeowner's
expectations up front.

**GFI/GFCI and AFCI — know the difference:** GFI (ground-fault) outlets protect against
shock by cutting power when current leaks to ground (required near water: kitchens, baths,
garages, exterior, within 6 ft of a sink). Code sets the real spacing number: NEC 210.52(C)
requires kitchen counter receptacles placed so no point along the counter is more than 24
inches from an outlet — effectively **no more than about 4 feet apart** — and every
counter-serving receptacle must be GFCI-protected. The Manual's habit of setting one roughly
every 3 feet on a full kitchen remodel stays safely inside that rule; a spacing anywhere near 5
feet would leave a gap that fails inspection. AFCI
(arc-fault) breakers protect against electrical fires from damaged wiring and are increasingly
required on bedroom and living-space circuits by current code.

**Recessed/can lighting and dimmers** are a constant remodel upsell — the Manual's kitchen scope
calls out recess lights, counter lights, ceiling lights, GFI outlets, switches, and dimmers as a
standard rough-electrical package.

**What fails, what inspectors check:** double-tapped breakers (two wires under one screw —
a common fire-risk violation), missing GFI/AFCI where required, undersized wire gauge for the
circuit's breaker rating (a #14 wire on a 20-amp breaker is a fire hazard), open junction boxes
without covers, and any wiring buried in a wall without a permit history.

**Rough LA costs:** whole-house rewire: roughly $8,000-$20,000+ depending on home size and wall
access (plaster walls cost more to open/patch than drywall); 200-amp panel upgrade: $3,000-
$6,000 not including any utility fees; adding recessed lighting: often $200-$350 per fixture
installed.

## Day 11 — HVAC, Ducts & Title 24/HERS

**Why it matters:** HVAC is a big-ticket, financeable sale with a dedicated Josh Bloom training
video — and Title 24/HERS compliance is a California-specific requirement that trips up
contractors who learned HVAC in another state.

### Learn
**System sizing — the single most important HVAC concept to get right.** The Manual states it
plainly: unit size (2.5, 3, 3.5, 4, 4.5, or 5 ton) is determined by the square footage of the
house — in practice, a proper sizing also accounts for insulation level, window area/orientation,
and ceiling height (a full **Manual J load calculation** is the industry-standard method, and
increasingly required for permit). Oversizing is a common and costly mistake: an oversized
system short-cycles (turns on/off rapidly), fails to properly dehumidify, wears out faster, and
costs more to run — "bigger is better" is wrong for HVAC and worth explicitly correcting when a
homeowner assumes it.

**The split system, piece by piece (per the Manual's real install sequence):** install the
furnace and/or evaporator coil inside the house (attic or a closet, decided before work begins);
run copper refrigerant line ("plumbing") from the coil location out to the condenser; prepare a
GFI electrical circuit in the furnace area; pour a concrete pad outside for the condenser unit;
set the condenser on the pad; install a dedicated electrical breaker/disconnect on the wall next
to the condenser; connect the refrigerant lines between coil and condenser. The furnace/coil
handles heating and moves air through the ducts; the condenser (outside) handles the
compression/heat-exchange side of cooling. **Note (2025+):** new residential AC/heat-pump
systems now ship with A2L (mildly flammable) refrigerants — R-454B or R-32 — as R-410A is phased
down under the EPA's HFC phasedown. This affects equipment cost/availability and adds
refrigerant-leak-detection requirements on new installs, so factor it into any new-system quote.

**Ductwork:** flexible duct (the Manual specifies **R-6 rated flex duct**) run from the
furnace/plenum to each room's supply register, sealed at every joint with mastic or foil tape
(not standard cloth duct tape, which fails over time) plus a silicone bead for extra seal per
the Manual's repair spec, with elbows added at direction changes. **Caution:** R-6 only meets
Title 24 in mild coastal climate zones (3, 5, 6, 7). Most LA-area work — climate zones 8, 9, and
10 in the basin/valleys, plus mountain zone 16 — requires **R-8** duct insulation in
unconditioned attics/crawl space under the 2022 Energy Code. Confirm the property's climate zone
and spec R-8 where required; R-6 in the wrong zone fails HERS/Title 24 verification. Leaky, poorly sealed, or
crushed ductwork is one of the most common and invisible energy-waste problems in older homes —
a duct pressure/leakage test is a strong diagnostic upsell tool. **Asbestos note (ties to Day
13):** any duct replacement in a pre-1990s home requires checking for asbestos-containing duct
wrap/insulation before demo, per the Manual's explicit callout — this must be tested and, if
positive, removed by a certified abatement contractor with a free air-clearance test before work
continues.

**Title 24 and HERS — the California-specific piece.** Title 24 is California's energy code;
any new HVAC system, duct replacement, or significant alteration triggers Title 24 energy
compliance documentation as part of permitting. **HERS (Home Energy Rating System) raters** are
independent third parties required to field-verify certain items — duct leakage testing,
refrigerant charge verification, airflow — on most new HVAC installs and duct replacements
before the permit can final. Budget the HERS rater's inspection (and the possibility of a
failed test requiring rework) into both your timeline and your price; this is a step out-of-state
contractors frequently forget and it stalls jobs.

**Rough LA costs:** full split-system replacement (condenser + furnace/coil + basic
ductwork touch-up), single-family home: roughly $8,000-$16,000 depending on tonnage and
efficiency tier; full duct system replacement: $3,000-$8,000 depending on home size and
attic access; HERS testing/verification fee: typically a few hundred dollars, paid regardless of
pass/fail on the first attempt.

## Day 12 — Insulation

**Why it matters:** Insulation is a fast, high-margin, low-drama sale that pairs naturally with
almost every other job (attic access is already open for HVAC/electrical; walls are already open
during a remodel) — knowing the R-values and where they apply cold makes you look sharp in a
five-minute conversation.

### Learn
**What R-value means:** it's a material's resistance to heat flow — higher R-value means better
insulating performance per inch. It is not a measure of thickness or brand, and more is not
infinitely better past the point of diminishing returns for the climate zone, but in almost all
existing SoCal homes the existing insulation is well under current recommended levels, so
"more" is nearly always a legitimate upgrade.

**Where it goes and at what R-value (per the Manual and current California energy-code
targets):** attic/ceiling insulation — the Manual specifies **R-30** batts or blown-in foam
installed between the ceiling joists/roof framing and under any air ducts and cables that run
through the attic (protecting the ducts from attic heat is part of the job, not an afterthought);
exterior wall insulation in new construction or an addition — the Manual's garage conversion
scope specifies **R-13** wall insulation in the new addition space and in the wall separating
old house from new addition (the shared wall matters because without it you're losing
conditioned air straight into an assembly that touches the unconditioned old structure during
construction, or simply because code requires insulating any newly exposed wall cavity).
Current Title 24 targets for California climate zones commonly call for higher wall R-values
(R-15 to R-21 with modern batts) in new construction — always check current code for the
specific climate zone rather than assuming the Manual's R-13 is still the code minimum
everywhere, since energy code updates on a multi-year cycle.

**Removal and prep (per the Manual's real process):** remove existing (often degraded,
compressed, or contaminated) insulation from the attic or foundation area and haul it away, then
disinfect the area with a fogger machine for bacteria and mold before installing new material —
this step exists because old attic insulation is a common home for rodent droppings and mold
spores, and skipping the disinfection step is a corner-cut that comes back as a homeowner
complaint or a health issue.

**Batt vs. blown-in vs. foam:** fiberglass batts are precut, DIY-friendly, and cost-effective for
open, accessible attics with standard joist spacing; blown-in (loose-fill fiberglass or
cellulose) fills irregular spaces and low-clearance areas batts can't reach well and is faster
for large open attics; spray foam offers the highest R-value per inch and also air-seals (stops
drafts, not just heat transfer) but costs significantly more and is typically reserved for new
construction, additions, or homes with unusual framing/space constraints.

**Why insulation is a natural upsell, not a separate sales cycle:** it's almost always sold
alongside HVAC replacement (a right-sized system paired with a leaky, under-insulated attic
still performs poorly — this is the honest answer when a homeowner asks why their new AC
"still isn't cold enough"), electrical/duct work (the attic's already open), and any wall
demo/remodel (exposed wall cavities are the cheapest opportunity you'll ever have to add
insulation, because the labor to open the wall is already being paid for by the other job).

**Rough LA costs:** attic insulation (removal of old + new blown-in or batt to R-30 in an
average 1,500-2,000 sq ft attic): roughly $2,000-$4,500; wall insulation added during an open-
wall remodel: often just material cost plus minimal labor, a few hundred dollars per room —
this is one of the best margin-per-labor-hour upsells in the whole business.

## Day 13 — Hazmat: Asbestos, Lead, Mold

**Why it matters:** This is the day that keeps you, your crew, and your company out of legal and
health trouble. Getting this wrong isn't a callback — it's a lawsuit, a fine, or someone getting
seriously sick. Every renovation on an older SoCal home needs this lens applied before demo
starts.

### Learn
**Asbestos — the pre-1990s (practically, often pre-1980) trigger.** Asbestos was used
extensively in construction materials through the 1970s-80s: popcorn/acoustic ceiling texture,
vinyl floor tile and its adhesive, HVAC duct wrap and insulation, some older drywall joint
compound, and old stucco mixes. The Manual is explicit about the correct process for the most
common encounter — popcorn ceiling removal: **remove a sample of the ceiling and send it to a
laboratory for testing before any demo begins.** If it comes back positive, abatement must be
done by a certified asbestos-removal company, not the general crew, and the Manual's duct
replacement scope makes the same point: if asbestos is found in old duct wrap, it must be
removed by a certified company with a free air-clearance test provided afterward. The rule to
internalize: **you test first, you never assume, and you never let an untrained crew disturb a
suspect material.** Disturbing asbestos (sanding, cutting, demolishing without containment) is
what makes it dangerous — intact and undisturbed, it's typically left in place if it's not
otherwise a nuisance, which is also a legitimate, cost-saving option to offer a homeowner instead
of automatic removal.

**SoCal-specific layer beyond the lab test — SCAQMD Rule 1403.** South Coast AQMD Rule 1403
governs asbestos on top of the lab-test step above: a certified-consultant asbestos survey is
required before any demolition or renovation, and AQMD must be notified electronically (with a
fee) at least 10 working days before work begins — a copy of that notification is required
before the city will issue a demolition permit. The one residential carve-out is a single-unit
dwelling disturbing less than 100 sq ft of intact material. The trigger is the activity and the
area being disturbed, not simply whether the house predates 1990, so build the 10-day AQMD lead
time into your schedule the same way you build in lab turnaround.

**Lead — the pre-1978 trigger.** Any home built before 1978 is presumed to have lead-based
paint until tested otherwise (this is federal EPA RRP — Renovation, Repair, and Painting — rule
territory, not optional). Contractors disturbing more than a minimal area of painted surface in
a pre-1978 home performing renovation, repair, or painting work are required to be **EPA
Lead-Safe Certified**, use lead-safe work practices (containment, wet methods to control dust,
specific cleanup/verification procedures), and provide the homeowner with the EPA's "Renovate
Right" pamphlet before work begins. This applies constantly in this business — LA's housing
stock is full of pre-1978 homes — and it's a compliance requirement, not a sales point, though
being certified is a legitimate credibility item to mention to a cautious homeowner.

**Mold — the Manual's real remediation process.** Cover and seal/contain the affected area,
demolish the contaminated material (drywall, plywood, whatever mold has grown into — mold on a
hard, cleanable surface can sometimes be cleaned, but mold that has grown into a porous material
has to be removed), install a negative-air machine that exhausts to the outside for a **minimum
of 3 days** (the HEPA-filtered machine keeps the sealed containment under negative pressure and
continuously scrubs airborne spores, so nothing escapes into the rest of the house while the
demo and treatment proceed — the mold itself is stopped by removing the moisture and the
contaminated material, not by any lack of oxygen), sand affected beams, disinfect the entire room, apply a special mold-treatment paint to all
affected beams/surfaces, clean up, and **order third-party air testing** to confirm the space is
clear before closing it back up. All work must be done in suits and respirator masks. The
Manual's field note on where to expect mold is worth memorizing verbatim: **bathroom walls,
kitchen walls around the sink, water heaters, laundry rooms, and A/C & furnace areas — any area
with regular moisture exposure.**

**What connects all three:** they're all about *not disturbing a hazard before you've confirmed
what it is and controlled it* — the discipline is identical even though the materials and
remediation methods differ.

**Mold above assumes the moisture source is otherwise contained.** Where the loss is an active
water or fire/smoke event instead, that's a distinct restoration vertical with its own
mitigation-vs-rebuild split, insurance-claim flow, and IICRC certification — see Day 31.

### 🗣️ How Josh sells this

Josh's go-to justification for the $50-$100 asbestos test nobody wants to pay for: a contractor
who skipped it got sued for **$170,000** after ripping out an acoustic ceiling — and settled for
**$70,000** out of pocket, on a job that was only worth **$15,000** to begin with. The liability
dwarfed the sale. He also drills the licensing rule hard: a GC is legally barred from removing
asbestos himself — a certified specialist has to do it, and the contractor keeps a copy of the
certificate of removal on file, which is what protects him if a claim shows up later. (Source:
`04.03-product-asbestos-mold.md`; the module flags this story's exact origin as unverified —
Josh's own account, a colleague's, or an industry cautionary tale is not specified.)

## Day 14 — Kitchen Remodel Deep-Dive

**Why it matters:** The kitchen is the single highest-ticket, highest-margin, most
design-intensive job in the residential remodeling business — and it touches almost every trade
you've studied so far (framing, electrical, plumbing, drywall, flooring, tile). Selling and
scoping kitchens well is a core business skill.

### Learn
**The three tiers, and why the distinction matters for pricing and timeline (straight from the
Manual):** *Re-finish* — sand and refinish the existing cabinet boxes and doors in place
(paint/lacquer or stain/varnish), replace hinges/handles; cheapest, fastest, keeps the existing
layout entirely. *Re-facing* — same prep, but instead of just refinishing, you cover the visible
cabinet face with veneer, Formica, or thermofoil, or refinish it, and install *new* door and
drawer panels (solid wood or melamine) with new hardware; a step up in look without a full gut.
*Full remodel* — complete demolition and replacement of cabinets, countertops, backsplash, and
floor. Knowing which tier a homeowner actually wants (and can afford) in the first five minutes
of the conversation saves everyone time.

**The full remodel sequence, exactly as the Manual scopes it:** design first — a 3D sketch
covering floor material/color, cabinet finish/color, backsplash material/finish/color,
countertop material/finish/color, and bull-nose edge design, approved by the customer before
any demo. Then order cabinets (**custom takes up to 3 weeks; semi-custom takes up to 5 days** —
this lead-time gap is a critical scheduling fact, and a damaged custom cabinet takes another 3
weeks to replace vs. 1 day for in-stock semi-custom, so cabinet choice is a timeline decision as
much as a style one). Cover work areas, then demo the existing cabinets, countertop, backsplash,
floor tile, and baseboards. Prepare fire-rated drywall (Type X, 5/8" — **"red board"** — per the
Manual's kitchen spec; the actual code trigger for fire-rated gypsum is a required separation —
garage-to-house, furnace room, occupancy separation — not simply proximity to a range, so use it
here because the Manual specifies it, not because it's a code-mandated range-clearance rule),
patch/prime/paint. Rough electrical:
recessed lights, under-counter lights, ceiling lights, **GFI outlets every 3 feet** of counter,
switches, dimmers, and dedicated circuits for the dishwasher and garbage disposal. Relocate/
replace plumbing in copper, up to code. Prepare the range hood system (duct and panels). Install
the new cabinets, then countertop with sink, faucet, dishwasher air-gap/diverter, soap dispenser,
and garbage disposal. Install backsplash (grout and seal). On a raised foundation, install tar
paper and cement board on the sub-floor before the finish floor goes down. Install the finish
floor (tile, linoleum, vinyl, marble, or stone), grout and seal. Hook up customer-provided
appliances. Final touch-ups, clean up.

**The one structural trap every kitchen remodeler must check:** the Manual flags it directly —
if you're demolishing a soffit (the boxed-in ceiling space above upper cabinets), you must first
check whether there's plumbing or a load-bearing beam hidden inside it. Soffits routinely hide
exactly that, and "let's just open it up for a modern look" is one of the most common
change-order traps in the business if this isn't checked before the sale is priced.

**Design/material lead times you're already managing:** slab countertops (granite, quartz/
Caesarstone) are template-measured after cabinets are set, then fabricated — so countertop
install always trails cabinet install by roughly 1-2 weeks, which is a scheduling fact worth
setting expectations around up front.

**Rough LA costs:** re-finish: $1,500-$4,000; re-facing: $4,000-$10,000; full mid-range remodel:
$35,000-$75,000+ depending on cabinet tier, countertop material, and layout changes; a full
gut-and-reconfigure with moved plumbing/electrical and structural changes can exceed $100,000.

### 🗣️ How Josh sells this

Two hard sequencing rules Josh teaches: never take final countertop templates/measurements
until cabinets are fully installed (fabrication then takes up to 3 days), and tell clients
upfront that stained cabinets can be repainted, but painted cabinets can never be stripped back
down to stain — it's a one-way door. His script for "you're $20K more than the other bids": sell
personal service and dedicated specialist crews over one generalist (the brain-surgeon analogy —
a great surgeon shouldn't do every kind of surgery), warn with horror stories of cheap
contractors taking 6-12+ months, and close by citing 15-25 years in business to back the
warranty. (Source: `04.06-product-kitchen.md`)

<a id="day-15"></a>
## Day 15 — Bathroom Deep-Dive

**Why it matters:** Bathrooms are the second-highest-margin remodel category after kitchens, and
they carry the single highest risk of a callback (waterproofing failure) if the shower pan isn't
tested correctly — this day is about getting that exactly right.

### Learn
**The full sequence, exactly as the Manual scopes a bathroom remodel:** pull city permits as
needed, prep the work area, demolish existing fixtures and tile (clean up and haul away),
upgrade the plumbing in the work area from galvanized to copper (Day 9 knowledge applied
directly). Then the waterproofing sequence that matters most: **hot-mop the shower pan and fill
it with water, letting it sit for 48 hours to test the waterproofing before anything else
happens** — this single step, done correctly, is what prevents a shower leak from becoming a Day
13 mold job or a Day 7 structural rot job two years later. Once the pan tests dry, pour concrete
over it and let it settle another 48 hours. Install wire mesh and tar paper on the shower/tub
walls, install the tub and drain, then install tile on the shower/tub area, apply grout and
sealer. Install cement board, "wonder board," or floating cement on the shower/tub area and
bathroom floor — required whenever it's a raised foundation or a second-story floor, because
those assemblies need a rigid, water-resistant substrate under tile that a slab-on-grade doesn't
need in the same way. Install tile on the shower pan, shower walls, tub walls, and bathroom
floor. Replace drywall as needed with **green board (moisture-resistant) or, in wetter zones,
a "20-minute" waterproof-rated board**, tape and patch, install baseboards, prime and paint.
Rough electrical: waterproof recessed lights (rated for wet locations directly over a shower),
standard recessed lights, GFI outlets, switches. Install fixtures — fan, toilet, shower
enclosure/door, showerhead, sink, vanity, countertop, faucets, medicine cabinet, track lighting,
toilet paper bar, towel bar, mirrors — all customer-selected. Clean up and haul away.

**What's explicitly NOT included per the Manual's own scope (a critical thing to communicate at
the appointment, not after):** tile, fixtures, permits, city fees, and the vanity itself are all
customer-supplied or separately billed — labor and rough material are what's included in the
base price. This exact inclusion/exclusion pattern (rough material and labor included; finish
materials selected and often supplied by the customer) repeats across nearly every job type in
the Manual and is core to how this company prices — get comfortable stating it out loud.

**The shampoo niche and other small details that separate a good bathroom from a great one:**
the Manual's master-bathroom garage conversion scope specifically frames in a shampoo niche
during the shower-pan framing stage — small design details like this have to be decided before
rough plumbing/framing, not after tile is ordered, because retrofitting one later means tearing
back into finished work.

**Why the 48-hour pan test is non-negotiable:** a shower pan is the single highest-consequence
waterproofing detail in a house, because it holds standing water directly against a wood-framed
structure daily, for the life of the house. Skipping the test to save two days is the single
most common root cause of a bathroom callback, and it's cheap insurance against a five-figure
mold/rot repair.

**Rough LA costs:** a standard mid-size bathroom remodel (tub-to-shower conversion, new tile,
vanity, fixtures): $15,000-$30,000; a primary/master bath with a larger footprint, custom tile
work, and a freestanding tub: $30,000-$60,000+.

## Day 16 — Flooring

**Why it matters:** Flooring is sold on nearly every remodel and as a standalone job, and the
right product recommendation depends entirely on moisture conditions and foundation type — get
that wrong and you install a floor that buckles or delaminates within a year.

### Learn
**The universal first step, every flooring type:** remove the existing floor, clean up and haul
away debris, then **test the surface for moisture.** This step is in the Manual for engineered
wood, hardwood, and laminate specifically because wood-based flooring products fail
catastrophically over moisture — installing over a damp slab without addressing it is the #1
cause of a flooring callback (cupping, buckling, mold underneath).

**Engineered wood vs. solid hardwood — the practical difference:** engineered wood has a
real-wood veneer over a plywood-like core, which makes it more dimensionally stable across
moisture and temperature swings, so it can go over a concrete slab (with proper prep) as well as
a raised foundation; solid hardwood expands/contracts more with moisture and is the Manual's
listed option for "raised or slab foundation" but generally needs more careful moisture control
on slab applications. Process for both (per the Manual): apply heavy-duty sealer on a concrete
slab or 15-year tar paper on a raised-foundation subfloor, install a cork pad if required, install
plywood underlayment (raised foundation only), apply glue to the foundation/slab (**one layer
for engineered, two layers for solid hardwood** — hardwood needs a stronger bond due to its
greater expansion/contraction), install piece by piece, install baseboard and quarter-round/
half-round trim, then clean the finished surface with turpentine to remove glue haze.

**Laminate:** a photographic wear-layer over a composite core, installed as a "floating" floor
over foil or cork padding rather than glued down — faster install, budget-friendly, holds up
reasonably well but doesn't add resale value the way real wood does.

**Linoleum/vinyl (sheet):** installed over a prepped, patched cement surface, glued down and
rolled to eliminate air bubbles — a durable, water-tolerant, budget option still common in older
homes' kitchens and baths.

**Tile flooring:** requires a proper substrate first — cement patch, floating cement, "wonder
board," or cement board depending on the base — then tile is set with spacers (customer selects
design, color, size), grout applied in the seams, and sealer applied to the grout only (not the
tile face, which is usually pre-sealed or doesn't need it depending on material). Tile is the
default for wet areas (bathrooms, entries) because it's essentially immune to the moisture
issues that kill wood flooring.

**Carpet:** move furniture, remove old carpet and padding (replacing wood tack strips as
needed), install new tack strips, install padding (recycled sponge or waterproof foam — the
waterproof option matters directly under kitchens/baths or in flood-prone areas), install the
customer-selected carpet, then move furniture back.

**Why the moisture-test step is the whole ballgame:** every wood-based product in this list is
sold with the tacit promise that it will lie flat for years — a homeowner who picks hardwood
over a slab that hasn't been tested for moisture is buying a floor that may fail, and testing
first is both an honest sales practice and cheap insurance against a warranty claim.

**Rough LA costs (installed, per SF, materials + labor):** laminate: $4-$8/SF; engineered wood:
$8-$14/SF; solid hardwood: $10-$18/SF; tile: $8-$16/SF depending on tile grade; carpet: $4-$9/SF;
linoleum/vinyl sheet: $5-$9/SF.

### 🗣️ How Josh sells this

Josh's pitch for upselling hardwood over laminate: hardwood and bamboo can be sanded and
refinished 10-20 times and last 200-300 years, while laminate's anti-scratch coating can't be
refinished at all once it scratches — it has to be replaced, and shows real wear by year 30-40.
Engineered wood splits the difference: real hardwood veneer, but only refinishable 2-3 times.
(As a factual matter, solid hardwood realistically refinishes roughly 4-10 times and lasts
100+ years with care — use the long-life story to sell durability, but don't put a specific
refinish count or year figure in writing as a promise.) On tile jobs, he never puts tile directly on plywood — plywood absorbs moisture and the tiles
pop loose; cement board is the required moisture barrier first, every time. (Source:
`04.02-product-flooring.md`)

## Day 17 — Paint, Drywall & Popcorn Removal

**Why it matters:** Paint and popcorn removal are the highest-frequency, lowest-barrier-to-entry
jobs in the business — fast to sell, fast to execute, and the volume workhorse that keeps crews
busy between bigger remodels. Doing prep correctly here is what separates a paint job that looks
good for ten years from one that peels in two.

### Learn
**Interior paint (per the Manual's real process):** move furniture, cover and protect all
work areas, let the customer select colors room by room, prep the drywall (patch and sand any
damage — this step is where most of the actual labor and skill lives, not in the paint
application itself), apply one coat of primer, then two coats of finish paint — water-based
(latex) or plastic-base depending on the area (kitchens/baths typically get a more washable,
moisture-resistant sheen than bedrooms/living rooms). Clean up, restore furniture to place.

**Exterior paint — a materially bigger job than interior, per the Manual:** cover everything
(windows, trees, planters, bushes, cars), then **pressure-wash the entire house at 2,500 PSI**
to remove chalking, dirt, and loose material. Prepare the stucco — open up any cracks wider so
patch material can actually key into them (a crack painted over without opening it first will
just re-crack through the new paint), then patch with stucco compound. Prepare wood trim
(fascia boards, eaves, window/door frames) with caulking and bondo (wood filler) and sand
smooth. Dig a 6" trench around the house perimeter (so paint can be applied down to and slightly
below grade, matching the stucco-install trench concept from Day 8). Apply one coat of primer to
both trim and stucco, then two coats of finish paint on the stucco and two coats of oil-based
finish paint on the trim (trim gets oil-based for its extra durability and sheen against
weather). Backfill the trench, clean up.

**Lead check before you sand or scrape a pre-1978 home.** Interior and exterior paint prep —
sanding, scraping, opening cracks, pressure-washing off old coatings — on a home built before
1978 disturbs lead-based paint and falls under the EPA Renovation, Repair and Painting (RRP)
rule, the paint-specific analog of the asbestos step below: the company must be Lead-Safe
(EPA/CDPH) certified, use contained lead-safe work practices, and give the homeowner the
required lead pamphlet before work begins. Flag a pre-1978 home at the appointment the same way
you flag popcorn ceilings for asbestos.

**Popcorn/acoustic ceiling — install:** cover everything, prep the ceiling (fix cracks, patch as
needed), spray on acoustic material in the customer's chosen texture (orange peel, eggshell, or
smooth), prime and paint if needed.

**Popcorn/acoustic ceiling — removal (the critical hazmat tie-in from Day 13):** **first, remove
a sample and send it to a lab for asbestos testing** — this is non-negotiable and happens before
any other step. Then cover the room, remove and scrape the existing texture, patch cracks and
prep the now-bare ceiling for paint, spray a new texture coat (commonly orange peel as a modern
replacement look), then prime and paint. If the lab test comes back positive, this entire job
routes to certified abatement instead of the general crew — this is the single most common way
an otherwise-simple paint/cosmetic job turns into a hazmat job, and it needs to be flagged
*before* pricing, not discovered mid-demo.

**Why prep work is where the money and the quality both live:** in nearly every paint scope
above, the actual paint application is a small fraction of total labor — patching, sanding,
pressure-washing, crack-opening, and caulking are what determine whether the finished job looks
sharp and lasts. A homeowner comparing a cheap bid to yours is usually comparing a "two coats,
no prep" quote against your full-prep scope — knowing this cold lets you explain the price gap
confidently instead of getting drawn into a race-to-the-bottom conversation.

**Rough LA costs:** interior paint (whole house, average SFR): $3,500-$8,000 depending on square
footage and trim detail; exterior paint (stucco + trim, full prep): $6,000-$12,000; popcorn
ceiling removal + retexture (per room, assuming clean asbestos test): $500-$1,200; asbestos
testing itself is typically a small flat lab fee, well worth it relative to the liability of
skipping it.

### 🗣️ How Josh sells this

Three protective moves Josh writes into every paint contract: the finish-match clause always
says "will match up to 85%" of existing paint — never 100%, so he always over-delivers instead
of under-delivering — and the base price explicitly covers only "up to two to three pieces" of
wood repair, so any additional rot found under old paint becomes a change order, not absorbed
cost. On subcontractor jobs, he always pitches and closes the full-home upsell with the client
BEFORE locking a fixed price with his painter — that order lets him negotiate the sub down to
patchwork-only pricing if the client declines the upsell, but never the reverse. (Source:
`04.05-product-painting.md`)

## Day 18 — Windows & Doors

**Why it matters:** Windows are a high-ticket, high-visibility upgrade that homeowners
notice daily (comfort, noise, energy bills) — and the choice between retrofit and new-
construction install changes both price and disruption dramatically, which is a conversation
you need to have correctly at the appointment, not after demo starts.

### Learn
**Retrofit vs. new construction — the single most important distinction, straight from the
Manual.** *Retrofit:* remove the existing window glass/sash only, prepare the existing frame,
and install a new aluminum or vinyl window *over* the existing frame, sealed with 50-year
caulking around the perimeter per code. This is faster, less invasive, doesn't touch the stucco
or interior finishes, and is the default choice when the existing frame is sound. *New
construction:* remove the existing window and its entire frame, exposing the rough framing
(studs/header), install the new window directly into the framed opening, then rebuild the
weatherproofing from scratch — tar paper and wire mesh, cement scratch coat and brown coat, and
a new stucco color coat to finish. This is the correct (and often required) method when the
existing frame is rotted, when the window size/shape is changing, or when a homeowner wants a
true clean, modern reveal rather than the visible "frame within a frame" look retrofit leaves —
but it costs meaningfully more because it drags in stucco repair on both the interior finish and
exterior weatherproofing.

**Window glazing specs you'll quote constantly:** double-pane, insulated (often gas-filled with
argon for extra thermal performance), Low-E (low-emissivity) coating to reduce heat transfer and
UV, in vinyl, aluminum, or wood frame, available as retrofit, new-construction, or **block
windows** (used for basement/garage/utility openings — smaller, often obscure or wire glass, set
into the framed opening with wood or foil trim rather than a full stucco rebuild). Standard
frame color options per the Manual: white, almond, black, and bronze.

**Permits and energy code — don't skip this in the pitch.** Window replacement is permitted work
in most LA-area jurisdictions, and replacement windows must meet California Title 24 fenestration
limits — generally U-factor ≤ 0.30 and SHGC ≤ 0.23 in most SoCal climate zones — which is exactly
why double-pane Low-E is the standard spec above, not just a nice-to-have. Pull the permit and
keep the CF/energy compliance documentation; an unpermitted whole-house window swap is a common
way to get red-tagged.

**Doors** follow the same logic set — exterior doors need weatherstripping and proper flashing
at the header to keep water out of the framing above (a poorly flashed exterior door header is
a very common, slow-developing rot source); interior doors are largely a style/budget choice
(hollow-core vs. solid-core, pre-hung vs. slab) and, per the garage-conversion scope, are
typically priced to "match existing doors as long as they're available at the supplier as
pre-hung doors" up to a stated cost ceiling — a useful real-world example of how to write an
inclusion that protects your margin against unlimited custom-matching requests.

**Patio enclosures (a Manual-specific, LA-relevant hybrid of window and structural work):** must
be at least 60% window/opening area per the Manual's own spec, with 2", 3", or 4" thick walls,
single or double-pane insulated glass (Low-E optional), installed into tracks set on the existing
concrete slab and existing patio cover structure, with electrical added for outlets/AC/lights as
needed and French doors for entry — effectively converting an open patio cover into a
semi-conditioned room, a popular and profitable "room addition without the full addition cost"
sell.

**What fails, what to check:** failed seals (fogging between panes on a double-pane window means
the seal is gone and the unit needs replacement, not repair), inadequate caulking allowing water
intrusion at the frame-to-stucco joint, and — on new-construction installs specifically — any
gap in the weatherproofing sequence (skipping wire mesh or a coat of the 3-coat stucco process)
that lets water behind the new stucco.

**Rough LA costs:** retrofit vinyl window, installed, per unit: $500-$900 depending on size;
new-construction window (including stucco rebuild), per unit: $900-$1,800+; full-house window
replacement (10-15 windows), retrofit: $8,000-$15,000; exterior door replacement, installed:
$1,200-$2,500 including hardware.

## Day 19 — Countertops & Cabinets

**Why it matters:** Countertops and cabinets are the visual centerpiece of a kitchen sale and
carry meaningful material-selection lead time and margin — get the sequencing and the tile/slab
distinction right and you avoid the most common kitchen-remodel scheduling complaint.

### Learn
**Countertop installation (slab, per the Manual's real process):** start with detailed
measurements of the existing countertop footprint (or the new cabinet layout once cabinets are
set), then fabricate the new slab off-site — granite, quartz/Caesarstone ("Caesar Stone" in the
Manual), or another natural slab material, including the customer-selected bull-nose (front
edge) profile. Cover all work areas (cabinets get heavy-duty plastic, flooring gets protective
blankets all the way to the front door — a detail worth noting because slab install involves
carrying genuinely heavy material through the house). Demolish the existing countertop and
backsplash, disconnect plumbing, clean up. Install new ¾" plywood substrate with mold-treatment
sealer, cut the sink opening (if the sink is on-site at time of template/install), then set the
new slab on the plywood, glued down with special epoxy. Install the sink (under-mount or
top-mount), plumbing for the garbage disposal, and electrical outlets. Install the backsplash
(same slab material, epoxy-glued). Finish with **GFI outlets spaced no more than 4 feet (48
inches) apart**, so no point along the counter is more than 24 inches from a receptacle (NEC
210.52(C)) — the Manual's older "every 5 feet" figure for a standalone countertop job is out of
code and will fail rough/final inspection. Spacing them every 3 feet, as the full-kitchen scope
on Day 14 does, is a safe conservative default that stays inside the 4-foot maximum. Do not
exceed 4-foot spacing on a kitchen counter regardless of what an older scope sheet says.

**Countertop tile (the budget/DIY-friendly alternative to slab):** frame a wood substrate,
install chicken wire lifted about ½" off the surface with special plates (this creates a
reinforced concrete bed rather than tile sitting directly on plywood), pour 2,500 PSI concrete
and let it cure 48 hours, install tile with spacers, apply color-matched grout, then a heavy-duty
sealer coat on the grout.

**Why slab lead time drives the whole kitchen schedule:** slab countertops are template-measured
*after* cabinets are installed (so the fabricator has exact, as-built dimensions, not
architectural drawings that may be off by a fraction of an inch) and then fabricated off-site —
this is why countertop install reliably trails cabinet install by 1-2 weeks on every job, a
scheduling fact worth stating up front so a homeowner doesn't expect a same-week finish.

**Cabinets — tying back to Day 14's three tiers:** re-finish (sand/paint or stain the existing
boxes and doors in place), re-facing (new veneer or thermofoil face plus new doors/drawers on
the existing boxes), or full replacement (new boxes entirely, in custom — 3-week lead time —
or semi-custom — 5-day lead time — per the Manual). Cabinet hardware (handles, knobs, hinges)
is a small line item that has an outsized visual impact and is worth calling out separately in a
proposal, since it's a common area homeowners want to upgrade or customize even on a re-facing
job.

**What fails, what to check:** epoxy bond failure at seams (slab sections separating or
shifting) usually traces back to an unlevel or inadequately prepped plywood substrate; grout
cracking on a tile countertop usually traces back to an inadequately reinforced concrete bed
(the chicken-wire/lift step exists specifically to prevent this); and any GFI spacing that falls
short of code is a straightforward inspection failure.

**Rough LA costs:** quartz/granite slab countertop, installed, per SF: $50-$90 depending on
material grade; tile countertop, installed, per SF: $25-$45; cabinet re-finish: $1,500-$4,000;
re-facing: $4,000-$10,000; full semi-custom cabinet replacement (average kitchen): $8,000-
$18,000; full custom: $18,000-$40,000+.

<a id="day-20"></a>
## Day 20 — Landscaping, Hardscape & Driveways

**Why it matters:** Exterior hardscape (driveways, walkways, fences, artificial turf,
retaining walls) is high-visibility curb-appeal work that's easy to walk a homeowner through
in person and price with straightforward per-SF/LF math — a great category for building
measuring confidence ahead of Day 23.

### Learn
**Driveways — three systems, three cost/durability tiers (per the Manual):** *Asphalt* —
demo existing surface, prep/level/compact the base, apply and compact roughly ½" of gravel base,
then apply and compact 3" of asphalt; cheapest, fastest, but shortest lifespan and prone to
cracking/softening in heat. *Concrete* — demo and haul away, prep/level/compact, install a
2x4 wood frame and dividers to control pour sections, install 5/8" rebar or wire mesh lifted 2"
off the ground (critical — reinforcement sitting on the dirt instead of suspended in the pour
does almost nothing), pour 4" of 2,500 PSI concrete, finish it in the customer's chosen texture
(smooth, broom, salt, or stamped), apply color/stain if selected, and seal. Most durable, most
common premium choice. *Pavers* — demo and prep the base the same way, apply gravel or sea sand
base, build a concrete frame around the perimeter to hold the field, set pavers one by one in
the customer's chosen pattern, sweep sea sand into the joints as grout, and compact.
Highest-end look, most labor-intensive (hand-set piece by piece), and the easiest to repair
later since individual pavers can be lifted and reset without breaking up a slab.

**Fencing — four systems (per the Manual), each with the same foundation logic:** every fence
type starts with 18"x18" deep concrete piers for the posts (the foundation is what actually
resists wind/lateral load, not the fence material itself). *Chain link:* galvanized posts 8-10
ft on center, galvanized horizontal pipe, chain-link fabric attached to the frame. *Wrought
iron:* iron posts 8-10 ft on center, horizontal iron beams, custom-design iron infill. *Wood:*
4x4 posts at 8 ft on center, 2x4 horizontal frame, 1x6 facing boards in Douglas fir or redwood,
finished with stain/varnish or primer/paint. *Vinyl:* posts 6-8 ft on center (filled with
concrete or a 4x4 wood core inside the vinyl sleeve for rigidity), 2x4 vinyl rails, 1x4 vinyl
infill (privacy or open-style), with optional integrated post-cap lighting (requires running
electrical to the fence line first).

**Retaining walls (per the Manual's real block-wall process):** demo the existing wall/fence,
prep an 18"x18" trench, set rebar in an "X" pattern vertically every 3 feet plus horizontal
rebar tied to it (the Manual's shorthand for this is ¼" bar, but any wall that needs a permit
gets its rebar sized by the engineer — typically #4 / ½" minimum, not ¼"), fill the trench with
2,500 PSI concrete to the top, and — before that concrete cures — set the first course of
concrete block into it so the blocks sink and key into the wet concrete. Continue stacking block
with vertical rebar run through the block cores and mortar between courses, fill the block cores
solid with 2,500 PSI concrete, install a finished cap course, and finish with stucco in the
customer's chosen color. This is genuinely structural work — a retaining wall holding back soil
that fails can undermine a foundation or a slope. Rule of thumb: a wall over 4 feet (measured
from the bottom of the footing to the top), or any wall holding back a slope or surcharge, needs
a permit and stamped engineering — price the plans first, then the wall.

**None of the systems above manage water intentionally.** If a yard pools, or a retaining wall
traps runoff against a foundation, that's a French-drain/site-grading job, not a hardscape one —
see Day 31 for the full drainage-fix process (`playbooks/31-french-drain-site-drainage.md`).

**Artificial turf (per the Manual):** spray the area with weed killer, remove 2-4" of topsoil
and compact, plan drainage and install a border, install a weed barrier, install a crushed-
rock/gravel base, grade/level/moisten/compact the base, lay out and cut the turf, seam sections
together, add infill product per the manufacturer's spec, then water the lawn for final
inspection (the water test checks that drainage actually works, not just that the turf looks
good dry).

**Rough LA costs (installed, per SF unless noted):** asphalt driveway: $6-$10; concrete
driveway: $10-$18 (stamped/colored adds $3-$6 more); pavers: $18-$30; wood fence, per LF: $35-
$60; vinyl fence, per LF: $45-$75; concrete block retaining wall, per SF of wall face: $35-$60+
depending on height/engineering; artificial turf: $10-$16/SF installed.

### 🗣️ How Josh sells this

## Day 21 — Pools, Decks & Patio Covers

**Why it matters:** Pools and outdoor living spaces are premium, high-ticket SoCal-lifestyle
sales — and patio covers/decks carry real waterproofing and structural stakes (they're
essentially small roofs) that a lot of competitors underscope.

### Learn
**Pool equipment repair/replacement (per the Manual):** empty the pool/spa of water as needed,
replace the pump, filter, heater/furnace, and the plumbing/pipes in the equipment pad area, refill
the pool, and test the system for proper function. This is mechanical, recurring-revenue work —
pumps and filters wear out on a predictable cycle, making it a good repeat-customer category.

**Pool re-plaster (per the Manual's real process — timing-sensitive work):** empty the pool/spa
completely, then "wipe" the old plaster off — demolish/blast away 2-3" of the existing plaster
using a high-pressure compressor with multiple outlets, clean up debris. Demo and replace old
tile (customer supplies tile). Pour new plaster in the customer's chosen color and hand-smooth it
from top to bottom before it sets — critically, **all plaster must be completed in a single day
with no cold joints** (a joint where one day's plaster meets the next day's is a visible, lasting
flaw and a failure point), sand and smooth as needed, grout the tile, install all plastic
fittings/covers, replace pool and spa lighting, then fill completely with water — and **the
customer must perform the chemical "start-up" process within 2 weeks**, a hard deadline because
fresh plaster that sits too long without proper water chemistry can etch or discolor
permanently.

**Wood patio cover (per the Manual, waterproof/lattice style):** demo the old cover, prep and
pour a 2,500 PSI concrete foundation for new posts (rebar suspended 2" off grade, same principle
as Day 20's driveway), set 4x4 or 4x6 posts, attach a ledger plate to the house's stucco wall,
run lattice or solid beams between the ledger and the posts, add angled bracing, sheath with ¾"
plywood, then — this is the roof-like part — install a full roofing assembly on top: torch-down
hot mop, OR 30/50-year composition shingles over 30 lb tar paper, OR tile/clay over 30 lb tar
paper, matching whatever roofing system the main house uses. Finish with stain/varnish or
paint, add a ceiling fan/light with its own switch, then **hose down the finished cover to
field-test the waterproofing** before calling it done — the same "test before you trust it"
discipline as the Day 15 shower pan. Critical note: if there's an existing electrical box on the
wall where the ledger attaches, it must be relocated — this is a common surprise mid-job if not
caught during the sales walkthrough.

**Aluminum patio cover (flat pan, W-shape, or insulated R-40):** same concrete foundation and
post-setting process, but instead of wood/plywood/roofing, you install an aluminum track on the
stucco and snap in prefabricated aluminum cover panels — the insulated R-40 option has built-in
rain gutters, so downspouts get added directly; skylights can be integrated; joints get 50-year
caulking for waterproofing. Aluminum covers install faster than wood/lattice covers (no
multi-layer roofing assembly) and need far less maintenance, which is a strong selling point
against the wood option for a homeowner who doesn't want to repaint every few years.

**Decking / re-decking (waterproof layer, per the Manual):** remove the old deck surface, install
new CDX plywood, add ventilation pipes if the assembly needs them, install 30 lb tar paper, new
edge metal, then 30/50-year fiberglass shingles as the wear surface, with new metal flashing at
every pipe/vent. This is functionally a small flat roof built as a walkable deck — the same
flashing-at-penetrations discipline from Day 6 applies directly.

**This exact waterproofing scope is also a hard legal requirement on multifamily buildings.**
SB721 (3+ unit rentals) and SB326 (condo/HOA developments) mandate periodic inspection of
elevated decks/balconies by a qualified inspector. AB 2579 extended only SB721's initial-
inspection deadline to January 1, 2026; SB326's initial deadline was January 1, 2025 and was
never extended, so a qualifying HOA without a completed inspection is already out of compliance
— see Day 29 for the full SB721/SB326 breakdown, including who's qualified to sign each.

**Rough LA costs:** pool equipment replacement (pump/filter/heater): $2,500-$6,000; pool
re-plaster (average pool, excluding tile): $6,000-$12,000; wood patio cover with roofing:
$12,000-$25,000 depending on size/roof type; aluminum insulated patio cover: $18-$28/SF; deck/
re-deck with waterproof membrane: $20-$35/SF.

### 🗣️ How Josh sells this

Guards are code-required once a deck's walking surface is more than 30 inches above grade (CRC
R312), and an attached deck generally needs a permit regardless of height in LA-area
jurisdictions — freestanding low decks may be exempt only under narrow size/height limits. Any
elevated or attached deck — and anything the city wants engineered — needs a permit and, where
required, a stamped plan. If a client wants to skip that and self-handle it, Josh writes an
exact liability-shield line into the contract:
"Owner will provide engineer plan and city permit plus call city inspector," which protects the
contractor if an inspector ever stops by. On room additions vs. patio enclosures, he steers
budget- or timeline-tight clients toward the enclosure: it takes under a month and doesn't count
toward taxable square footage, while a full room addition can take nearly a year and does raise
property taxes — a better outcome for the client that also closes faster for him. (Source:
`04.07-product-deck-patio.md`)

## Day 22 — Solar, Garage Doors & ADUs

**Why it matters:** These are the three biggest-ticket, most jurisdictionally complex sales in
the business — solar and ADUs both require multi-agency approval, and garage conversions are
effectively a full miniature construction project with every trade from Days 1-19 rolled into
one job.

### Learn
**Solar — the approval chain matters as much as the install (per the Manual's real process):**
first, produce a drawing set specifying exact panel locations and all attached equipment. That
plan set then goes to **three separate approvals**: the city/county Building & Safety and
Electrical departments (structural and electrical permit), the **fire department** (solar
installs have fire-code setback and access-pathway requirements so firefighters can safely work
a roof), and the **utility company** (DWP or SCE) for interconnection review, since the system
will tie into the grid. Only after those approvals does install happen: mount roughly 11 panels
(sized to the customer's target kW/DC system size) using rails, mounting hardware, and ground
clamps; install a combiner box, a DC disconnect, and an AC disconnect; run conduit for all
wiring; make the connections. All work follows the local code and the panel manufacturer's
install specs. **Why this matters in sales conversations:** the utility interconnection step
(known industry-wide as "permission to operate") is often the longest lead-time item on a solar
job — homeowners frequently think the system is "done" once panels are physically installed and
are surprised it can't be turned on for weeks while the utility processes the interconnection
paperwork; setting that expectation up front avoids a frustrated customer at the finish line.

**Garage doors:** a lower-ticket but high-frequency sale — sectional roll-up doors (steel,
wood, or composite) in standard single (8-9 ft) or double (16 ft) widths, with insulated vs.
non-insulated panel options (insulated adds R-value and reduces noise transfer into an attached
garage — relevant if the garage shares a wall with living space), and increasingly sold with a
smart opener (app/Wi-Fi control) as a natural upsell. Sizing and structural header requirements
above a garage door opening follow the same header logic from Day 5 — a wider opening needs a
correspondingly larger header, which matters if a homeowner wants to widen a single garage door
to a double.

**ADUs (Accessory Dwelling Units) — the two paths:** *Garage conversion* — converting an
existing attached or detached garage into livable space, which the Manual's own 5-page real scope
walks through end to end: demo interior/exterior walls and old fixtures (water heater, windows,
closet), frame the new addition per approved architectural plans with new load-bearing posts
and a flush beam replacing any removed wall, install shear walls per engineering, frame in new
windows and doors, insulate at R-13 (the Manual's figure — confirm against current Title 24 for
the project's climate zone; 2x4 walls commonly need R-15 cavity, or R-13 plus continuous
insulation, to pass energy compliance). Then site work: relocate the electrical panel and gas
meter per utility requirements, install a new high-efficiency tankless water heater, match new
stucco to the existing house, install a new torch-down roof section, and — a detail worth
remembering — **identify and protect existing sewer/water/electrical lines before pouring any
new foundation**, wrapping any line that will run through the new concrete so the pour itself
doesn't damage it. The scope then runs through a full mini bathroom remodel (shower pan framing,
rough plumbing, electrical, hot mop, tile, drywall/paint, fixtures), full electrical package
(recessed lighting, smoke/CO detectors — now required by code in bedrooms and hallways),
interior paint, and new wood flooring — genuinely a complete small addition, not a simple
"finish the garage" job, and the Manual's own version states a real **4-month estimated
duration** with a stated daily late-fee clause, which is a useful real template for how this
company writes a timeline commitment into a contract. *Detached ADU (new structure)* — a
free-standing unit built new in the backyard, which involves a full foundation, framing, and
all-systems build (essentially a small house) rather than converting existing space, and
carries its own, generally more involved planning/zoning review (setbacks, height limits, parking
requirements vary significantly by jurisdiction) even though California state ADU law has
streamlined approval statewide in recent years.

**CA ADU law you can quote at the appointment:** approval is ministerial with a 60-day clock (no
discretionary hearing); side/rear setbacks can be as low as 4 feet; an 800 sq ft / 16-ft-tall ADU
is allowed by right on most single-family lots; impact/development fees are waived under 750 sq
ft; converting a garage requires no replacement parking, and no new parking is required within
1/2 mile of transit; owner-occupancy is generally not required. A detached new-build still needs
full plan check, but it rides these same state streamlining rules.

**What inspectors/jurisdictions specifically watch on ADUs and garage conversions:** confirmed
egress (a legal bedroom needs an operable window meeting minimum size for emergency exit),
correct smoke/CO detector placement, proper separation/fire-rating if the ADU shares a wall with
the main house or another structure, and utility capacity (panel amperage, water heater sizing)
adequate for the added living space — this ties directly back to Day 10's panel-upgrade
conversation.

**Rough LA costs:** garage door replacement, installed: $1,200-$3,000 (insulated, standard
size); solar system, average SFR (5-7 kW): $15,000-$25,000 before incentives; garage conversion
to livable ADU space (per the Manual's real scope, roughly 368 SF addition plus bath): often
$120,000-$180,000+ all-in; detached new ADU: typically $250-$400+/SF given full ground-up
systems.

## Day 23 — Estimating & Pricing: The Anchor-and-Close Formula

**Why it matters:** Every day before this one taught you what the work *is*. This day teaches
you how to turn what you see into a number — the skill that actually generates revenue. Josh's
real formula is a **two-tier** system, not one multiple applied once, and getting the two tiers
straight is the whole game.

### Learn
**Measuring is the foundation of every accurate bid — and it's also a sales tool.** Measure
everything, even where it isn't strictly necessary — it builds credibility by showing your price
is grounded in real numbers, not a guess (06-the-soup, step 3 "Measurements"; Ori's-notes
cross-reference). Measure in the units the trade actually uses: **linear feet (LF)** for
anything sold by run length — fencing, baseboards, countertop edge, gutters, crown molding;
**square feet (SF)** for anything sold by area — flooring, paint (wall area), roofing,
driveways, patio covers, drywall; and count/each for fixtures, outlets, windows, doors, recessed
lights. Always measure the room itself (length x width for floor SF; perimeter x wall height for
paint SF, then subtract large openings if being precise) and record it on the spot. If a client
skips rapport and asks for the price right away, measuring is also your fallback opener — start
there instead of forcing small talk first (06-the-soup, step 3).

**Building a takeoff — the practical method:** walk the space methodically (a consistent order —
clockwise around the room, for instance — prevents missed walls or fixtures), write measurements
down as you go with a simple sketch, then convert raw dimensions into the quantities each trade
needs: a 12'x10' kitchen floor is 120 SF of flooring; a run of upper and lower cabinets along two
walls totaling 22 feet is 22 LF of cabinet run; a bathroom with an 8'x5' floor and a 5'x7' shower
enclosure at 7' high is 40 SF of floor tile and roughly 84 SF of shower wall tile. This is
exactly the math you practiced in the field exercises on Days 14, 15, 16, 19, and 20 — today you
formalize it into a repeatable process, and feed it into the real pricing formula below.

**The real formula is two numbers, not one — the anchor and the close (00-sales-playbook §4;
06-the-soup, steps 8-9).** Josh doesn't price a job by applying a single multiple once. He gives
the client two different prices, on two different bases, in sequence:
- **The anchor** — about 10 minutes before the client's actual price, tell them about 2-3
  similar past projects and quote **those** at **4x-4.5x your cost**. This is not the client's
  price; it's a reference point that makes the real number feel like a deal.
- **The real price** — the client's actual job, quoted at **3x-3.5x your cost** — lower than the
  anchor, so it reads as a discount even though it's still a healthy margin. **Never give a
  round number** — quote $42,750, not "$43k" (Ori's-notes cross-reference).

**Cash vs. finance — same job, framed differently (06-the-soup, step 7).** Cash is quoted as a
total price; finance is quoted in payments — the same underlying number, presented in whichever
form matches how the client is actually paying.

**The exact sequence for giving the price (06-the-soup, step 9 "Giving the Price"):** tell the
client what you're going to do → ask if they have questions → answer them → recap what you're
going to do → **then** state the price → stop talking and let the client speak first. If they
say no on the spot: **"Not a problem, the price is good for 90 days"** — leaves the door open
without pressure (06-the-soup, step 9).

**Why the multiple has to be that large, not cost-plus-30%.** The 4-4.5x/3-3.5x range has to
cover far more than materials and labor: overhead (office, insurance, vehicles, admin staff),
sales commission, marketing/lead cost to generate the appointment, project management time,
warranty/callback reserve, and the fact that not every appointment closes and not every closed
job runs at full profit — the multiple on jobs that *do* close has to carry the ones that don't.
A contractor pricing at cost-plus-30% looks more "honest" on paper but runs out of cash the first
time a job overruns or a lead goes cold.

**Putting it together on a real bid:** (1) measure and count everything on-site, narrating what
you're checking and why as you go (that's Day 24's credibility point, but it starts here); (2)
price out the hard cost — actual supplier cost for materials, actual crew cost for labor; (3)
build the anchor price on 2-3 reference-project comparables at 4-4.5x cost; (4) build the real
price on the client's actual hard cost at 3-3.5x cost, as an exact non-round number; (5)
sanity-check both against the "rough LA cost" ranges you've been collecting all course (Days
4-22 each gave you one) — if either number is wildly outside range, re-check your takeoff or
cost inputs before presenting.

- [Ori's Construction Sales Notes](https://docs.google.com/document/d/16PMU4xuGZwRIaVFGovdoTGlldmT1dBhsVHbowcb1A5g/edit) — cross-referenced against the whiteboard transcription for the pricing/measuring sections
- `knowledge/sales-training/06-the-soup.md` and `knowledge/sales-training/00-sales-playbook.md` §4 — the primary sourced material for today

## Day 24 — The Soup & Closing: The Josh Bloom Sales System

**Why it matters:** Everything you've learned in Days 1-23 is useless in a sales role until you
can deliver it in a room with a homeowner, build trust fast, and close. "The Soup" is Josh's own
name for the full 9-step in-home call, literally titled "SOUP" on his whiteboard — this is the
day the technical knowledge becomes revenue.

### Learn
**The Soup — Josh's 9-step call structure, in order (06-the-soup):**
1. **Connection** — find something in common with the client, compliment them, smile, use
   physical connection (handshake/shoulder tap) and mental connection (look/smell/dress well).
2. **Building a Strategy** — tell the client about 2-3 projects you're doing in their
   neighborhood, for three reasons: proves you're local, pre-loads ammunition for closing later,
   and gives you leverage if they later want to cancel.
3. **Measurements** — take measurements to show how you arrived at the price. This is also the
   fallback opener if the client skips rapport and wants a number right away (Day 23).
4. **Build the Product** — explain the product/scope in extreme detail.
5. **Build Yourself and the Company** — personal credibility (family man, time in the business,
   charity, successful, smart, religious, no drinking/gambling) and company credibility
   (licensed/bonded/insured/workers' comp, reviews, big projects, warranties, family business,
   "apples to apples" guarantee).
6. **Stories** — **75% of the time you speak must be in stories**, not raw facts (exact
   whiteboard quote).
7. **Cash vs. Finance** — cash is quoted as a total price; finance is quoted in payments.
8. **Building the Price** — the anchor: 2-3 reference projects at 4-4.5x cost, given about 10
   minutes before the real price (Day 23).
9. **Giving the Price** — the real price at 3-3.5x cost, delivered via the tell/ask/answer/
   recap/price/shut-up sequence, then wait 10 minutes before any rebuttal (Day 23).

Ori's own notes (an independent source) describe this exact rapport method as "The Soup" and
separately confirm the same numbers — compliments 2-3x/hour, measure everything, 4-4.5x anchor,
3-3.5x close, non-round prices — which is strong cross-confirmation this is the real, consistently
taught system and not a one-off example. Ori's notes also reference "look above the fireplace" —
scanning for family photos or mementos as a Connection-step cue — though this specific
interpretation is unverified beyond Ori's own notes.

**The 6 Closing/Rebuttal tools, deployed after the 10-minute wait (07-closing):**
1. **Exit / Bathroom / Phone** — a natural pause/reset point if the close stalls.
2. **Company gift card** — an incentive/concession to restart momentum.
3. **Social proof** — front yard sign, before/after photos, video testimonial, or a good review
   (sometimes traded for a discount).
4. **Benjamin Franklin (pros/cons) close** — Pros, asked as yes/no questions: "You like me?"
   "You like the company?" "You like the product?" "You like that we're licensed, bonded, and
   insured?" Cons: **only** "Do you like the price?" — deliberately the single item on the cons
   side, isolating price as the one objection against a wall of "yes" answers.
5. **Good cop / bad cop.**
6. **"Car"** — exact mechanic unverified; possibly a manager-escalation stall (the source video
   for this module wasn't accessed in the extraction pass, so items 1, 5, and 6 are captured only
   as one-line labels, not full scripts).

These 6 tools are framed as "Rebuttals/Loops" — meant to be cycled through if the client keeps
objecting, not a single one-shot close.

**Before you run any of this as paid work, not training:** everything in this day assumes you're
legally allowed to sell home improvement contracts as a salesperson. California requires anyone
who solicits, negotiates, or executes a home improvement contract for a licensed contractor to be
registered with CSLB as a Home Improvement Salesperson (HIS) — see Day 25 for the exact rule
before you ever run a live, unsupervised appointment.

**Objection-handling scripts, tagged to their source module (00-sales-playbook §5):**
- Price is $20K+ higher than other bids → sell personal service and dedicated specialist crews
  over one generalist; brain-surgeon analogy; horror stories of cheap contractors taking 6-12+
  months; close with years in business to back the warranty (04.06-kitchen).
- Client thinks a paint/remodel job takes 1-2 days → explain a professional job takes 2-3 weeks
  of real prep, and that skipping prep is exactly what let the fast/cheap job happen
  (04.05-painting).
- Client anxious about mid-project mess/damage → normalize it up front and explain the
  end-of-job walkthrough/cleanup process before it can blindside them (04.05-painting;
  04.06-kitchen).
- "Let me ask my neighbor" / a shared-cost wall or fence → stop, don't quote yet; get the
  neighbor's contact info immediately and book both parties together same-day or next-day
  (04.04-walls-fences).
- Client wants a cheaper shortcut on scope → advise against it, and if they insist, document the
  warning IN WRITING on the contract to protect yourself (04.05-painting; 04.06-kitchen).
- Client doesn't trust giving a down payment → if you trust them, write "$0.00" instead of
  leaving it blank; otherwise negotiate down to $500/$100/$50 rather than $0
  (05-write-a-contract; see Day 25).
- Client objects to the arbitration clause → explain arbitration is faster than court (though
  pricier); if they flatly refuse to initial, call your boss/owner immediately
  (05-write-a-contract; see Day 25).

**Why this holds together as one thing, not nine separate tricks:** rapport (steps 1-2) earns the
right to ask real diagnostic questions; measuring and building the product/company (3-5) generate
real numbers while building visible credibility; stories (6) carry 75% of the persuasion; the
anchor and real price (7-9) are given in a deliberate sequence designed to make the real number
feel like a deal; and the 10-minute wait plus the 6 closing tools handle what's left. Skipping
"The Soup" to rush to a price makes every later stage harder, because none of the credibility or
diagnostic information it generates exists yet.

## Day 25 — Write a Contract + Capstone: Run the Full Appointment

**Why it matters:** A closed verbal price means nothing until it's written correctly — and
written wrong, it's a legal liability instead of a protection. This is also the test: everything
from the load path in Day 1 to the closing tools in Day 24 gets used in one realistic exercise.
If you can do both convincingly, you can run a real appointment start to finish.

### Learn
**Before any of this: HIS registration.** Everything on this page assumes you're legally
entitled to solicit, price, and sign home improvement contracts as a salesperson. California law
(B&P §7150.1 et seq., specifically §7152) requires anyone who solicits, negotiates, or executes a
home improvement contract for a licensed contractor to be registered with CSLB as a Home
Improvement Salesperson (HIS) — at any dollar amount, regardless of experience. The only
carve-outs are an officer, member, or general partner listed on the license itself, or someone
who purely books appointments without pricing or closing. The company must also file the HIS
association form with CSLB before you start selling. Running appointments unregistered is a
misdemeanor for both you and the company — get your HIS registration confirmed before you're
ever alone in a client's home with a contract to sign.

**The down payment cap is real California law, not a company preference
(05-write-a-contract).** A home improvement contract cannot legally take a down payment larger
than **$1,000 or 10% of the contract price, whichever is LESS** — this maps to California
Business & Professions Code §7159.5, the down-payment and progress-payment limit (§7159 is the
companion statute governing the contract's required content and disclosures), and it's the
practical rule Josh's training teaches salesmen to apply on every deal. On a $450,000 ADU, the
cap is $1,000 (not $45,000 — 10% would be far more, so "whichever is less" caps it at $1,000);
on an $8,000 job, the cap is 10% = $800. If a client won't give the full legal max, still try to
secure at least $500, $100, or $50 — never leave it truly blank; if you trust the client, write
"$0.00" rather than a blank field.

**Final-payment sizing — never leave a large chunk on the table for the end
(05-write-a-contract).** On a $450,000 project, the final payment should be only ~$20,000 (about
4-5%); a $50,000 project should hold no more than $4,000-$5,000 for the end; a $10,000 project,
no more than $1,500. Exact milestone wording matters: first milestone = **"Upon start of job and
delivery of some materials"**; final milestone = **"Upon job completion."**

**Completion-date buffer table — never write a tight date (05-write-a-contract).** Missing an
exact completion date is a breach of contract, so pad generously: the Manual's own numbers are a
1-2 day job → give yourself 1 month; a 1-month job (bathroom, kitchen) → 6 months; a
6-month-to-1-year job (room addition, ADU) → 2 years. Know the legal boundary on this: B&P §7159
requires the completion date be "approximate" — a good-faith estimate, not an arbitrary
placeholder — so padding this generous is defensible as weather/permit/change-order protection,
but coaching a client that it "isn't a real estimate" drifts toward misrepresentation if a
regulator or a dispute ever looks at it. A more proportionate buffer (a 1-month job → 2-3 months,
a 6-month job → 9-12 months) gives the same protection with far less exposure, and is worth using
if a client pushes back hard on the longer numbers. If a client questions a buffer that looks
long for a short job: "Listen, you
never know what could happen. Something will happen, God forbid, and we don't want to [be in
breach]" — frame the buffer as protection, not a real estimate.

**Contract-writing mechanics (05-write-a-contract):** the salesman never puts personal
name/phone/email in the contractor-info block — "you're not the contractor; you're the
salesman" — personal info only goes at the bottom. List all owners by name (husband + wife;
note a co-signing adult child in parentheses) and get 2-3 phone numbers plus email. By law, the
client gets **3 business days to cancel** before work starts, so the scheduled start date should
be at least 3-4 days out from signing — but a buyer 65 or older gets **5 business days** to
cancel (Civil Code §1689.6), with the 5-day language printed in the notice; for a senior client,
schedule the start at least 5-6 days out and make sure the contract shows the 5-day senior
cancellation notice, not the 3-day one. For a genuine emergency (leak, crumbling foundation),
have the client sign a specific waiver of the cancellation right, on which they write WHY they're
waiving it. Mark all required disclosure checkboxes (Home Improvement Contract, Arbitration Notice, 3-Day
Cancellation Notice, Liability/Insurance Disclosure, Workers' Comp Insurance, License copy,
Mechanic's Lien Warning) and collect all mandatory initials before leaving. If a client objects
to the arbitration clause, explain it resolves disputes faster than court (though pricier); if
they flatly refuse to initial, call your boss/owner before proceeding.

**The 62+ elderly-client script (05-write-a-contract).** For a client 62 or older, living alone,
with no family present: ask **"Has anybody helped you make the decision?"** If they say no, have
them sign the standard line, then have them physically hand-write a second line: **"I am the
only decision maker."** This protects the contractor if an adult child later complains about a
parent being signed up alone.

**The protective clauses you've already seen this course, now as a set (all
05-write-a-contract-adjacent, sourced from their trade modules):**
- **85% paint-match clause** — contract states "will match up to 85%" of existing paint, never
  100% (Day 17; 04.05-painting).
- **Wood-damage contingency clause** — base price covers "up to two to three pieces of wood";
  more found = change order (Day 17; 04.05-painting).
- **Engineering liability shield** — "Owner will provide engineer plan and city permit plus call
  city inspector," written whenever a client self-handles engineering on a deck or wall (Day 20,
  21; 04.04-walls-fences, 04.07-deck-patio). Josh's rough trigger is height ("3-3.5 ft"), but the
  actual rules are stricter: deck guards are code-required past 30 inches, attached decks
  generally need a permit at any height, and a retaining wall needs a permit/engineer past 4 feet
  or when it holds back a slope/surcharge — use this clause any time engineering is in play, not
  just past a height guess.

These clauses all work the same way: they pre-authorize a change order the moment a stated
threshold is crossed, rather than leaving it to an on-site argument. See Day 33 for the full
change-order document mechanics — pricing convention, unforeseen-conditions clause language, and
the rule that work never proceeds on changed scope until the client has signed.

**Post-sale protection you're already carrying, tied together:** general liability insurance
typically covers $500K-$2M in home damage; the CSLB contractor license bond is **$25,000**
(raised from $15,000 on January 1, 2023 by SB 607) — a recovery pool if the licensed company
fails to perform or pay, and a floor set by the state, not full damage coverage; Workers' Comp
absorbs medical liability if a worker is hurt on the property.
If unpaid, a mechanic's lien must be filed within **90 days** of completion, then enforced via
court/arbitration within another **90 days** after filing.

**Capstone — two mock jobs, run start to finish, now using the full system (Days 1-24
integrated):**

**Job 1 — a kitchen remodel appointment.** Run the full Soup (Day 24): connection, 2-3
neighborhood-project mentions, measure the kitchen out loud (LF of cabinet run, SF of floor and
countertop, outlet count — Day 23), build the product and your credibility, tell a story instead
of a fact list, flag the soffit/structural check (Day 1) and any hazmat flag if the home predates
1978/1990 (Day 13), identify which of the three kitchen tiers fits their stated budget (Day 14).
Give the cash-vs-finance framing, then the anchor (2-3 reference kitchens at 4-4.5x cost) about
10 minutes before the real price (this job's actual hard cost at 3-3.5x, as an exact non-round
number). Wait 10 minutes, then handle at least one objection with a scripted rebuttal
(Day 24) — deploy the Benjamin Franklin close if it stalls. Write the contract: correct down
payment (capped at $1,000 or 10% of this job's price, whichever is less), correct milestone
wording, a completion-date buffer sized to the job's real length, and the elderly-client script
if the homeowner is 62+ and alone.

**Job 2 — a roof appointment for a homeowner who called about a leak.** Diagnose flashing
failure (Day 6) vs. end-of-life shingle failure using the load-path lens (Day 1), run the Soup's
rapport and product-building steps, measure the roof in squares and count penetrations (Day 23),
give the anchor and real price the same way as Job 1, and close using the Soup/Closing system.
Write the contract with the same rigor: correct down payment cap, milestone wording, and
completion buffer for a job this length.

**What "done well" looks like for both:** a scope specific enough that another contractor
couldn't quietly underbid it by leaving out permits, engineering, hazmat testing, or a
waterproofing test; a price you can defend line by line back to a hard cost and the anchor/close

the down payment cap, milestone wording, and completion buffer; and an appointment that opened
with genuine rapport, not a rushed pitch.

## Day 26 — Design Styles: the 24-Style Library & Reading a Client's Taste

**Why it matters:** Every remodel sale is secretly a design sale — the client isn't buying
drywall and cabinets, they're buying a picture of how their house will feel. A GC who can name
the style a client is gesturing at, cite its three signature elements, and spec real, sourceable
materials for it closes against contractors who say "we can do whatever you want." This day
plugs you into the same style library that powers the app's Design Studio and the Knowledge Q&A
— one knowledge base, three doors into it.

### Learn
**The library.** The company style library lives in the app (`knowledge/design-styles.json`,
served at the Design view's style chips, and queryable in Knowledge Q&A — ask it "what defines
Japandi?"). It holds 24 styles across five families:

- **Modern:** Mid-Century Modern, Minimalist, Contemporary Luxe, Industrial Loft, Organic Modern, Desert Modern (Palm Springs)
- **Classic:** Traditional, Transitional, Craftsman, French Country, Art Deco, Resort / Country Club
- **Organic:** Japandi, Japanese Zen, Scandinavian, Wabi-Sabi, California Casual
- **Mediterranean/Revival:** Spanish Revival, Mediterranean, Tuscan
- **Eclectic/Coastal:** Modern Farmhouse, Coastal (Hamptons), Bohemian, Moroccan

For each style the library carries: 5-7 signature elements, 6-9 real sourceable materials with
rough SoCal installed costs, a palette, lighting and furniture guidance, a mood, what to AVOID
(the tells that make a style read fake or cheap), and designer notes you can say to a client
verbatim.

**Reading a client's taste (the skill, not the list).** Never open with "what style do you
want?" — most clients don't have the vocabulary and will freeze or guess wrong. Instead read
three concrete sources: (1) **their saved images** — ask to see their Pinterest board or
screenshots folder; you're looking for repeated elements (always white kitchens? always warm
wood? always black windows?), not the labels they put on them; (2) **their existing furniture
and objects** — what they own and love survives the remodel and constrains the palette; (3)
**the house's own architecture** — a 1920s Craftsman bungalow fights a Contemporary Luxe
interior, and SoCal housing stock has natural pairings (Spanish Revival and Mediterranean for
1920s-30s stucco; Mid-Century and Desert Modern for 1950s-60s ranches and post-and-beams;
Craftsman for pre-war bungalows; almost anything for 1980s+ boxes). When the client's cues
conflict, Transitional is the honest recommendation — it's the style built for "some of both."

**Style families as a sales tool.** Clients say adjectives, not styles: "warm but modern" →
Japandi / Organic Modern / California Casual; "clean, no clutter" → Minimalist / Scandinavian;
"like a Santa Barbara hotel" → Spanish Revival / Mediterranean; "cozy, like a cabin but nicer"
→ Craftsman / Modern Farmhouse. Learn the adjective-to-family map and you can steer any
conversation to 2-3 named candidates, then let the Design Studio's renders settle it.

**What "sourceable" means.** Every material in the library is something you can actually buy
and install in LA — if a client falls in love with a render, the spec behind it can't be
fantasy. That's the standard for anything you promise: name the material, know its rough
installed cost, know its lead time class (stock, 2-3 weeks, or 6-8+ weeks).

## Day 27 — The Design Studio: AI Renders, Design-to-Scope & Sourcing

**Why it matters:** The Design view (`app → 🎨 Design`) turns a phone photo of the client's
actual room into a photoreal restyled render in ~10 seconds, in any of the 24 styles — in the
client's living room, on your iPhone or iPad, mid-appointment. Used right it's the strongest
closing tool the company has; used wrong (renders presented as photos, specs that can't be
sourced) it's a liability. This day is how to run it right, and how to turn an approved concept
into a scope of work.

### Learn
**The tool, end to end.** Photograph the room (landscape, from a corner, lights on, declutter
what you can — the render preserves what the camera sees). Tap a style chip or type plain
speech ("white shaker cabinets, brass hardware, keep the window"). The chat composes a render
instruction on five axes — style, lighting, materials, mood, camera/structure preservation —
grounded in the style library's card for the named style. Modes: **Redecorate** (walls,
cabinets, layout all stay — finishes and furnishings change) vs **Remodel** (cabinetry/layout
may reconfigure inside the same footprint). **Variations** 1/2/4 render in parallel (4 costs
the same wait as 1, ~10s). **Quality** Fast (~8-15s) for live appointments, Max (~15-25s) for
the send-home version. Exteriors get their own structure lock: the tool keeps the camera
framing, footprint, rooflines, and window/door positions, and only re-finishes surfaces,
fixtures, hardscape and planting.

**The legal/trust line (non-negotiable).** Every render is an AI **concept**, never a photo,
and never a promise: the app bakes an "AI concept visualization — JOON Design Studio" caption
onto every saved after-image, and you SAY it in the room ("this is a concept of the direction —
the finish schedule is what we actually contract"). A CSLB-licensed contractor showing a
homeowner a fake "photo" of a promised result is a consumer-protection problem; a contractor
showing a labeled concept next to a written allowance schedule is a professional.

**Design-to-scope (where the money is).** An approved concept converts to a contract through a
**finishes schedule**: walk the render surface by surface and write the line items — cabinet
fronts (door style, material, finish), counters (material, edge, thickness), backsplash,
flooring, paint (walls/trim/ceiling), lighting fixtures (count + type), plumbing fixtures,
hardware — each with either a firm spec (material named, cost known) or an **allowance** (a
dollar bucket the client shops within, e.g. "tile allowance $12/sqft installed"). The style
library's materials-with-costs exist precisely so your allowances are realistic on day one.
Rule: anything visible in the render the client fell in love with must appear in the schedule
— a render element with no scope line is a future change-order fight.

**Sourcing reality.** Stock/quick-ship (paint, big-box tile, stock hardware): days. Semi-custom
cabinets: ~5 days to 3 weeks (Manual's tiers, Day 19). Custom cabinets, specialty tile, plaster
finishes, imported stone: 6-8+ weeks — flag these in the appointment so the schedule
conversation happens BEFORE the contract. Tie back to Day 23 pricing: the finishes schedule is
the "build the product" step with real numbers attached.

**Failure modes to catch before the client does.** Check every render before presenting:
window/door positions moved? furniture scale believable? duplicated fixtures (old faucet AND
new)? a view through a window that changed? If yes — regenerate; it takes 10 seconds. Never
hand-wave a broken render; it undercuts every real photo in your portfolio.

## Day 28 — Ground-Up New Construction: Lot to Foundation

**Why it matters:** Building a house from a bare lot is the highest-dollar job type in
the business, and every mistake made before the first stud goes up — a bad soils
assumption, a missed setback, an unpermitted grading cut — compounds for the life of
the house. Every other day in this course assumes a foundation already exists; this is
the front half that makes it legal and buildable.

### Learn
**Lot due diligence.** Pull zoning first — ZIMAS (zimas.lacity.org) for City of LA,
the applicable city/county planning portal elsewhere (see Day 3). Confirm the buildable
envelope (setbacks, height limit for the zone/height district) and the Floor Area Ratio
(FAR) or lot-coverage cap, which caps house size independent of setbacks. Cross-check
the parcel against CAL FIRE's Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zone (VHFHSZ) map — a
hit changes the construction spec (Chapter 7A, defensible space — Day 30) and can
affect insurability before a permit is pulled. Hillside lots add a Hillside
Construction Regulation (HCR)/Baseline Hillside Ordinance (BHO) overlay — grading
limits and height-from-grade rules. Full playbook:
`knowledge/playbooks/53-zoning-basics-la.md`.

**Soils & geotechnical report.** Required on essentially every new-SFR plan check. A
licensed geotechnical engineer drills borings, tests bearing capacity (how much load
the soil can carry before it compresses or fails), checks for expansive clay (soil that
swells/shrinks and can crack a slab if undesigned-for), and flags groundwater depth and
slope stability on hillside lots. The foundation type gets picked from this report — not
the other way around. Rough cost/timeline: $2,500-$6,000 [EST], 2-4 weeks [EST] for a
flat-lot SFR; hillside runs higher and longer.

**Land/boundary survey.** Pulled before design, not after. A licensed surveyor
establishes legal property lines, easements, and a benchmark elevation the architect
uses for finish-floor height. Designing off an assumed lot line instead is how a
foundation ends up encroaching a setback or easement — often an unfixable
mid-construction discovery. Rough cost: $1,500-$4,000 [EST].

**Plan check for new SFR construction.** A new house needs a full sheet set:
architectural, structural (stamped by a licensed engineer), Title 24 energy (Day 11),
plus a civil/grading sheet on hillside/complex lots. Expect a longer plan-check cycle
than an addition — commonly 2-3 correction/resubmittal rounds [EST] versus one or two
for a remodel, since the department reviews the whole structure, not a tie-in. Set
client timeline expectations early.

**Demolition of an existing structure.** A separate demolition permit, with its own
sequence: an AQMD Rule 1403 asbestos survey before any disturbance regardless of
building age (see the hazmat-gate playbook), utility disconnect by the utility/a
licensed sub (not just switched off), tree-protection for protected native species
(verify with local urban forestry), and erosion/sediment control once the lot is bare.
Full playbook: `knowledge/playbooks/54-hazmat-gate.md`.

**Grading & excavation.** A grading plan specifies cut (soil removed) and fill (soil
added/compacted) to reach design pad elevation; compaction testing confirms fill
density before foundation work — a real inspection point. Sites disturbing 1+ acre
trigger the federal NPDES Construction General Permit and a SWPPP (silt fencing, fiber
rolls, inlet protection); most SFR lots stay under that, but many jurisdictions still
require a local grading/erosion plan — verify, don't assume it's exempt.

**Underground utilities — new stub-outs.** Distinct from a lateral *replacement*:
fresh water/gas/electrical/sewer connections run from the street main to the house.
Call DigAlert first. The sewer lateral gets a new stub-out and cleanout, sized/sloped
correctly from day one (compare `knowledge/playbooks/43-sewer-lateral-replacement.md`).
Electrical/gas need utility coordination (DWP/SCE, SoCalGas) and inspection before
backfill — never bury before inspection.

**Foundation types.** *Slab-on-grade* — monolithic slab over compacted fill/vapor
barrier; cheapest, fastest, but plumbing is embedded (a future repipe means breaking
concrete). *Raised* — perimeter footing, stem wall, mudsill, joists over a crawl
space; costs more, gives future plumbing/electrical access (Day 4). *Hillside
caisson/pier-and-grade-beam* — where the soils report finds weak near-surface bearing
or the lot is sloped, deep drilled caissons reach competent soil/bedrock, tied together
above grade by a grade beam carrying the structure's loads — fully engineered, no
prescriptive option. Type is set by the soils report and slope, not preference. Rough
cost: slab, $15-$25/SF [EST]; raised, $20-$35/SF [EST]; hillside caisson/grade-beam,
$40-$80+/SF [EST].

**Path to Certificate of Occupancy.** Unlike a remodel's single final inspection, a new
SFR needs final structural, final MEP, final building, and often final zoning sign-off
before the department issues a Certificate of Occupancy (CofO) — the legal document
allowing occupancy. No CofO, no legal move-in, and it's often a sale-closing condition.
Build it into the schedule as its own milestone.

**Residential fire sprinklers (CRC R313).** California has required automatic fire
sprinkler systems in all new one- and two-family dwellings and townhouses since 2011 —
every new-construction SFR in this day's scope needs one, it's not just a multifamily/
commercial requirement. Systems are almost always designed to **NFPA 13D**, the lighter
residential standard sized for occupant escape time (not full property protection like
commercial NFPA 13). Rough cost: $1.50-$4/SF of new construction [EST], run off a
dedicated supply line sized by the sprinkler contractor's own hydraulic calc. This is
licensed scope for a dedicated **C-16** (fire protection) sub, not something the general
framing or plumbing crew self-performs, and it needs its own inspection tied into the
rough sequence before drywall. Additions/remodels generally do **not** trigger a
whole-house retrofit unless the local AHJ (city/county fire marshal or building
department) determines the scope is substantial enough to require it — verify locally
before assuming a remodel is exempt.

## Day 29 — Two-Story & Multi-Level Structural

**Why it matters:** A second story isn't "framing but taller" — every load has to
travel through one more floor and one more seismic connection than a single-story house
needs, and a stair built to the wrong numbers is both a code failure and a real injury
risk. This is what separates a company that can competently run a two-story job from
one that only does single-story remodels.

### Learn
**Second-floor framing & floor systems.** A second-story wall or post must land on a
header/beam/post that runs unbroken down to the foundation — the Day 1 load path, one
story higher. A second-floor bearing wall that doesn't stack over a first-floor bearing
wall or sized beam is the most common two-story framing defect, and exactly what the
second-story-addition sequence below checks for first. Engineered I-joists (TJI — OSB
web, laminated-veneer or solid-sawn flanges) span farther than dimensional lumber at
less weight and resist crowning/shrinkage. Every hole cut through a TJI web for MEP
must follow the manufacturer's punch-out chart or get engineering approval — cutting
freehand is Day 5's "don't cut a truss chord" lesson applied to floors, and it can
silently weaken the system for years. Dimensional-lumber floors are still common on
shorter spans, sized by the same span tables as Day 5.

**Stair code — checked with a tape measure at inspection.** Under CRC R311.7 (verify
the current code-cycle's exact subsection numbers): max riser height 7-3/4"; min tread
depth 10"; min clear width 36"; min headroom 80" (6'8"), measured from the nosing.
Guards are required wherever a walking surface sits more than 30" above grade, with
openings limited so a 4-inch sphere can't pass through. Handrails are required at 4+
risers, mounted 34"-38" above the nosing, graspable profile (roughly 1-1/4" to 2"),
continuous, returning to a wall or newel post at each end. Get any of these wrong and
framing inspection fails on the spot.

**Two-story seismic load path — distinct from Day 4's cripple-wall retrofit.** A
single-story house only keeps its walls bolted to the foundation; a two-story house
also has to transfer lateral force between floors without the connection tearing loose.
The floor acts as a diaphragm — a rigid plane that collects seismic force and
delivers it to the shear walls below — and that force must be positively connected at
the diaphragm-to-shear-wall boundary. Where a shear wall is tall and narrow, force can
try to overturn it; **hold-downs** (steel connectors bolted through end studs, anchored
to the foundation/framing below) resist that. This load path — roof diaphragm to
upper shear wall to floor diaphragm to lower shear wall to foundation — must be
continuous and engineered end to end; verify the hold-down schedule with the project
engineer rather than assuming a standard answer.

**Second-story additions — the sequence, distilled.** Full architect/engineer plan
set, including whether the existing foundation and first floor can carry the new load
— both commonly need upgrading first. Permit with a longer plan-check given the
structural complexity. Shore the first floor per engineering before demo. Reinforce the
existing foundation/framing. Remove/modify the roof to build the new platform, frame
walls and new roof, dry-in fast — the house is far less livable with the roof open,
flag this to the client. Rough MEP for the new floor often routes a brand-new stack up
through the house, planned before framing closes it off. Rough inspections, insulate to
Title 24, exterior finish, windows, interior finish, final inspections. Rough cost:
$450-$750+/SF [EST] for the new floor area; a modest 600-800 SF second story commonly
runs $350,000-$600,000+ [EST]. Full playbook:
`knowledge/playbooks/52-second-story-addition.md`.

**Balconies & decks — the compliance layer behind Day 21.** Elevated balconies/decks
on 3+ unit rentals fall under **SB721**; condo/HOA developments fall under **SB326**
— single-family homes aren't covered, though the dry-rot risk is real regardless. 
SB721 allows a licensed architect, engineer, or qualified contractor to inspect; SB326
requires a licensed structural engineer or architect — a contractor cannot sign a
valid SB326 inspection. **AB 2579** (Sept. 2024) extended only **SB721**'s
initial-inspection deadline to **January 1, 2026**; **SB326**'s initial deadline was
**January 1, 2025** and was never extended — it has already passed, so a qualifying
condo/HOA property without a completed inspection is currently out of compliance.
Re-inspection then runs every 6 years (SB721) or 9 years (SB326) after each statute's
own initial inspection. The report goes
to the owner or HOA board, not routinely the city — code enforcement is notified only
on an immediate hazard. Open any deck job on a qualifying building with "has this had
its SB721/SB326 inspection." Full playbooks:
`knowledge/playbooks/33-balcony-deck-waterproofing.md` and
`knowledge/playbooks/55-balcony-inspection-law.md`.

## Day 30 — SoCal Hazard & Compliance Overlays

**Why it matters:** SoCal construction sits under a stack of overlays a building permit
doesn't tell you about — fire, green-building, hillside, historic, and coastal rules
that can add real cost and months of schedule to an otherwise straightforward job. A
permit is not proof of zoning or hazard-overlay compliance, and missing one is how a
job stalls at plan-check or gets built non-compliant.

### Learn
**Wildfire / Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI) hardening.** Any parcel in or near a Very
High Fire Hazard Severity Zone (VHFHSZ) — CAL FIRE and local jurisdictions publish
the maps — triggers **California Building Code Chapter 7A**, the ignition-resistant
construction standard for new construction and qualifying additions/re-roofs there.
Chapter 7A governs materials most exposed to ember/radiant-heat attack: Class A
roofing, ember-resistant vents (mesh-screened or baffled, not open louvers),
non-combustible or ignition-resistant siding near grade, multi-pane/tempered glazing,
and enclosed eaves. **Defensible space** is the separate property-wide layer — CAL
FIRE's zones run roughly Zone 1 (0-30 ft: lean/clean/green) and Zone 2 (30-100 ft:
reduced fuel load); verify current distances with CAL FIRE or the local fire
department, since ordinances are set locally on top of the state baseline. **Zone 0**
(0-5 ft, the ember-resistant zone) was created by **AB 3074 (2020)**, directing the
Board of Forestry to adopt rules keeping this perimeter essentially free of combustible
material in VHFHSZ areas — the adopted rule text and compliance date have shifted
more than once, so say "verify current Zone 0 regulation and deadline with CAL FIRE"
rather than quoting one from memory. WUI hardening is increasingly tied to
insurability, not just code — carriers have been non-renewing hillside/foothill
policies over this exact gap, a real, timely sales angle for re-roofs, siding, vents,
and fencing/landscape work in these zones.

**CalGreen (Part 11 of Title 24)** — distinct from the Title 24 *energy* code in Days
11, 12, 18. CalGreen is California's mandatory green-building code, applying to new
construction and many additions alongside (not instead of) Title 24 energy. Three areas
matter most: **construction waste diversion** — a minimum diversion rate (commonly
cited around 65%, though local ordinances can set it higher — verify current
edition/local override) of C&D debris from landfill, documented via a waste-management
plan that can hold up closeout if missing; **low-VOC materials** — limits on volatile
organic compounds in paints, adhesives, sealants, and flooring, meaning a sub can't
grab any bucket off the shelf on a permitted job; **water-efficient fixtures** —
low-flow toilets, showerheads, and faucets to current CalGreen flow-rate limits,
stricter than big-box-store defaults. Checked at plan-check and again at final —
missing waste-diversion receipts at closeout is a common, avoidable hold-up (see
`knowledge/playbooks/50-detached-adu-new-build.md`).

**Hillside, HPOZ, and Coastal overlays.** All three are invisible until you look them
up — the house "looking normal" tells you nothing. *Hillside* — HCR/BHO overlays
add grading limits, height-from-grade rules, retaining-wall restrictions, and often
trigger the soils report from Day 28 even where it wouldn't otherwise apply. *HPOZ
(Historic Preservation Overlay Zone)* — any street-visible exterior change (windows,
doors, siding, roofing, fences) typically needs a **Certificate of Appropriateness**
from the HPOZ board before a permit issues, constraining material choices and adding
review time. *Coastal Zone* — parcels near the coast (Venice, Pacific Palisades,
Playa del Rey, and similar) may need a **Coastal Development Permit (CDP)** from the
California Coastal Commission or a delegated local authority, a separate, often slower
track the building code doesn't trigger on its own. Full playbook:
`knowledge/playbooks/53-zoning-basics-la.md`.

**When each overlay triggers, and what it adds.** VHFHSZ/WUI: triggered by location on
the fire hazard map, not project scope — changes material specs and can affect
underwriting; added cost is mostly a materials-spec premium, not a separate fee.
CalGreen: triggered by project type/scope — added cost is documentation plus modestly
pricier low-VOC materials/fixtures, smaller than a discretionary approval. Hillside:
triggered by slope/overlay designation — adds engineering/geotechnical scope and can
reshape the design via grading limits. HPOZ: triggered by location inside a historic
district — adds a discretionary review cycle (weeks to months) before a permit can be
pulled. Coastal: triggered by proximity to the coastal boundary — the slowest, most
expensive track, sometimes months and real consultant/attorney cost if contested.
Document which overlays apply, with the source, before quoting price or timeline near a
fire-hazard edge, hillside, historic district, or coast.

## Day 31 — Repair Verticals: Termite, Drainage, Chimney, Restoration

**Why it matters:** These four verticals share a pattern that makes them some of the best leads
you'll ever get: they're usually discovered by someone else first (an escrow WDO inspector, a
homeowner's own eyes after a storm, an insurance adjuster) and they close fast because the
homeowner didn't go looking for a remodel — a real problem found them. Less competition than
kitchens/baths, higher trust once you can speak the language correctly, and — for restoration —
a completely different payer (the insurance company, not the homeowner's wallet) that changes
how the whole job is priced and scheduled.

### Learn
**Termite & dry-rot repair.** A WDO (wood-destroying organism) inspection — usually triggered by
a real estate sale — identifies drywood termite, subterranean termite, or fungal dry-rot damage
to structural wood, and it is regulated by the Structural Pest Control Board, a completely
separate licensing track from your LADBS building permit. Treat the pest company and the repair
contractor as two different vendors on two different clocks: if whole-structure fumigation/
tenting is called for, that has to happen and clear *before* your framing crew opens the wall,
not concurrently. Once treatment is done (or if it's localized and doesn't require tenting), pull
a structural permit, remove the damaged members — sill plates, studs, joists, fascia, rafter
tails — sistering where damage is partial and fully replacing where it's extensive per the WDO
report's scope, treat and seal the sound wood at the repair joint, and — critically — find and
fix the moisture source that let rot take hold in the first place (bad drainage, a roof leak,
failed flashing); repairing the wood without fixing the water is a guaranteed repeat call.
Full playbook: `knowledge/playbooks/44-termite-dry-rot-repair.md`. **Rough LA cost:** $1,500-$4,000 for localized
sill/stud repair; $6,000-$15,000+ for extensive framing replacement; fumigation/tenting bills
separately, typically $2,000-$5,000+ depending on structure size.

**Site drainage & grading fixes.** A yard that ponds, or a low spot that never drains, is a
design problem, not a "wait for it to dry" problem — and if it's pooling against the foundation
it's feeding the moisture that causes the dry rot above. A French drain is usually the fix: locate
the low point and water source, call DigAlert before trenching, and pick a legal discharge point
— the street, a dry well, or an approved easement, **never** onto a neighbor's property (this is
the single most common neighbor-dispute trigger in this trade). Trench with a consistent slope
the entire run, line with filter fabric, bed in gravel, lay perforated pipe, wrap in more gravel,
close the fabric over the top (prevents silt clogging years later), cap to finish grade, and
always hose-test before backfilling — skipping the hose test is how a flat spot doesn't show up
until the next storm. Full playbook: `knowledge/playbooks/31-french-drain-site-drainage.md`. **Rough LA cost:**
$25-$50 per linear foot depending on trench depth and discharge complexity.

**Chimney & fireplace repair.** Masonry chimneys are a well-documented soft-point failure in LA
earthquakes, so a seismic strap retrofit is a legitimate standalone sale even on a chimney with no
visible damage — don't treat it as an upsell add-on, treat it as its own lead. Pull a structural
permit (LADBS is typically over-the-counter for a straightforward strap/brace job; full removal
down to or below the roofline can trigger plan check if it touches roof framing). If materials
look old, test for asbestos before any demo. On the repair path: assess mortar/brick/flue
condition, tuckpoint limited cracking or rebuild damaged sections, then strap the chimney to roof
and wall framing. On the removal ("chop and cap") path: disconnect any active appliance venting
first, remove to the roofline, frame/sheathe/flash the new roof section to match, and patch the
interior ceiling/wall where the chimney passed through. Full playbook: `knowledge/playbooks/21-chimney-repair.md`.
**Rough LA cost:** $2,500-$6,000 for masonry repair plus seismic strap; $6,000-$15,000+ for full
removal with roof/ceiling patch.

**Water, fire & mold restoration — this is a distinct vertical from a Day 13 mold job, and it
runs on a different clock and a different payer.** [EST throughout this section — restoration
pricing and process detail below reflects general industry practice, not a Joon-specific source;
verify current figures before quoting.] The industry splits every loss into two phases:
**mitigation** (stop the damage from getting worse — extract standing water, set air movers and
dehumidifiers, moisture-map framing with a meter, remove unsalvageable porous material, and for
fire/smoke, scrub soot and neutralize odor) and **rebuild** (the actual reconstruction —
drywall, flooring, paint, cabinets — which only starts once mitigation is signed off and the
space reads dry/clean). Treat them as two engagements even if your company runs both: mitigation
has to start within hours of the loss — most homeowner policies have a "duty to mitigate" clause
— delay can reduce the claim payout — while rebuild waits on an agreed scope.

**The insurance-claim flow you're actually working inside:** homeowner reports the loss →
insurer assigns an adjuster → the adjuster or your own estimator writes the scope in
**Xactimate** (the industry-standard estimating software both insurers and restoration
contractors use, priced by ZIP/county, so a well-documented Xactimate estimate is far harder to
dispute than a generic bid) → homeowner signs a work authorization → mitigation starts
immediately, often before the adjuster has even inspected in person → a **supplement** gets
filed if demo reveals hidden damage the initial scope missed (very common — expect it) → rebuild
scope gets agreed and proceeds like any other contract, with the insurer's approved scope
functioning as your bid. Know two terms homeowners won't understand: **ACV** (actual cash value —
depreciated payout) vs. **RCV** (replacement cost value — full payout, released once work is
complete) — a homeowner confused by a "recoverable depreciation" holdback is asking a real,
answerable question.

**IICRC certification is the credential that matters in this vertical**, the way EPA Lead-Safe
matters in Day 13 — the Institute of Inspection Cleaning and Restoration Certification issues
WRT (Water Damage Restoration Technician), ASD (Applied Structural Drying), FSRT (Fire and Smoke
Restoration Technician), and AMRT (Applied Microbial Remediation Technician) credentials, and the
industry's own published standards — ANSI/IICRC S500 (water) and S520 (mold) — are what a
structural-drying or clearance-testing dispute gets measured against if a claim is ever contested.
A tech-level WRT course runs roughly $500-$700 and 2-3 days [EST]; a shop doing any real volume of
water/fire work should have at least one IICRC-certified lead on staff before marketing the
service, the same way you wouldn't market lead-safe work without the EPA certification.
**Rough LA cost:** contained-room water mitigation, $3-$8/sq ft [EST] for extraction and
structural drying (equipment rental alone runs roughly $35-$50/day per air mover and $75-$150/day
per dehumidifier [EST]); fire/smoke mitigation on a single contained room, $3,000-$15,000+ [EST];
whole-house smoke/soot remediation, $20,000-$75,000+ [EST] — all separate from the rebuild scope
that follows.

## Day 32 — The Annual Home Care Program (Maintenance as a Product)

**Why it matters:** Every other day in this course sells a project with a start and an end.
This day sells something different — a *relationship* that doesn't end, priced as a recurring
membership instead of a one-time invoice. It's also the cheapest lead-generation channel you'll
ever build: a maintenance visit puts your own tech inside a homeowner's house on a schedule you
control, which is exactly the setup Day 24's upsell training (and `sales-training/12-upsell-the-
client.md`) already teaches you to convert into the next big remodel.

### Learn
**The SoCal seasonal calendar is built around two seasons, not four** — a wet season (roughly
November-March) and a long dry season (April-October) that overlaps with peak fire risk
(roughly May-December depending on rain year). Sequence the calendar around what fails when:

- **Pre-rainy-season (September-October), the single most important visit of the year:** clean
  and inspect gutters and downspouts, walk the roof for slipped tile/cracked shingles/failed
  flashing (Day 6), check the weep screed and stucco for gaps (Day 8), and re-test yard drainage
  and any French drain discharge with a hose (Day 31) — every item on this list is cheap to fix
  in October and expensive to fix in January after a storm has already gotten behind it.
- **HVAC service cadence:** homeowner-level filter changes every 1-3 months depending on filter
  type and pets [EST]; a professional tune-up twice a year — spring before AC season, fall before
  heating season — covering coil cleaning, refrigerant-level check, and blower/duct inspection
  (Day 11) [EST].
- **Water heater flush:** annually, to clear sediment buildup that's worse in SoCal's hard-water
  areas and shortens tank life; tankless units need an annual descaling (vinegar) flush too
  (Day 9) [EST].
- **Caulk & seal maintenance:** inspect exterior caulking at windows, doors, and penetrations
  annually; SoCal sun/UV dries and cracks sealant faster than people expect, so plan to recaulk
  every 2-3 years even with no visible leak yet [EST].
- **Termite inspection cadence:** there's no legal mandate outside a real estate transaction, but
  a voluntary annual or every-other-year visual WDO-style check catches drywood/subterranean
  activity and dry rot (Day 31) long before it's a structural repair — position this as cheap
  insurance, not an upsell.
- **Drainage checks:** re-walk the yard and hose-test any French drain before the first rain of
  the season (Day 31) — the same discharge point that worked fine in a dry year can silt up over
  a summer.
- **Fire-hardening / defensible-space upkeep:** before fire season, clear dead vegetation and
  debris from the roof, gutters, and the zone immediately around the structure, and check ember-
  resistant venting where it's been installed. [EST] — defensible-space and ember-resistant-zone
  rules are actively evolving at the state level; confirm the current CAL FIRE/local requirement
  before quoting this as a compliance item rather than general prevention.
- **Sewer hydro-jet cadence:** for older clay or cast-iron laterals with known root intrusion,
  a preventive hydro-jet every 1-2 years plus a camera check every 2-3 years is cheaper than an
  emergency backup and a full lateral replacement (Day 9, Day 31) [EST].

**Packaging it as a membership, not a to-do list.** The mechanics you already know from HVAC
"club" plans and pest-control quarterly contracts translate directly to a whole-house program —
the shift is charging one annual (or monthly) fee that bundles several trades' cadences into
scheduled visits, instead of selling each visit separately. [EST tiers below — build final
pricing off your actual crew-hour cost, not these numbers directly.]

| Tier | Annual price [EST] | Visits/year | What's included |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Basic** | $349-$499 | 2 | Pre-rainy-season gutter/roof/drainage walk + one HVAC filter/tune-up visit; priority scheduling for repairs |
| **Plus** | $799-$1,200 | 4 | Basic, plus water-heater flush, caulk/seal check, and a termite/dry-rot visual pass; discounted rate (10-15%) on any repair found |
| **Premier** | $1,800-$2,800+ | 6+ | Plus, plus sewer camera/hydro-jet cadence and seasonal fire-hardening debris clearance; first-priority emergency response and an annual comprehensive written condition report |

Homeowners who've owned 15-20+ years and are used to paying a landscaper or pool guy monthly
already understand this pricing model — frame it exactly that way ("this is like your pool
service, but for the house itself") rather than as insurance or a warranty, which invites
different (and harder) objections.

**The upsell path is the real reason to build this product.** A maintenance visit is the
"Subcontractor Hand-off" and "Free Gift Upfront" approaches from `sales-training/12-upsell-the-
client.md` running on autopilot: your tech is inside the attic, crawl space, or under the sink on
a schedule *you* set, and finds the next real job — a cracked flue liner, a water heater at end
of life, a stucco crack that's stepped, not hairline. Report findings back to the office the same
day (the same rule as the sub hand-off: follow up same-day or next-day, not a week later), and
size the follow-on quote using the Multiplier Rule (2-2.5x, minimum) as a separate contract, never
folded into the membership fee itself — the Contract Separatist Rule applies here exactly as it
does to any other referral upsell.

## Day 33 — Running the Job After the Sale

**Why it matters:** Day 25 taught you how to write the contract that starts a job. This day
teaches you how to run it without bleeding money or trust between the signature and the final
walkthrough — the four skills here (change orders, scheduling, punch list, warranty) are where a
profitable job quietly turns unprofitable, or where a satisfied client turns into your next
referral. Both directions run through the same handful of documents and habits.

### Learn
**Change orders: the document, not the verbal "sure, we'll just add that."** Day 2 already named
verbal change orders as one of the two things that most commonly blow up a job's profitability —
this is the fix. Every change order is a **written, separately signed document**, executed and
signed by the client **before** the added or changed work proceeds, that states: the specific
scope being added or changed, the price, and the effect (if any) on the completion date. The
pricing convention follows the same logic as your original bid, not a discount version of it —
this is added-scope work discovered mid-job, with the same overhead, sales, and warranty-reserve
load as anything else you price (Day 23), so price it at your normal margin, not "at cost as a
favor." **Unforeseen-conditions language is what makes a change order defensible instead of a
fight:** you've already seen this pattern twice this course without naming it as a system — the
85% paint-match clause and the "up to two to three pieces of wood" wood-damage contingency
(Day 17, Day 25) are both unforeseen-conditions clauses, written into the *original* contract, that
pre-authorize a change order the moment the stated threshold is crossed. Build every bid this way
going forward: state what's included, state the threshold where a condition becomes "more than
expected," and let that threshold — not an argument on-site — trigger the change order. **The
one rule that can't bend:** work never proceeds on the changed scope until the client has signed
the change order and, where the added cost is material, adjusted the payment schedule to match —
proceeding on a verbal "go ahead" is exactly the exposure Day 2 flagged.

**Scheduling & trade sequencing on a live job.** The kickoff moves fast and follows a fixed
pattern: record a full video/photo walkthrough of the scope the day the job is sold and send it
to the office, the project manager, and the subcontractor so everyone shares one understanding of
the job before anyone shows up — this single habit prevents more disputes than any contract
clause. Bid the subcontractor work by scheduling **2-3 contractors** to walk and bid the same
job, staggered in roughly one-hour windows, with your strongest sub going first; expect one of
the three to no-show, which is exactly why you never schedule just one. Write every task the
winning sub is responsible for in explicit, itemized detail in their own written agreement — the
same unforeseen-conditions discipline above, one level down the chain, because an undocumented
sub scope is where "who pays to replace this piece of wood" disputes start. On day one, introduce
the sub to the client personally as your foreman/company crew, hand over a project manager's
direct phone number, and have the crew mask floors and protect vulnerable surfaces before a
single tool comes out — a clean, professional first day buys you patience for whatever goes wrong
later. Once the job is running, the project lead needs to physically show up on site at least
every 2-3 days — oversight, not micromanagement, but frequent enough that a small problem doesn't
become a client complaint before you've heard about it. **One tactic from the source training —
"spiking the job" (starting a sliver of visible work, like pulling a few kitchen doors "for
measurements," during the legally required 3-business-day cancellation window so the client feels
the job is already underway and is less likely to cancel) — is flagged here as a compliance risk,
not a technique to use.** It directly undermines the consumer-protection purpose of the
cancellation right from Day 25 and is not something to teach as a clean best practice. Full
source: `sales-training/11-open-a-job.md`.

**Punch-list methodology.** A punch list is the final walkthrough — homeowner and PM together —
that lists everything incomplete, incorrect, or below spec before final payment (Day 2). Run it
as a structured walk, not a casual stroll: go room by room in one consistent order, and write
down every item as you find it rather than trying to remember them at the end. Sort what you find
into three buckets, because they're handled differently: **incomplete** (a fixture not yet
installed, a final coat not yet applied — expected, just finish it), **defective/incorrect** (the
wrong tile, a crooked cabinet door, paint that didn't hit the 85% match threshold — your cost to
fix), and **client-requested change** (something the homeowner wants different from what was
contracted — that's a change order, not a punch-list item, and gets priced as one). Give the punch
list a real close-out date, not "we'll get to it" — an open-ended punch list is exactly what
strands a retention holdback (a percentage, commonly 5-10% [EST], withheld from final payment
until the punch list clears) and turns a happy client into an impatient one during the exact week
you want a five-star review and a referral.

**Warranty policy & callback handling.** A warranty is a *policy*, not just the sales-floor talking
point about "years in business to back it up" (Day 14) — define it in writing before you need it:
coverage by trade (workmanship warranty duration is a business decision, commonly 1-2 years on
workmanship with manufacturer warranties on materials/equipment running longer and separately
[EST]), a stated callback response window (e.g., respond within 24-48 hours, resolve or schedule
resolution within a set number of days [EST]), and a scheduled first-year walkthrough — a
proactive check-in that catches minor settling/shrinkage issues (nail pops, small drywall cracks,
a door that needs a small adjustment) before they become a complaint call, and doubles as a
built-in reason to be back in the house with a referral or Day 32 maintenance-membership pitch
ready to go.

**The close-out packet — what actually gets handed to the client at the end.** Three documents,
every time: an **unconditional lien release**, using California's statutory release forms (Civil
Code §§8132-8138, already flagged in Day 2) so subcontractors and suppliers can't later lien a
paid-off client's property; a **warranty letter** stating the coverage and duration in writing,
not just spoken; and the **review ask** — timed to the moment the client is happiest (final
walkthrough passed, punch list clear), using the same neighborhood-credibility and relationship
mindset from Day 24 and `sales-training/12-upsell-the-client.md` ("the client is essentially
married to you during construction") — a five-star review and a referral name are the actual last
two line items on every job, not an afterthought after the invoice is paid.

**Day 29**
1. The load path from roof to foundation must be continuous; if the upper wall doesn't line up with support below, load lands on framing never designed to carry it — showing up later as sag or failure.

3. A hold-down is a steel connector resisting a shear wall overturning under lateral force; two-story buildings need it because seismic force must transfer between two stacked diaphragms/shear walls, a path a single-story retrofit doesn't have.
4. The existing foundation and first-floor framing must be evaluated and, if inadequate, reinforced to carry the new load.
5. SB721 allows an architect, engineer, or qualified contractor; SB326 requires a licensed structural engineer or architect — a contractor cannot sign a valid SB326 inspection.
6. SB721 (apartments): January 1, 2026, per AB 2579's extension. SB326 (condo/HOA): January 1, 2025, and it was never extended — that deadline has already passed, so a non-compliant HOA is already in violation, not "on track." Calling them shared is wrong and could mislead an HOA into thinking it has more time than it does. 6. Zoning/hazard overlays are governed by separate codes and maps; the building department won't permit a zoning-violating design, but the permit itself doesn't verify overlay compliance — that has to be checked independently.

**Day 31**
1. Whole-structure treatment is a separate licensed process (Structural Pest Control Board) from your building permit work, and it needs to fully clear before framing crews reopen the treated area — doing them concurrently risks re-contaminating repaired wood or working in an unsafe treated space.
2. Directing discharge onto a neighboring property — design the trench to route to the street, a dry well, or an approved easement instead.
3. Because unbraced masonry chimneys are a well-documented, real earthquake hazard on their own, independent of any other visible damage — it's a legitimate lead, not just an add-on to a bigger job.
4. Mitigation is the emergency stabilization (extraction, drying, moisture-mapping) that must start within hours per most policies' "duty to mitigate" clause; rebuild is the reconstruction that only starts once the space is confirmed dry/clean. They're billed and scheduled as effectively two separate engagements even under one contractor.
5. It produces a ZIP/county-indexed, line-item estimate in the same format the insurer's own adjuster uses, making the scope far harder to dispute or lowball than a generic bid.

**Day 32**
1. Every item on it (gutters, roof, drainage, stucco/weep screed) is cheap to fix before the first storm and expensive to fix after water has already gotten behind the envelope.
2. HVAC service, pest/termite inspection, and sewer line maintenance are the clearest analogs (water heater flush and caulk/seal checks also qualify).
3. The Contract Separatist Rule — bundling a real repair into the membership fee undersells the work and creates the same "I thought this was included" confusion a bundled upsell contract creates elsewhere.
4. The Multiplier Rule — target at least 2x-2.5x the size of the original sale, never a smaller follow-on.
5. A homeowner who already pays a landscaper or pool service monthly recognizes and accepts that pricing pattern instantly; "insurance" or "warranty" framing invites a different, harder set of objections about what's actually covered.

**Day 33**
1. The scope/price/schedule-impact must be written down as its own document, and the client must sign it, before the changed or added work proceeds.
2. A clause in the original contract that pre-authorizes a change order once a stated threshold is crossed — e.g., the 85% paint-match clause (Day 17) and the "up to two to three pieces of wood" wood-damage contingency (Day 17/25).
3. Because one of the three will almost always no-show, and it also lets you walk away immediately from a bid that comes in over budget without losing schedule time waiting on a second bid.
4. Missing cabinet pull = incomplete; tile color change = client-requested change (change order); crooked outlet cover = defective/incorrect (contractor's cost to fix).
5. An unconditional lien release (California statutory form, Civil Code §§8132-8138), a written warranty letter, and the review ask.
