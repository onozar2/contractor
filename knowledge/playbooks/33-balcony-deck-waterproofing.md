# Playbook: Balcony / Deck Waterproofing (incl. SB326/SB721)

*When you need this:* an elevated balcony, deck, walkway, or stairway with a waterproof walking surface is showing cracked coating, soft/spongy decking, staining on the framing below, or is due for its mandatory inspection under California's exterior elevated element (EEE) laws.

## Sequence

1. Determine trigger: SB721 covers elevated exterior elements (EEE) on 3+ unit apartment/multifamily rental buildings; SB326 covers condo/HOA common-interest developments. Single-family homes aren't covered but should still be inspected proactively — deck-membrane dry rot is one of the most common hidden-damage findings on older LA homes. **Both statutes' initial inspection deadline is January 1, 2026** — this is a hard date, not a "confirm current interval" hedge, and re-inspection cycles run every 6 years (SB721) / every 9 years (SB326) after that.
2. Inspector eligibility differs by statute — do not conflate them: **SB721 (apartments)** allows a broader inspector list including a licensed architect, licensed engineer, or a qualified/certified contractor. **SB326 (condo/HOA)** is stricter — inspection MUST be performed by a licensed structural engineer or architect; a contractor cannot self-inspect or sign off a SB326 report even if licensed. The inspector performs a visual and, where accessible, probe inspection of the membrane, framing, and connections. **The report goes to the building owner (SB721) or the HOA board, where it feeds the reserve study (SB326) — it is NOT routinely "filed with the local building authority."** Local code enforcement is notified only when the inspection finds an immediate-hazard condition (SB721: written notice to the enforcement agency within 15 days) or if the agency requests the report; verify current notification specifics with the local building department.
3. If deficiencies are found, an engineer/architect prepares a repair plan (required whenever framing, not just the surface membrane, is compromised).
4. Permit: required for waterproofing membrane replacement tied to structural repair (LADBS in LA city; county Building & Safety in unincorporated areas; independent cities like Santa Monica/Pasadena/Burbank have their own process). Surface-only membrane recoat on a sound deck may not require a permit — verify with LADBS.
5. Prep: remove railing/fixtures as needed, test for asbestos/lead before disturbing suspect coatings or materials (pre-1990/pre-1978 raises the odds but is not the legal trigger — an AQMD Rule 1403 survey is required before regulated demo/reno work regardless of building age), protect areas below.
6. Demo: strip failed waterproof coating/membrane down to the structural deck sheathing; remove and inspect all framing, ledger board connection to the house, and any embedded posts.
7. Repair or sister any dry-rotted or termite-damaged framing (see Sistering Floor Joists playbook for method); re-flash the ledger-to-house connection — this is the single most common origin point of failure.
8. Framing inspection before covering.
9. Install new waterproof deck membrane system (fluid-applied or sheet membrane) per manufacturer spec, with proper slope to drain and integrated flashing at all penetrations, walls, and the ledger.
10. Final inspection.
11. Reinstall railings, guardrails (verify code-height compliance), and finish surface (pavers on pedestals, tile, or textured coating).

## Commonly missed

- Treating this as a simple recoat when the underlying framing is already wet and compromised — always probe, don't just look at the surface.
- Skipping the ledger-to-house flashing detail, which is the most common leak and dry-rot origin point.
- Missing the January 1, 2026 initial-inspection deadline for qualifying SB721 (apartments) or SB326 (condo/HOA) properties, or using a contractor to "inspect" a SB326 condo property when the statute requires a licensed structural engineer or architect.
- Assuming the inspection report goes to the city — it goes to the owner (SB721) or HOA board (SB326); the enforcement agency is only looped in on immediate-hazard findings or on request.
- Not verifying guardrail height/spacing meets current code when reinstalling railings.

## Rough SoCal cost (2025-26)

$35-$75/sf for full membrane tear-off and replacement with framing repair; add $2,000-$8,000+ if significant dry rot/sistering is required; SB326/SB721 inspection reports typically run $500-$1,500 per building depending on size and accessibility.
