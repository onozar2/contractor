# Playbook: Zoning Gate — What to Check Before Quoting

*When you need this:* ANY job that adds height, footprint, or a structure near a property line — fences/walls, patio covers, ADUs, additions, second stories, driveway/hardscape changes — before you promise a height, setback, or timeline. Zoning is separate from and can be more restrictive than the building code; a building permit does not mean a design is zoning-legal, and LADBS will not issue a permit for a design that violates zoning.

## Sequence

1. **Identify the jurisdiction first.** LA city, LA County (unincorporated), and independent cities (Santa Monica, Pasadena, Burbank, Beverly Hills, Culver City, Long Beach, etc.) each run their own zoning code. Nothing below is universal — it's the LA city baseline. Confirm the parcel's actual jurisdiction before applying any of it.
2. **Pull the parcel in ZIMAS** (zimas.lacity.org, City of LA only) before quoting. ZIMAS gives zone designation, lot dimensions, front-yard setback line, height district, and overlay flags (hillside, HPOZ, coastal, specific plan) in one lookup. Independent cities don't appear in ZIMAS — check that city's own planning/GIS portal instead.
3. **Front-yard height limits (fences/walls):** solid walls and fences in the *required front yard* are capped at roughly 3.5 ft (42 in) in standard LA residential zones. Going higher (up to 6-8 ft) requires either the parcel sitting in a designated **Fence Height District** (and even then, typically only the portion above 3.5 ft may be open/view-through, e.g., wrought iron — not solid block) or a discretionary **Zoning Administrator adjustment** (LAMC 12.24 X.7), which means a public hearing, notice period, and no guaranteed outcome or timeline.
4. **Side and rear yard heights:** generally allowed up to 6 ft by right in standard zones — still verify against the specific zone, corner-lot sight-triangle rules, and any overlay.
5. **Setbacks (additions, ADUs, patio covers, any new structure):** every zone has minimum front/side/rear setback distances; a structure or addition inside the setback needs either redesign or a variance/adjustment. Don't assume the existing structure's footprint defines what's legal to match — additions often can't extend the nonconforming setback further.
6. **Hillside lots:** check for Hillside Area / Hillside Construction Regulation (HCR) or Baseline Hillside Ordinance (BHO) overlay — these add grading limits, height-from-grade rules (not just height-from-roof), retaining wall restrictions, and often geotechnical/soils report requirements beyond flat-lot norms.
7. **HPOZ (Historic Preservation Overlay Zone):** if the parcel is in an HPOZ, exterior changes visible from the street (windows, doors, siding, roofing material, fences) typically need a Certificate of Appropriateness from the HPOZ board *before* a building permit will be issued — this can add weeks to months and constrains material/design choices the homeowner may not expect.
8. **Coastal Zone:** parcels near the coast (Venice, Pacific Palisades, Playa del Rey, and similar) may require a Coastal Development Permit (CDP) from the California Coastal Commission or a delegated local authority, on top of the standard building permit — a separate, often slower approval track.
9. **Document what you found before quoting a price or timeline** — cite the zone, setback numbers, and any overlay flags in the estimate so the homeowner understands what's driving cost/schedule if a discretionary approval is needed.

## Commonly missed

- Treating "permit required over X ft" (a building-code question) as the only height question and skipping the separate zoning height-in-setback question — this is the single most common and most expensive miss (see Block Wall playbook).
- Assuming a fence/wall design that's fine in the side or rear yard is also fine in the front yard — the front-yard limit is much lower.
- Assuming ZIMAS covers independent cities — it doesn't; Pasadena, Long Beach, Santa Monica, Burbank, etc. run their own zoning systems.
- Missing an HPOZ or hillside overlay because the house "looks normal" — overlays are parcel-specific, not visually obvious, and only show up in a zoning lookup.
- Quoting a discretionary approval (ZA adjustment, HPOZ Certificate of Appropriateness, CDP) on the same timeline as an over-the-counter permit — these involve hearings/review cycles measured in months, not days.

## Rough SoCal cost (2025-26)

Zoning research itself is typically a no-cost or low-cost step (ZIMAS lookup is free; an independent city's planning counter may charge a small research fee). Discretionary approvals add real cost: a Zoning Administrator adjustment/variance commonly runs $3,000-$10,000+ in application fees plus consultant/attorney time; an HPOZ Certificate of Appropriateness review is typically lower-cost but adds schedule; a Coastal Development Permit can run into the tens of thousands and many months if contested. Always say "verify current fee schedule with the authority" rather than quoting a specific dollar figure from memory.
