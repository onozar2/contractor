# Playbook: Block Wall

*When you need this:* homeowner wants a CMU (concrete masonry unit) wall for property-line enclosure, privacy, or a low garden wall that is not retaining significant soil.

## Zoning Gate — check BEFORE quoting height

A building permit only means the structure is safe; it does not mean it's legal to build at that height in that location. Zoning height limits are separate from — and override — the building-code permit threshold, and they are the #1 reason a priced block-wall job turns into a tear-down.

- **Front yard (City of LA):** solid walls/fences in the *required front yard* are capped at roughly 3.5 ft (42 in). A 6-ft solid CMU wall in a front yard is NOT permittable by right in a standard LA residential zone.
- **To go higher in the front yard:** the lot needs to sit in a **Fence Height District** (and even then, only the portion above 3.5 ft may be open-work wrought iron/view-through — solid block above 3.5 ft still isn't allowed), or the owner pursues a **discretionary Zoning Administrator adjustment** (LAMC 12.24 X.7), which requires a public hearing and is not fast or guaranteed.
- **Side and rear yards:** generally allowed up to 6 ft by right (verify against the specific zone and any hillside/corner-lot sight-triangle rules).
- **Always verify before quoting:** pull the parcel in **ZIMAS** (LA city) to confirm zone, front-yard setback line, and any Fence Height District or overlay (hillside, HPOZ, coastal) before promising a height or a price. Independent cities (Pasadena, Long Beach, Santa Monica, Burbank, etc.) have their own zoning code and ZIMAS does not cover them — check that city's planning department instead.
- If the wall the homeowner wants is anywhere in the front-yard setback, the first question is height-vs-setback-line, not engineering or permit category.

## Sequence

1. **Engineer plan:** required for taller walls, walls in high-seismic/hillside zones, or any wall retaining soil (if it's retaining soil, treat it as a retaining wall — see that playbook instead, since load requirements differ).
2. **Permit:** required once wall height exceeds the local threshold — verify the exact number with LADBS (commonly cited as a low threshold in the 3-4 ft range in many CA jurisdictions, but confirm current requirement before quoting).
3. **Utility locate:** call DigAlert before excavating the footing trench.
4. Excavate the footing trench per plan dimensions.
5. Form the footing and place rebar per the stamped plan, with dowels positioned for the block cores above.
6. **Footing/rebar inspection:** mandatory before pouring — this is the single most commonly missed inspection on block wall jobs; contractors get flagged for pouring before city sign-off.
7. Pour the footing and allow proper cure time before loading with block.
8. Lay CMU block courses, aligning rebar dowels through the cores and maintaining running bond and mortar joints per plan.
9. Grout reinforced cells per plan (grout inspection required before enclosing cells, if called for by the permit).
10. Cap the block per spec (solid cap block or poured cap).
11. Install control joints at intervals per plan to manage shrinkage cracking.
12. **Final inspection.**
13. Apply stucco/finish coat or paint if specified in the scope.

## Commonly missed

- Pouring the footing or grouting cells before the required city inspection — this is the top violation on block wall permits.
- Treating a soil-retaining wall as a simple block wall instead of engineering it as a retaining wall.
- Skipping seismic dowel/rebar continuity between footing and block cores.
- Not spacing control joints correctly, leading to visible shrinkage cracks.
- Quoting a 6-ft solid wall in a front yard without checking zoning first — this is a legal/zoning failure, not a building-code one, and LADBS will not issue a permit that violates the height limit regardless of engineering.

## Rough SoCal cost (2025-26)

$75-$150 per linear foot for a standard 6-ft CMU wall (block + footing + rebar/grout + stucco finish), depending on height, engineering requirements, access, and finish. Do not quote off a $45/lf floor — that undercuts 2025-26 SoCal labor/material/permit costs and is a money-loser once engineering, permit, and stucco are included; lower per-lf pricing only applies to short garden walls under ~3 ft with no engineering.
