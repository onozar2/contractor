# Playbook: HVAC Changeout / Heat-Pump Conversion

*When you need this:* existing furnace/AC or straight AC system is end-of-life, inefficient, or the homeowner wants to convert from gas to an electric heat pump.

## Sequence

1. Load calculation (Manual J or equivalent) to size new equipment correctly for the home — don't just match the old system's tonnage, especially on a heat-pump conversion where duty cycle differs from gas.
2. Permit application: mechanical permit plus electrical permit (heat pump needs a dedicated high-voltage circuit); typically OTC at LADBS.
3. Verify existing panel has capacity for the new heat-pump circuit; if not, flag a panel upgrade as a prerequisite before quoting final price.
4. Electrical rough-in for new equipment.
5. Disconnect and remove old condenser, furnace/air handler, and any obsolete refrigerant lines.
6. Set new condenser/heat pump unit and air handler in place.
7. Connect refrigerant lines, leak-test, and evacuate per manufacturer spec.
8. Connect to existing ductwork — inspect condition and repair/reseal as needed before hooking up new equipment.
9. Complete electrical connections and controls wiring (thermostat, low-voltage).
10. Commission the system: verify refrigerant charge, airflow, and temperature split against manufacturer spec.
11. Schedule the HERS rater for required Title 24 field verification/diagnostic testing (refrigerant charge verification, airflow, duct leakage) — this is required for most HVAC changeouts and is the single most commonly missed scheduling step, and it directly holds up final inspection if skipped.
12. Schedule and pass mechanical/electrical inspection.
13. Obtain final HERS certificate.
14. Walk the homeowner through the new thermostat and controls.

## Commonly missed

- Not scheduling the HERS rater until after the equipment is already installed and the crew has moved on — this delays final by weeks and requires a callback visit.
- Sizing new equipment to match old tonnage instead of running a fresh load calculation.
- Not confirming panel capacity for a heat-pump's electrical draw before committing to a price.
- Skipping duct inspection/resealing, so the new efficient equipment is pushing air through leaky old ducts.

## Rough SoCal cost (2025-26)

$9,000–$18,000 for a standard single-family heat-pump changeout (equipment + install + HERS testing), scaling with tonnage, ductwork condition, and electrical upgrade needs.
