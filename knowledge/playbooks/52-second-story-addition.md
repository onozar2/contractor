# Playbook: Second-Story Addition

*When you need this:* A homeowner wants to add a second floor over some or all of an existing single-story house rather than build out — the most structurally demanding job type in the playbook set.

## Sequence

1. **Full architect/engineer plan set**, including a structural evaluation of the existing foundation and first-floor framing to confirm they can carry the new second-floor load — and a reinforcement plan for whatever falls short. It's common for the existing foundation and first-floor framing to need upgrading before any second-story framing can begin.
2. **Permit.** Plan-check application with the local building authority (LADBS for City of LA, LA County Building & Safety for unincorporated areas, or the applicable independent city). Expect a longer plan-check timeline than most residential work given the structural complexity — set homeowner expectations early.
3. **Shore/temporarily support** the existing first-floor structure as needed, per the engineer's sequencing, before any reinforcement or demo work begins.
4. **Reinforce the existing foundation and first-floor framing** per the engineer's plan before new second-story framing begins.
5. Remove or modify the existing roof to build the new second-floor platform.
6. Frame the second-story walls and new roof structure.
7. **Roof/wall dry-in** — get the structure weathertight as fast as possible once framed.
8. Rough plumbing, electrical, and mechanical for the new floor, which often includes running a new stack or chase from the ground floor up through the existing house.
9. **Rough MEP inspections.**
10. Insulate to Title 24 requirement.
11. **Insulation/energy inspection.**
12. Exterior finish — matching the existing material or intentionally differentiating per the design.
13. Windows.
14. Interior finishes and paint.
15. **Final structural, MEP, and building inspections.**
16. Final walkthrough.

## Commonly missed

- Assuming the existing foundation/first floor can carry the new load without engineered reinforcement — this is the job's single biggest risk if skipped.
- Underestimating how long the house is exposed with the roof open — sequence framing and dry-in carefully, and use temporary roofing/tarping to protect the first floor through the transition. Flag to the homeowner up front that the house will be significantly less livable during this window.
- Not planning the new floor's plumbing stack/chase routing before framing closes it off.
- Underbidding plan-check timeline, since structural complexity typically pushes this well past a standard residential review.

## Rough SoCal cost (2025-26)

$450-$750+/sf for the new second-floor area, a real premium over ground-floor additions once foundation/first-floor reinforcement, shoring, and roof rework are factored in — a modest 600-800 sf second story commonly runs $350,000-$600,000+ in the LA market.
