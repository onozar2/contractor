# Playbook: Skylight Install

*When you need this:* a homeowner wants natural light added to a hallway, bathroom, or kitchen with no exterior wall access, requiring a new roof and ceiling opening.

## Sequence

1. Pull a permit — this covers both the roofing tie-in and, if a new opening is being framed between or through rafters, the structural aspect; for larger units spanning a rafter, get engineer input on header framing before cutting. Verify OTC eligibility vs. plan-check with LADBS (City of LA) or the applicable local building department.
2. Confirm the skylight unit's glazing meets current Title 24 skylight energy performance requirements before ordering — verify exact U-factor/SHGC with LADBS or the product's compliance documentation.
3. Lay out the roof opening location from inside the attic/rafter space first, confirming clearance from any framing members, then transfer layout to the roof.
4. Cut the roof opening — if it spans a rafter, frame new headers to carry the load per the approved plan; if it fits between rafters, no header framing is needed.
5. If the ceiling below is not a flat cathedral ceiling (i.e., there's an attic gap between roof and ceiling), frame the ceiling opening for a light well/shaft, sized and angled to route light from roof to ceiling.
6. Build and insulate the light well shaft — proper insulation and air-sealing here prevents condensation and energy loss, since this shaft cuts straight through the attic's thermal boundary.
7. Set the skylight unit — curb-mounted (built curb, unit sits on top) or deck-mounted (unit integrates directly to deck) — per manufacturer instructions.
8. Integrate roofing material and flashing around the unit using the manufacturer's flashing kit — step flashing under shingle courses for shingle roofs, or proper tile flashing/pan details for tile roofs. This is the single highest-risk leak point in the whole job.
9. Finish the interior drywall of the light well shaft, taping and mudding corners.
10. Paint and trim the interior shaft and ceiling opening.
11. Schedule final roofing and building inspection.

## Commonly missed

- Cutting the roof opening across a rafter without engineer-approved header framing.
- Under-insulating or failing to air-seal the light well shaft, causing condensation drips inside the shaft in winter.
- Using generic flashing instead of the manufacturer's matched flashing kit — mismatched flashing is the top cause of skylight leaks.
- Not accounting for tile-specific flashing details on tile roofs, where standard shingle flashing kits don't seal properly.

## Rough SoCal cost (2025-26)

$2,000–$4,500 per unit installed for a standard fixed skylight with light well; $4,500–$8,000+ for larger or solar/venting units with complex framing.
