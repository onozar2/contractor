# Playbook: Sistering Floor Joists & Subfloor Repair

*When you need this:* Floor joists or subfloor are damaged by dry rot, termites, or long-term water intrusion — often discovered mid-demo during a bathroom or kitchen remodel, not as a standalone job.

## Sequence

1. **Assess the damage.** Determine extent — localized rot at a joist end near a leaking tub/shower vs. widespread damage across a bay — and whether affected joists can be sistered (reinforced alongside with new lumber) or need full replacement because too little sound wood remains to fasten into. If this surfaces mid-remodel, flag it to the homeowner as a change order immediately; don't proceed on verbal assumption.
2. **Engineer sizing** if spans are long, damage is extensive, or the original joists were already undersized for current use — get sizing/spacing confirmed rather than guessing at lumber dimension.
3. **Permit.** Typically OTC for straightforward joist sistering with no layout change; plan-check if the repair involves significant structural modification (removing a bearing point, altering span/support conditions). Verify OTC eligibility with LADBS or the applicable local building authority.
4. **Shore/support the floor from below** if extensive sections are compromised — from a crawlspace or basement, before removing any more material than necessary to inspect.
5. Remove damaged subfloor sheathing to expose the joists.
6. **Sister** new lumber alongside each damaged joist, fastened per the code nailing/bolting schedule (through-bolts or structural screws for full-depth sistering, not just toe-nails), or **fully replace** any joist too rotted to take fasteners.
7. Install new subfloor sheathing per code thickness and fastening schedule, staggered joints, glued and screwed/nailed to the joists.
8. **Framing inspection** before covering with finish flooring.
9. Remove temporary shoring once the new structure is confirmed adequate.
10. Install finish flooring per the original scope.
11. Patch any ceiling below that was opened to access the joists from underneath.

## Commonly missed

- Treating a mid-remodel discovery as "just fix it" instead of running it through the change-order process with the homeowner.
- Sistering into wood that's too rotted to actually hold fasteners — should have been a full replace.
- Skipping the framing inspection because the subfloor "looks fine" once it's back down.
- Not shoring before removing subfloor over a compromised bay, risking a foot going through.

## Rough SoCal cost (2025-26)

$800-$1,800 per joist for straightforward sistering with easy access; $3,000-$8,000+ for a full bay of joist replacement plus new subfloor, more if discovered as a surprise mid-remodel with tile/plumbing rework layered on top.
