# Playbook: French Drain & Site Drainage

*When you need this:* yard holds standing water, water pools against the foundation, or a low spot never drains after rain — needs a designed drainage path instead of relying on grade alone.

## Sequence

1. **Permit:** typically not required for a standard yard French drain. Permit may apply if tying into the street storm drain system or substantially altering grading near a structure or property line — check with the local public works department before starting if either applies.
2. Assess existing grading and identify the water source and low points driving the ponding.
3. **Utility locate:** call DigAlert before trenching.
4. Design the discharge point: to the street, a dry well, or an approved drainage easement — never directly onto a neighbor's property. California/LA code generally requires drainage to be directed away from foundations and not discharged onto adjoining land; this is the core design driver and the most common neighbor-dispute trigger on this job.
5. Excavate the trench with a consistent slope toward the discharge point, maintaining minimum slope for positive flow along the entire run.
6. Line the trench with filter fabric before adding aggregate.
7. Add a gravel bed in the trench bottom.
8. Install perforated pipe, orienting perforations per manufacturer spec (commonly down or per local practice for the pipe type).
9. Wrap the pipe in additional gravel, then close the fabric over the top to prevent silt intrusion and clogging over time.
10. Cap the trench with topsoil, sod, or decorative rock per finish spec.
11. Route the discharge cleanly to the street, a dry well, or the approved easement.
12. Test with a hose to confirm flow before fully backfilling and finishing the surface.

## Commonly missed

- Discharging water directly onto a neighboring property — the most common source of neighbor complaints and code violations on this job.
- Insufficient or inconsistent trench slope, creating flat spots where the pipe silts up and stops draining.
- Skipping the hose test before final backfill, missing a low spot until the next rain.
- Not checking whether tying into the street storm drain requires a public works permit.

## Rough SoCal cost (2025-26)

$25-$50 per linear foot for a standard French drain (trench, pipe, gravel, fabric, and surface restoration), depending on trench depth and discharge routing complexity.
