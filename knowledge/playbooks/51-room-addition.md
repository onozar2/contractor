# Playbook: Room Addition (Ground Floor)

*When you need this:* A homeowner wants to expand the existing footprint with a new ground-floor room — bedroom, family room, expanded kitchen — tied into the existing house.

## Sequence

1. **Architect/engineer plan set.** Foundation design, structural framing tie-in to the existing house, and Title 24 energy compliance for the newly conditioned space.
2. **Permit.** Plan-check application with the local building authority — LADBS for City of LA, LA County Building & Safety for unincorporated areas, or the applicable independent city's building department.
3. **DigAlert** before excavation.
4. Excavate and pour new foundation, tied into or adjacent to the existing foundation per the engineer's detail.
5. **Foundation/rebar inspection** before the pour.
6. Frame walls and roof, tying into the existing structure per the engineer's connection detail — this tie-in is the most technically demanding and most closely inspected part of the job, since it has to transfer loads correctly between old and new framing.
7. **Roof tie-in and flashing** at the junction with the existing roofline — the single biggest water-intrusion risk point on this job type, so get the flashing detail right before moving on.
8. Rough plumbing, electrical, and mechanical, extending from existing systems.
9. **Rough MEP inspections.**
10. Insulate to Title 24 requirement.
11. **Insulation/energy inspection.**
12. Drywall.
13. Exterior finish matching the existing siding/stucco texture and color, and roofing material — mismatched finish is what makes an addition read as an obvious add-on.
14. Windows and doors.
15. Interior finishes and paint.
16. **Final MEP and building inspections.**
17. Final walkthrough.

## Commonly missed

- Underdetailing the structural tie-in between new and existing framing, leading to inspection kickback.
- Poor roof tie-in flashing — the most common source of leaks on additions.
- Not matching existing exterior texture/material closely enough, leaving a visible seam.
- Extending MEP from existing systems without confirming those systems have capacity for the added load.

## Rough SoCal cost (2025-26)

$350-$550/sf all-in for a ground-floor addition (foundation, framing, MEP, finishes), with tie-in complexity and site access driving cost toward the higher end.
