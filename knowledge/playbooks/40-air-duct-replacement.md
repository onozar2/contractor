# Playbook: Air Duct Replacement

*When you need this:* old ducts are crushed, disconnected, leaking heavily, or wrapped in deteriorating insulation in an attic/crawlspace, causing poor airflow and high energy bills.

## Sequence

1. Test old duct insulation/wrap for asbestos before disturbing it (very common on original 1960s-80s systems, so pre-1990 raises the odds — but the survey itself is required before regulated demo regardless of building age) — South Coast AQMD Rule 1403 governs notification requirements for disturbing asbestos-containing material, and demolition notification is required even if the survey comes back negative.
2. Permit application: mechanical permit, typically OTC at LADBS.
3. Disconnect and remove old ductwork from attic, crawlspace, or wall chases.
4. Inspect and seal the plenum and equipment connections before installing new duct runs.
5. Install new ducts sized to match the equipment's actual airflow requirements (don't just replicate old duct sizing if the equipment has changed).
6. Insulate new ducts to the current Title 24 R-value requirement (verify the exact target with LADBS/HERS documentation rather than assuming a number).
7. Seal all joints with mastic — code requires mastic sealing, not tape alone, at every connection.
8. Schedule the HERS rater for required duct leakage testing under Title 24 — this is the single most commonly missed step on duct jobs and the reason final inspections stall, so book it as soon as duct work is substantially complete.
9. Schedule and pass mechanical inspection.
10. Restore any attic or crawlspace insulation and access panels disturbed during the work.

## Commonly missed

- Not testing old duct wrap for asbestos before tearing it out — a pre-1990 home with original ducts should be treated as suspect until tested.
- Sealing joints with tape only instead of mastic, which fails the HERS leakage test.
- Forgetting to book the HERS rater until the crew has already broken down, adding weeks to the final inspection timeline.
- Undersizing new ducts to old branch sizing without checking against actual equipment CFM.

## Rough SoCal cost (2025-26)

$3,500–$9,000 for a full attic duct system replacement in an average single-family home (including HERS testing), depending on linear footage, number of runs, and access difficulty.
