# Playbook: SB721 vs SB326 Cheat Sheet (Exterior Elevated Elements)

*When you need this:* quick reference alongside the Balcony/Deck Waterproofing playbook whenever a job involves an elevated balcony, deck, walkway, or stairway on a multifamily rental building or a condo/HOA property — use this to keep the two statutes straight instead of treating them as interchangeable.

## Sequence (how to work the two statutes)

1. **Determine which statute applies — they don't overlap.** SB721 applies to buildings with 3 or more dwelling units where units are rented (apartment buildings). SB326 applies to condominiums and other common-interest developments governed by an HOA. Single-family homes and duplexes aren't covered by either, though proactive inspection is still good practice.
2. **Check inspector eligibility — this is where the two statutes diverge and where mistakes happen.** SB721 (apartments) allows a broader list of qualified inspectors: a licensed architect, a licensed engineer, or a qualified/certified contractor. SB326 (condo/HOA) is stricter: the inspection **must** be performed by a licensed structural engineer or architect — a contractor, even a licensed one, cannot perform or sign a valid SB326 inspection.
3. **Know the deadline.** The initial inspection deadline for **both** SB721 and SB326 is **January 1, 2026**. This is a fixed statutory date, not a "verify current interval" placeholder — treat any property that hasn't had its inspection yet as urgent.
4. **Know the re-inspection cycle after the initial one.** SB721 (apartments): every **6 years**. SB326 (condo/HOA): every **9 years**.
5. **Know who receives the report.** The inspection report goes to the **building owner** (SB721) or the **HOA board**, where it feeds into the association's reserve study (SB326). The report is not routinely filed with the city or county building department.
6. **Know the escalation trigger.** If the inspection finds an *immediate hazard* — a condition posing a risk to life safety — the inspector/owner must notify the local code enforcement agency within a short statutory window (commonly cited as 15 days; verify current requirement) and the affected area must be closed off from use until repaired. Absent an immediate-hazard finding, the local building authority is not automatically looped in.
7. **If repairs are needed:** any framing-level deficiency requires an engineer/architect-prepared repair plan and typically a permit for the repair work — hand off to the Balcony/Deck Waterproofing playbook for the construction sequence once the inspection has scoped the damage.

## Commonly missed

- Assuming "architect, engineer, or licensed contractor" applies to both statutes — it's only true for SB721; a contractor cannot self-inspect a SB326 condo property.
- Treating the January 1, 2026 deadline as a soft "sometime soon" date instead of a fixed compliance cutoff.
- Assuming the report "gets filed with the city" as standard procedure — it goes to the owner/HOA; the city is only notified on an immediate-hazard finding or upon request.
- Mixing up the re-inspection cycles (6 years SB721 vs. 9 years SB326) when advising a property manager on their next inspection window.
- Treating a single-family home job with the same statutory urgency — neither statute covers single-family homes, though the underlying dry-rot/waterproofing risk is real regardless.

## Rough SoCal cost (2025-26)

SB721/SB326 inspection reports typically run $500-$1,500 per building depending on size and accessibility — say "verify current pricing with the inspector" rather than quoting a fixed number for a specific building without scoping it.
