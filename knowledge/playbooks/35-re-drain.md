# Playbook: Re-Drain (Waste/Drain Line Replacement)

*When you need this:* recurring backups, slow drains, sewer odor, or a camera inspection showing cracked/bellied cast iron or corroded galvanized drain lines.

## Sequence

1. Permit: plumbing permit, typically OTC at LADBS for standard scope (unincorporated County via LA County Public Works/Building & Safety; independent cities have their own plumbing permit desk). Under-slab main line work may draw more plan-check scrutiny — verify with LADBS.
2. Camera-inspect existing drain lines to confirm scope, routing, and material.
3. Locate and expose existing lines — this may mean opening walls, cutting attic chases, or trenching/breaking slab for under-slab runs.
4. If old cast iron pipe has wrap or mastic coating, test for asbestos before disturbing it (common in homes built before 1990, but the AQMD Rule 1403 survey requirement applies regardless of building age — treat any suspect wrap as testable).
5. Disconnect old drain/waste/vent (DWV) piping section by section, keeping fixtures functional as long as possible if this is a partial re-drain.
6. Install new DWV piping (ABS or PVC per local code) with correct slope toward the sewer connection — this is the single most inspected and most commonly failed item, so hold minimum fall per foot exactly per code.
7. Tie in vents correctly at each fixture group per code venting requirements.
8. Where new PVC/ABS ties into remaining old cast iron, use proper transition fittings (no-hub bands or code-approved transition couplings) — a common failure point in older homes.
9. Pressure/air-test the new system.
10. Schedule and pass rough plumbing inspection before closing walls or pouring back over trenches — inspector needs to see slope and joints.
11. Backfill and patch slab, or close and patch walls.
12. Reconnect all fixtures.
13. Final inspection.
14. Test all drains for proper flow and confirm no backups under full-fixture load (run multiple fixtures simultaneously).

## Commonly missed

- Losing slope over a long run because of an obstruction (joist, beam, existing utility) — plan the path before cutting.
- Not testing old cast iron insulation/wrap for asbestos before breaking it open.
- Wrong transition fitting between old cast iron and new plastic pipe, causing a leak point at the joint.
- Not scheduling inspection before backfilling slab trenches — forces a costly re-dig.

## Rough SoCal cost (2025-26)

$3,500–$8,000 for a partial re-drain (single bath/kitchen branch); $10,000–$25,000+ for a full house re-drain including slab trenching, depending on linear footage and access.
