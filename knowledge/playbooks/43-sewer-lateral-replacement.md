# Playbook: Sewer Lateral Replacement (Trench vs. Trenchless)

*When you need this:* a camera inspection or recurring backups reveal a collapsed, root-infiltrated, or badly offset sewer lateral between the house and the city main.

## Sequence

1. Camera-inspect the existing lateral to diagnose condition and determine the appropriate method — this drives everything downstream, including whether trenchless is even viable.
2. Permits: plumbing permit at LADBS (or LA County/independent city equivalent), plus a separate street/encroachment permit from public works if the lateral crosses the public right-of-way to reach the city main — this second permit is commonly missed since it's not issued by the same plumbing counter.
3. Call DigAlert for utility locates before any excavation or boring.
4. Choose method based on the camera findings:
   - Open trench: excavate the full length, remove the old pipe, install new pipe at correct slope, backfill and compact in lifts.
   - Trenchless: dig access pits at each end only, then pull new pipe via pipe-bursting or install a cured-in-place liner through the existing pipe — chosen specifically to avoid tearing up a driveway, mature landscaping, or hardscape a trench would destroy, but it can't fix a badly offset or fully collapsed section (camera findings determine eligibility).
5. Connect the new line to the city main and to the house stub.
6. Pressure/flow-test the completed line.
7. Schedule and pass rough inspection before backfilling or patching over the trench/pits — inspector needs to see the connection and slope.
8. Restore any hardscape or landscape disturbed during access.
9. Final inspection.
10. Camera-verify the completed line to confirm slope and a clear bore before closing out the job.

## Commonly missed

- Not pulling the separate street/encroachment permit for right-of-way work, which stalls the job or draws a stop-work notice.
- Assuming trenchless is always viable without confirming via camera that the line isn't badly offset or collapsed in a section.
- Skipping DigAlert before boring near the property line, risking utility strikes.
- Backfilling before the inspector has verified slope and connections.

## Rough SoCal cost (2025-26)

$4,000–$9,000 for trenchless replacement of a standard 40-60 ft residential lateral; $8,000–$18,000+ for open-trench replacement, driven up sharply by driveway/hardscape/landscape restoration and depth.
