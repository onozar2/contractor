# Product Module — Solar

Source: "23. Solar.mp4" (Gemini video-analysis panel, Google Drive) + whiteboard "23_Solar WB.jpg"

## Products & materials

- **Wind turbines**: mentioned only as background — a government-offered option for utility companies to generate electricity, not something he sells directly.
- **Solar panels/units**: capture "pure electricity" from the sun. Individual panel outputs vary (example: 285-kilowatt panels among others). Size and cost scale with efficiency — a smaller panel producing more power costs more. Made by various manufacturers including companies from China and the US.
- **Rails and clamps** (whiteboard confirms all of these labeled on the roof cutaway): panels mount on rails; mid clamps (typically 2 per panel) hold panels together; end clamps secure the outer edges of end panels.
- **Flashing**: connects the rail system to the roof so installers don't damage the roof at drill points.
- **Special racks**: required specifically for tile roofing so installers don't crack tiles.
- **Inverter**: converts DC electricity from the panels into AC electricity; this conversion loses potency, delivering about **80% of the initial power** coming off the panels.
- **Electrical panel & meter**: the home needs an updated panel to handle solar — old 60/80/100-amp panels are commonly insufficient and need upgrading. The meter tracks power produced by the panels vs. power drawn from the grid.
- **Solar batteries** (do NOT come standard with a normal install) — whiteboard confirms two types matching Gemini exactly:
  - DC battery: installed before the inverter (panel → DC battery → inverter).
  - AC battery: installed after the inverter (panel → inverter → AC → battery).
- **Freestanding solar panels**: common in parking lots/commercial spaces, much more expensive than roof-mounted — whiteboard has a dedicated "Free Standing Solar Panel" sketch.
- **Solar patios**: a newer trend — a solid, strong custom wood-built patio (not aluminum or vinyl) with solar integrated as the roof; very expensive. Whiteboard shows a "Solar Patio" sketch matching this.
- **Sensor-based/tracking panels**: use sensors to track the sun through the day; significantly more expensive than fixed panels.
- **Pool solar**: setups designed specifically to warm pools.

## Pricing rules

- **Government incentive history**: incentives started around 2006 at **$2.00/kilowatt**. Today incentives vary by area/company — anywhere from 5¢, 10¢, 20¢, up to $2.00/kilowatt depending on the program.
- **Federal tax credit**: **30%** tax credit on solar systems. Example: a $100,000 system yields a **$30,000 tax credit**, deducted directly from owed taxes, and can roll over up to **3 years** before expiring unused.
- **Utility bill inflation**: utility companies have government approval to raise electric bills **6%-9% every year**, compounding — this is the core "buy now" leverage point (see objections below).
- **Grid connection fee**: even grid-tied solar homes may still pay a "luxury" fee of **$40-$50/month** just to stay connected to the grid.
- **Contractor cost vs. retail formula**: general contractor cost to build a system averages about **$2.00/kilowatt** of usage, ranging **$1.50-$3.50** depending on location/equipment. A standard large system might cost a contractor roughly **$25,000** to install (before retail markup). Smaller systems (5,000-8,000 kWh/year usage) cost proportionally less.
- **Battery costs**: expensive add-on — **+$5,000-$15,000**, sometimes up to **$20,000+**.

## Process & permit rules

- **Determine usage first**: the first step in any home is figuring out the client's daily average kilowatt usage. If they don't have a physical bill, help them open an online utility account on the spot, or call the utility company directly.
- **The 12-month formula**: if a daily average isn't available, pull the exact last 12 months of total electricity usage and average it to size the system correctly.
- **System size limitations**: cities and utility companies will NOT allow a system sized to produce more than the home's actual usage — "homes cannot be used as 'a money-making thing'" by over-producing and forcing the utility to buy back excess power. There's a regulatory cap tied to real consumption.
- **Tier-lowering strategy**: utility bills are split into three tiers — the more electricity used, the higher the tier/rate. Instead of selling a costly full-coverage system, you can write a contract sized just to cover the client's excess usage and drop them out of the expensive top tier(s).
- **Billing adjustments**: clients running a complete solar system are billed by the utility only **once a year** (annual true-up).
- **Engineering plans & permits**: an engineering plan is required before installation. Solar contractor pricing usually covers the engineering plan but typically EXCLUDES the city permit cost — account for that separately in the contract.
- **Flat roof clearance code**: minimum **3-foot clearance** required between the edge of a flat roof and the solar panels.
- **Tilt/elevation**: panels on a flat roof cannot lie flat — must be elevated and ideally face true south (whiteboard has a dedicated "Facing True South" callout with a sun icon).
- **Inspection**: a city inspector must visit to verify rails/panels are bolted securely (and check electrical/grounding, consistent with the inspection pattern in other trade modules).

## What he says to do

- Client has no bill or online account → open an online utility account with them on the spot, or call the utility company directly to pull usage data right there.
- Complete system feels too expensive against a high utility bill → don't sell 100% coverage; design a partial system that offsets just enough usage to drop the client out of the expensive top billing tier (directly uses the Tier-Lowering Strategy above).
- Working on a tile roof → use special tile racks, charge a higher installation rate because it's more complex/riskier, and explicitly tell the client you'll take full responsibility for fixing any tiles broken during install — a written liability assurance to close hesitant tile-roof clients.
- Client questions why buy now vs. later → lean on the utility company's compounding 6%-9% annual rate hikes: explain that staying on standard electricity means their bill will "triple or quadruple" over time, while buying solar now locks in their rate and protects them from future grid-connection cost increases.
