# Product Module — Walls & Fences

Source: "19. Walls-fences.mp4" (Gemini video-analysis panel, Google Drive, ~14:38 runtime) + whiteboard "19_Walls & Fences WB.jpg"

## Products & materials

- **Cinder block wall**: very strong wall type. Standard/mentioned heights of 6 ft or 8 ft. Requires rebar laid out at specific inches-on-center spacing per engineering plans.
- **Garden wall**: a low freestanding wall or small retaining wall holding back dirt. Whiteboard labels it "up to 3 foot high" ([discrepancy] — Gemini's transcript said "up to 3.5 feet"; whiteboard is trusted as ground truth: **3 ft**). Can be freestanding or built as multi-tiered terraces to avoid the cost of one massive tall wall.
- **Rebar**: used inside walls and footings. Spacing (e.g., 4" or 6" on center) and thickness must follow the engineer's plans exactly.
- **Footing types**: three types shown/discussed — L-type, reverse-L-type, and a third T/pier-style type (whiteboard sketches three footing profiles under "Footing Foundation Type" but doesn't label the third by name — [unverified] exact name of the third type).
- **Concrete/cement**: used for footings and filling cinder blocks — minimum **2,500 PSI** [exact upper bound of the range cut off in source].
- **Capping**: finishing material placed on top of a cinder block wall.
- **Cosmetic treatments**: stucco and paint — explicitly categorized as cosmetic add-ons applied to the cinder block, not structural.
- **Electrical/lighting**: wiring for lights can be run through the cinder blocks, but must be done before the concrete is poured.
- **Retaining wall**: holds back a slope/hillside to keep a house or yard from sliding/eroding.
- **French drain / crushed gravel**: mandatory drainage system installed behind retaining walls, layered crushed gravel (shown running along the top-back of the retaining wall on the whiteboard).
- **Weep holes**: small holes designed into the face of some retaining walls to let excess water escape.
- **Pillars/columns**: used at the ends of walls (especially near gates) or spaced along a wall's length for structural support.
- **Fence types**: wood, wrought iron, vinyl, chain-link (all four listed together on the whiteboard). All fence types require footings.
- **Gates**: heavy structures (wood, vinyl, wrought iron, or chain-link) that cannot be anchored directly to the fence panels — need their own dedicated posts/footings.

## Pricing rules

- Cinder block wall: **$90-$150 per linear foot** for a 6-ft-high wall (a different/base range was also referenced later in the video — exact second figure cut off in source, [unverified]).
- City permit fees: **$300-$500**.
- Retaining wall: **$250-$350 per linear foot**, depending on height.
- Height multiplier: as a retaining wall gets taller, price increases exponentially (not linearly) — deeper footing + heavier engineering required.
- Retaining wall engineering plans: **$3,500** flat.
- Soil engineering report: **$3,800-$4,800**.
- Pillars, when bundled into a larger combined job: **$250-$300 per pillar**.
- Pillar repair/replacement as a standalone job (client wants only 1-2 pillars fixed): triggers a minimum job charge [exact minimum dollar figure cut off in source, unverified].
- Gates: **$500-$1,000** standard.
- "Plan rebate formula": when selling a retaining wall job, charge for the engineering plans upfront, then offer some form of credit/rebate against the full job if they proceed [exact rebate mechanism cut off in source — directionally: pay-for-plans-first, credit-back-on-signing].

## Process & contract rules

- **City permit rule**: any block wall or retaining wall over 3.5 ft (per Gemini's phrasing — note this height threshold is different from the "3 ft" garden-wall whiteboard label; the whiteboard doesn't specify the permit-trigger height directly, so 3.5 ft is used here per source, [unverified exact threshold]) requires formal engineering plans and city permitting.
- **Contract fee clause**: city permit fees must NEVER be bundled into the base price — write an explicit clause: "Owner will pay for city fees."
- **Inspection protocol**: dig the trench and set rebar strictly per the approved engineering diagrams before pouring any concrete. If it doesn't match plans, you'll be forced to redo it [consequence detail cut off].
- **Plan modification process**: if the engineer's plan is "overkill" (excessive/expensive material specs), the contractor has the option to push back and get it revised (see objection-handling below).
- **Retaining wall quoting rule**: never give a final price on a retaining wall until engineering plans are fully completed — quote the plans first, price the wall after.
- **Soil report process**: for most retaining walls, a soil engineer must drill into the bedrock/mountain to map soil conditions before final city plans can be designed.

## What he says to do

- Engineer's plan comes back as "overkill" (e.g., digging unnecessarily deep/wide footings) → don't just blindly build to spec. Go back to city planning, submit a revised and more reasonable plan, and wait for approval before proceeding.
- Client wants a pool on a sloped yard → tell them there's no cheap way around it — the slope rules out cheap terraced garden walls, so they must absorb the higher cost of a proper retaining wall to make the pad level and safe.
- Client doesn't like the look of visible weep holes on a retaining wall → redesign to a heavier-duty hidden French drain system instead of exposed weep holes.
- Client says "let me ask my neighbor" or the neighbor is sharing the cost (shared wall/fence line) → stop, do not give a price yet. Get the neighbor's contact info immediately and get both parties booked together as fast as possible — same day or the very next day — rather than letting the decision stall.
