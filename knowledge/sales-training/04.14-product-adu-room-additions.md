## Products & materials

- **ADU (Accessory Dwelling Unit)**: can be a standard one-story garage conversion, a second-story addition on top of a garage (to preserve parking), or new ground-up construction. Standard garage-to-ADU conversions are highly popular as income properties, but physical lot limitations often cap what's buildable.
- **Junior ADU (JADU)**: typically created by converting an attached garage; more size-limited than a full ADU. Pairing a JADU with a standard ADU can effectively turn a single-family R1 lot into a triplex. Josh's explicit warning: "if any of you have never done an ADU I wouldn't try doing this for your first project" — JADUs involve navigating multiple utility meters and complex plumbing connections, making them one of the harder product types to sell/build correctly.
- **Water heaters — standard tank vs. tankless**: a standard tank heater must be raised off the floor on a platform, needs direct exterior ventilation, and takes up interior room — Josh advises against putting a standard tank heater on the interior for an ADU. Tankless heaters are energy-efficient, wall-mounted, and best installed as close as possible to the major hot-water fixtures (bathroom/kitchen) to avoid delayed hot water at the faucet if installed too far away.
- **Rain barrels vs. built-in planters**: California public-works drought rules mandate a rainwater catchment solution, with the city specifying exact placement/sizing — required to pass final inspection but "pretty ugly." Built-in planters (downspouts routed into planter boxes doubling as drip irrigation) are a more attractive, higher-margin upsell alternative to a standalone rain barrel.
- **Windows & railings (code)**: any large window with its bottom sitting 30 inches or higher off the floor requires a 42-inch safety railing — matches the whiteboard's "42"" / "30"" callouts on the elevation sketch exactly.
- **Framing materials**: standard studs are 2x4s spaced 16 inches apart; larger structures over 20 ft in height need different/reinforced framing [exact spec cut off].
- **Foundations — raised crawlspace vs. concrete slab**: ground-level room additions can use either. Raised foundations cost slightly more in materials/labor, but matching the existing home's foundation type is often preferred for structural/aesthetic consistency.

## Pricing rules

- **ADU ballpark/qualifying range**: always pitch a **$50,000 spread** on the first walkthrough — give the client a baseline range of **$150,000-$200,000** to qualify them before investing more time.
- **ADU planning contract fee**: secure the client first with a **$2,500-$3,500** contract covering initial city research, surveys, and architectural footwork — this amount is later deducted from the full construction contract if they proceed.
- **Historical zoning upcharge**: building in an HPOZ (historic preservation overlay zone) increases exterior material costs (stucco, siding, windows) by **30% more** than a normal ADU.
- **Rain barrel cost**: **$260-$460**, depending on city-mandated size.
- **Tankless water heater**: standard unit **$3,800-$4,000**; adding a hot-water circulation system (to fix delayed hot water) is an additional **$800-$1,500** upsell.
- **Room addition cost per square foot**: single-story **$150-$250/sq ft** (**$180/sq ft considered a "fair price"**); second-story **$200-$300/sq ft** due to structural reinforcement requirements.
- **Engineering plan costs**: first-story addition plans **$3,000-$5,000**; second-story addition plans **$5,000-$8,000**.

## Process & permit rules

- **Power line easement process**: if power lines run directly over a proposed ADU footprint, a utility easement application is required and can take **6-8 months** — a major schedule risk to flag early. Matches whiteboard's "Things to Look Out For" list item #1, "Power Line."
- **Contract exclusions (critical rule)**: never bundle municipal/permit fees into the construction contract — state explicitly "owner will pay for city permit." Soil reports (thousands of dollars, **4-6 months**) and hillside topography surveys should also be explicitly excluded from the base contract.
- **Architectural corrections cap**: a standard design contract should specify the architect covers city corrections only "up to three corrections" — beyond that is a change order.
- **Wet signatures**: required before submitting plans to city plan check or handing drafts to engineers [exact requirement detail cut off].
- **Utility/mailing address rule**: establishing a separate electrical meter (so a tenant gets an independent bill) requires a distinct process [exact steps cut off].
- **Certificate of Occupancy (CFO)**: an ADU is legally uninhabitable until the city issues a CFO — moving a tenant in before that is a compliance risk.
- **Timeline expectations**: one-story ADU on flat land with an experienced architect ≈ **12 months** total (plans, city approval, construction); two-story ADU ≈ **14-16 months**.
- **Whiteboard's "Things to Look Out For" checklist** (matches the process-risk framing above): 1. Power Line, 2. Zoning, 3. Historical, 4. HOA, 5. Parking.

## What he says to do

- **"I want to do this as cheap as possible"** → understand this isn't about being cheap personally — the client is evaluating the project strictly as an investment/income property, so frame the pitch around ROI, not just price.
- **"Your competitor is 25% cheaper"** → reframe the objection around time-to-income and hassle, not price: point out a small mom-and-pop crew (e.g., two guys) will take months longer to finish — "How long do you think this is going to take?" — and that a faster, professional crew means less neighbor friction, dust, and driveway disruption.
- **"The overall construction bid is too high"** → use the "design-first" structure: get plans finalized and bid the actual construction cost BEFORE submitting to city plan check, so the client can adjust scope/design if the number comes in high, without wasting permit fees or time.
- **"I don't want a tankless heater visible on the outside of my house"** → agree and pivot to an upsell: relocate it out of the sightline and recommend adding the hot-water circulation system as a bundled improvement.
