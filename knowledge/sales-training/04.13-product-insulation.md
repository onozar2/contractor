# Product Module — Insulation

## Products & materials

- **Fiberglass batt rolls**: the pink roll material that's rolled out by hand. Recommended R-value for attics is **R38** (very thick); R30, R20, R15, R13 are also used depending on the job. Walls use **R13** (not very thick); underneath the home uses **R19** (whiteboard confirms exactly: Attic R-38, Wall R-13, Under Floor R-19). Pros: denser and more efficient than blown-in insulation. Cons: slightly more expensive and can't easily reach tight/tiny attic corners the way blown-in can.
- **Blown-in insulation**: loose material blown into the space via a hose connected to a large supply truck. Pros: the go-to for very small attics you must crawl through (reaches tight corners) and for insulating already-closed walls without tearing them down (blown through drilled 1-inch holes). Cons: much looser/less dense than fiberglass batts; not recommended under the home because it makes "a big mess going on down there."
- **Foam insulation**: sprayed into the attic (including the top, where it holds strongly), between wall studs, or underneath the floor. Described as "the best insulation" — dries to become incredibly strong. Cons: more expensive to install, and very difficult to remove once dry (has to be physically broken out).
- **Radiant barrier**: deflects heat away from the home. Either integrated into specialized plywood during a roof replacement, or laid down under shingles. Inexpensive add-on. **Placement rule/warning**: never put it directly on the attic floor or on top of floor insulation — "that's something I wouldn't do, it's basically defeating the whole purpose of radiant barrier." Best method is installing it on the underside of the roof decking inside the attic.
- **Soundproofing**: standard insulation provides only minor soundproofing as a side effect. Dedicated soundproofing is expensive and requires drywall removal to do on a wall.

## Pricing rules

- Attic fiberglass batt install: **$1.80-$2.00/sq ft**; for an R38 rating specifically, **$1.80-$2.50/sq ft**.
- Attic foam insulation: add **$0.50-$1.00/sq ft more** than the fiberglass batt price (higher material + labor cost).
- **R-value upgrade pricing**: jumping from R20 or R30 up to R38 costs only "cents per square foot" more — a very cheap upsell.
- Radiant barrier: **$0.30-$0.80/sq ft**.
- Soundproofing: **$30-$50/sq ft**, with a **$1,000 project minimum**. This price covers the soundproofing material only — it does NOT include removing/replacing the drywall needed to access the wall cavity.

## Process & permit rules

- **Old insulation removal**: strongly advised to use a large vacuum machine to remove old attic insulation rather than just adding new material on top.
- **Blow-in process for already-closed walls**: drill 1-inch holes every 16 inches around the home (stud to stud); drill 3 vertical holes per section (bottom, middle, top) so the entire wall cavity fills; blow insulation in starting from the bottom hole, then middle, then top; cap the holes and patch/texture-match.
- **Exterior wall-material variation**: stucco siding → drill and patch from the exterior. Wood siding → do NOT drill from the outside (too hard to patch seamlessly) — drill from inside through the drywall instead, then patch/tape/texture the interior wall.
- **Under-home crawl space rule**: never over-pack insulation under the home to the point it blocks physical access — must leave room to crawl under and service plumbing.
- **Permits**: standard insulation replacement typically does not require a permit; new construction or broader home remodeling likely does.

## What he says to do

- **Client fears mismatched wall texture from drilled blow-in holes** → set the expectation up front, in conversation and in the contract: "We cannot match it 100 percent, we'll try and match it as much as possible."
- **Client objects to cost** → reframe insulation as "a very, very easy thing to sell" because it doesn't cost much relative to the energy-efficiency savings it delivers. If they're hesitant about upgrading to a higher R-value (e.g., R38), point out the price difference is only cents per square foot — an easy, high-margin upsell.
