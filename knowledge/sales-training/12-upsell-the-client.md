# Upsell the Client (Upsale / Referrals)

Source: "FINAL 1080p 38. Upsale Referrals.mp4" (Gemini video-analysis panel, Google Drive folder "10. Upsell the Client"). No whiteboard exists for this module. This is one of the richest modules in the training — heavy on named dollar examples and verbatim scripts.

## Core framing: needs vs. luxury

Josh splits project types into two buckets that drive how hard/fast you can upsell:
- **"Need" projects** (sell with urgency, little resistance): active plumbing leaks ($10,000-$50,000 — "clients have nowhere to go"), old/fire-hazard electrical panels, structural issues (foundation, underpinning, re-bolting), roofing framed as damage-prevention (his example: $25,000 today vs. a much worse number if you wait ~10 years and let damage compound).
- **"Luxury/pleasure" projects** (require more selling, no urgency): kitchens, bathrooms, flooring, windows — unless one of these is tied to an actual active problem.

## Product/project examples with real numbers

- **$50,000 kitchen** — his running example for "advanced salesmen should feel comfortable" pursuing a large upsell off an existing job.
- **Gazebo + 2 free recessed lights** — personal story: sold a gazebo at the company's baseline cost, then threw in two recessed lights for free. The free electrical work gave him a legitimate reason to bring an electrician into the house, which built trust and led to upselling the client on multiple other jobs — including a **$22,000 electrical job**.
- **Driveways** — 1-2 day jobs; because the crew is only on-site briefly, you get exactly one day to prep and pitch the client before the upsell window closes.
- **Exterior painting → foundation upsell chain** — put your subcontractor to work painting, and have them flag foundation issues (needing underpinning/re-bolting) while they're already on the ladder/at the house. The paint job becomes the delivery vehicle for a structural referral.

## Pricing rules

- **The Multiplier Rule**: target an upsell that's a minimum of **2x-2.5x** the original sale. Don't chase progressively smaller add-ons (e.g., $10,000 job → $5,000 → $2,000) — that's the wrong direction. Go bigger on the follow-on sale, not smaller.
- **The 50/50 Split Rule**: a sales manager offered to split commission 50/50 with the salesman if the manager personally came along to help close an upsell/referral on a baseline-cost project — an example of when to loop in management for a bigger swing.
- **Financing & cash deposits**: even on a financed job, always collect the initial deposit (example given: **$1,000 down payment**) in actual cash or check. This mentally moves the client out of the "financing box" and makes them comfortable paying cash on the next (upsell) project.
- **Contract Separatist Rule**: never bundle an upsell into the original contract, and never cancel/rewrite the original contract to merge them. Example: a $50,000 kitchen + a $30,000 roof combined into one $80,000 contract looks too big/scary. Keep them as physically separate documents — "Contract Number One" and "Contract Number Two" — to avoid sticker shock.

## Process & timing — four approaches

1. **Day-After Preparation**: plant a seed on visit one (mention other jobs you've done, ask a hypothetical), let it sit, then come back **1-2 days later** to formally pitch and write the referral contract.
2. **Subcontractor Hand-off**: your sub (painter, electrician, etc.) inspects the property and photographs a hidden problem while doing the original job. You must follow up **the same day or the very next day** — waiting a week is too long and kills the opportunity.
3. **On-the-Spot Presentation**: treat the client like a brand-new lead with zero prior context. Sit down for **2-3 hours**, run a full presentation from scratch (including the neighborhood-credibility play from 06-the-soup), and close right then.
4. **Free Gift Upfront**: give away a relevant, high-value freebie (recessed lights, a fan light, a faucet, etc.) while closing the ORIGINAL contract. This gets your specialized crew physically inside the home's utility systems, which is where they spot the next upsell.

## Objections & scripts

**"I don't have the money" / "we're on a tight budget"**
- Read: this is usually a reluctance-to-spend-right-now objection, not a real reflection of their bank balance.
- Response: stop projecting your own financial limits onto the client. If they've owned the home 20+ years, assume they have funds or credit — a baseline of at least $50,000-$60,000 cash, possibly much more in other assets.

**"They only got approved for $X in financing" (salesman self-limits the upsell)**
- Response: bad credit ≠ zero money. Break the client out of the "financing box" early by requiring a cash/check down payment on the original job; once they've paid cash once, pivot openly to pitching cash for the upsell work too.

**The "Magic Wand" discovery script** (use this to find the next upsell):
- "If I had a magic wand and I could give you anything you want, what would be the next project you wi[sh you could do]?"

**"I want to wait 2 years to do that"** (client names a project via the Magic Wand script but stalls the timeline)
- Response: immediately plant urgency using a nearby-job name-drop, e.g.: "The bathroom? Funny that you're talking about the bathroom. We are doing Mrs. Weiner — [a nearby neighbor/example]..." Then walk away and let the social-proof/FOMO sit.

**"I'm confused, I thought this was included"** (client feigns misunderstanding on upsell pricing, e.g. "I thought the $50,000 kitchen included the roof")
- Response: physically hold up the two separate contract documents in front of them: "You see, this is Contract Number One, and this is Contract Number Two." Show it clearly, then put it away. Since they know exactly what they signed, the physical visual ends the objection without an argument — this is why the Contract Separatist Rule (above) matters.

**The relationship/trust principle behind all of this**: remodeling is treated as an emotional relationship — "the client is essentially married to you during construction." Keep small gifts in your car at all times (examples given: holy oils/water from a trip to Israel, other small tokens) to give spontaneously. His summary line: **"You give them a $20 flower, they give you a $20,000 bathroom. That's how it works."**
