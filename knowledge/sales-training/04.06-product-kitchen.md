# Product Module — Kitchen

Source: "31. Kitchen Export RED.mp4" (Gemini video-analysis panel, Google Drive) + whiteboard "31_Kitchen WB.jpg"

## Products & materials

**Electrical**:
- GFCI outlet: a "normal outlet" with a reset button — if water touches it, only that outlet trips instead of blacking out the house. Code requires one every 3 feet of countertop space.
- 220 outlet/line: a direct, separately-switched line to the panel that powers only the stove — thick wire, big outlet.
- Recessed lights: require running new electrical lines (more holes/patchwork).
- Under-cabinet lighting: a dedicated electrical line point to specific cabinets, connected to a single [switch/transformer — detail cut off].

**Cabinets** — whiteboard confirms 3-way "Cabinet Type" list (Custom, Semi-Custom/Prefab, Laminate) matching Gemini exactly:
- Full custom: built exactly to the client's measurements, style, wood type, and special features.
- Semi-custom: pre-made boxes assembled like Lego from a catalog of options — client can't choose wood type or fully custom dimensions.
- Laminated: crushed wood with a laminate top; shiny or matte finish; common in apartment buildings to save cost.
- Add-ons: spice rack pull-out, garbage pull-out (looks like a normal door, slides open), soft-closing drawers/hinges, wine rack (usually requires upgrading to full custom — not typically available on semi-custom), glass cabinet doors (upsell), pantry (floor-to-ceiling cabinet, no backsplash gap — can be shelving or pull-out drawers, each shelf level priced as its own "cabinet"), lazy Susan (rotating corner cabinet — recommended for every kitchen corner), crown molding (top finish), toe-kick molding (bottom finish), door knobs (rounded) vs. door pulls (longer bar hardware).
- Refinishing: acrylic paint is sprayed on when repainting cabinets. Varnish stain darkens/enriches color; clear coat stain preserves original wood color while adding protective sheen.
- Cabinet refacing: replace only the front doors, keep the existing boxes; hinges must also be replaced; the new doors must be full-custom to fit the original boxes exactly.
- Island legs (decorative) and waterfall island design (countertop material wraps down the sides) are both shown on the whiteboard under "Island."

**Countertops** — whiteboard's "Counter-Top Type" list (Granite, Quartz, Marble, Formica/Laminate, Butcher Block) matches Gemini:
- Quartz: engineered stone (crushed rock + additives); stronger/more durable than granite or marble, scratch-resistant, handles heat well.
- Granite: natural stone, high upkeep — needs resealing every 6 months or it cracks/crumbles; can crack under hot items.
- Marble: similar to granite but slightly stronger/more durable; more expensive due to rarity.
- Laminated Formica: crushed wood + laminate top; cheap, lasts 20-25 years but starts peeling [before that — exact peel-onset timing cut off].
- Butcher block: solid wood planks glued/sanded/stained; usually only on islands or small prep areas.
- Tile countertop: old-school, prone to black mold/dirt in grout lines; hard to source matching custom tile.

**Backsplash**: can match the countertop material, or be tile/glass/subway tile. Whiteboard specifies backsplash sizing: Full, 6" (tile/glass), or 4" (subway) — matches Gemini's "full, 4-inch, or 6-inch" sizing.

**Sink types** (whiteboard: "Sink Type — 1. Top/Overmount 2. Undermount 3. Farmhouse"):
- Over-mount: traditional, sits on top of the countertop.
- Under-mount: modern, installed from below — easier to clean (no lip to catch crumbs).
- Farmhouse/farm sink: traditional-but-modern style, sink face fully exposed/visible at the cabinet front.

**Plumbing/appliances**:
- Dishwasher air gap: ensures dirty water drains correctly, prevents backflow.
- Garbage disposal: mounted under the sink; can be operated by wall switch or a button package next to the sink (button recommended to keep kids from accidentally activating it).
- Downdraft hood: pulls cooking air down and out under the home — hard to install on a concrete slab unless you break into the concrete.
- Micro-hood: combined microwave + range hood, vents via metal/aluminum ducting through the cabinets and out the roof.

## Pricing rules

- End-of-project cleaning lady: $250.
- Cabinets priced per linear foot (side to side):
  - Full custom: $200-$300/linear ft.
  - Semi-custom: $110-$180/linear ft.
  - Laminated: $70-$100/linear ft (cheap) up to $180-$300/linear ft (expensive versions).
  - Glass cabinet doors upsell: +$20-$30/linear ft.
  - Pantry pull-out drawers: ~$30-$40 per drawer.
  - Lazy Susan: +$50-$100.
  - Cabinet refacing (doors only): $180-$250.
  - Island legs: no more than $150/leg.
- Countertops:
  - Fabricator risk fee: fabricators may raise their rate from a base of $25 up to $35-$40 depending on how risky/complex the slab cut is.
  - Laminated Formica: $25-$35/sq ft.
  - Butcher block: $1,000-$1,500 for a small area.
- Farmhouse sink: $500-$2,000 depending on design/manufacturer.
- Garbage disposal: normal unit ~$80-$100; unit with safety button package ~$150 (+$50-$60 over base).
- City gas line main install (street to home meter): most cities charge $1,000-$2,000.
- Refrigerator ice-maker line install: $200-$350.
- Project timeline: low-priced competitors can take 4-8 months (sometimes over a year); his company completes a kitchen professionally in 4-5 weeks.
- Company tenure: 15-25 years in business, used to back long-term warranty claims.

## Process & contract rules

- **Permits**: a city permit is required for a kitchen remodel (structural inspections) and separately for running a gas line from the meter to the home — these can sometimes be combined [exact combination condition cut off]. Whiteboard flags this directly: "Permit Needed."
- **Countertop outlet code**: outlets every 3 ft of countertop space to pass inspection.
- **Appliance line code**: every appliance (microwave, fridge, dishwasher, stove) needs its own dedicated electrical line to the panel.
- **Electrical contract exclusion**: always state that the price does NOT include running dedicated appliance lines to the panel unless specified/known beforehand.
- **Plumbing contract exclusion**: plumbing work is limited strictly to the work area — does not include re-plumbing under the home.
- **Wall closure rule**: never close up walls/holes until the city inspector checks electrical/plumbing and signs off.
- **Painting sequence**: apply only primer before installing cabinets/countertops; do the final coat after installation (to cover inevitable dirt/scratches from the install).
- **Flooring sequence**: on a full remodel with new flooring, lay the floor FIRST so cabinets sit on top of it; if keeping old cabinets and just redoing flooring, run the floor up to the cabinet and finish with trim.
- **Cabinet sanding clarification**: tell the client you sand to prep, but do NOT sand cabinets all the way down to bare wood.
- **Cabinet interior rule**: cabinet painting/staining quotes do NOT include the inside of the cabinets — state this clearly.
- **Staining vs. painting limitation**: stained cabinets can be painted over; painted cabinets can be repainted; but painted cabinets CANNOT be stripped back down to stain — this is a one-way door, tell the client.
- **Island clearance code**: at least 4 feet of clearance on one side of an island for handicap accessibility, to pass code.
- **Countertop measurement rule**: never take final templates/measurements for the countertop until cabinet installation is complete; fabrication then takes up to 3 days.
- **Outlet backsplash placement**: outlets must be set elevated during construction so they end up fully above a 4" or 6" backsplash.
- **Stove ventilation code**: a stove fails inspection without a functional downdraft or ducted hood vent.
- **Walkthrough process**: walk the finished kitchen with the client using blue tape to mark all touch-up items.

## What he says to do

- Client is scared by the word "demo" → soften it: explain it just means carefully removing the old cabinets, countertops, flooring, etc. — not a violent teardown.
- Client worried about remodeling dust → warn upfront the home will be dusty for about a month, and reassure them a professional house cleaning is included at the end (ties to the $250 cleaning-lady line item).
- Client upset about a city inspector finding a violation → tell them at the very start that 1-2 corrections during inspection is normal, to manage expectations in advance.
- Client insists on an island in a too-tight space → formally recommend against it; if they insist anyway, document that recommendation in writing on the contract to protect yourself.
- Client wants a short 4" or 6" tile/glass backsplash → advise that tile/glass/subway should only be done as a FULL backsplash (a short run of individual tiles looks bad); if they insist, write the warning into the contract for protection.
- Client anxious about scratches/damage mid-project → prep them at the very start: "Don't worry, you will have problems, you will..." [rest of the line cut off, but the gist is normalizing mid-project cosmetic damage]. Walk them through the blue-tape end-of-job process so they know it gets fixed.
- Client objects that the estimate is $20,000 more than 2-3 other bids (example given: his $85,000 vs. others' $50,000-$60,000) → sell personal customer service, communication, and professionalism; explain cheap contractors use one generalist for the whole kitchen poorly, vs. his dedicated specialized crews (a cabinet guy, a countertop guy, etc.); uses the analogy that a world-class brain surgeon shouldn't do heart surgery just because he's a surgeon; tells horror stories of cheap contractors taking 6-12+ months; reassures that paying more gets it done beautifully in 4-5 weeks; closes with the 15-25 years in business point to back the warranty.
