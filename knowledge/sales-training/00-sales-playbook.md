## 1. Walk-in / First Contact

- Find something in common between you and the client; give compliments; smile; use physical connection (handshake/tap on shoulder) and mental connection (look/smell/dress well). (06-the-soup, step 1 "Connection")
- Compliment the client 2-3x per hour; scan for personal-connection cues (Ori's notes reference "look above the fireplace" — reading the room for family photos/mementos to find common ground). (06-the-soup, Ori's-notes cross-reference — [unverified] interpretation)
- Tell the client about 2-3 projects you're currently doing in their neighborhood — this (a) proves you're a local contractor, (b) pre-loads ammunition for closing rebuttals later, (c) gives you leverage if the client later wants to cancel. (06-the-soup, step 2 "Building a Strategy")

## 2. Rapport / Credibility Building

- If the client wants the price immediately (skips small talk), skip ahead to Measurements rather than forcing rapport-building first. (06-the-soup, step 3)
- Explain the product/scope to the client in extreme detail. (06-the-soup, step 4 "Build the Product")
- Build personal credibility: family man, time in the business, giving to charity, successful, smart, religious, no drinking/gambling. Build company credibility: time in business, licensed/bonded/insured/workers' comp, good reviews, big company, big projects, warranties, family business, return clients, "apples to apples" guarantee. (06-the-soup, step 5)
- 75% of the time you speak must be in stories — not raw facts/features. (06-the-soup, step 6)
- Measure everything, even where not strictly necessary — it builds credibility by showing your price is grounded in real numbers, not guesswork. (Ori's-notes cross-reference)

## 3. Decision-Maker Qualification ("One Legger")

**Never give a price to a one-legger.** If only one decision-maker (spouse/co-owner) is present or engaged, connect, build rapport, take measurements — but do NOT quote a price. A price given to only one partner gets shopped around with no one there to defend it. Both partners must be physically present to sign a contract; couples almost never (95-99% of the time, per the video) make a structural financial decision without both present. (08-one-legger)

Five situational scripts:
1. **Won't let you inside** (one spouse talks outside, the other stays in): build rapport with the outside spouse first; the indoor spouse usually comes out naturally within 30-40 min out of curiosity — greet them warmly by name. If nobody comes out by 30-45 min, state (don't ask) "Okay, let's go in the house" and walk in with confidence. (08-one-legger)
2. **Spouse hiding in the bedroom/office** ("I'm making all the decisions") — treat that claim as false; build rapport with the present spouse for 30-40 min first, then ask them to call their partner out, or walk toward the room yourself and invite them out. (08-one-legger)
3. **Only one spouse physically home**: build rapport for 30-40 min, do NOT price, and book a second meeting within 24 hours using the "run the numbers" cover story (you need to go back to the office). Rebuild rapport from scratch with the second partner when you return. (08-one-legger)
4. **Neighbor is financially involved but absent** (e.g., a shared-cost fence) → do not price; reschedule to meet both households together. (08-one-legger; cross-reference 04.04-walls-fences neighbor-cost-share script)
5. **A partner tries to leave right as you reach pricing** → stop them immediately and take charge: "I need you here in order to finish/decide" — don't let the meeting fragment right before the close. (08-one-legger)

**The 24-hour follow-up rule**: schedule any one-legger's second meeting same-day or within 24 hours — wait longer and "most likely you'll never walk into that house again." (08-one-legger)

## 4. Measuring / Diagnosing the Job

- Take measurements to show the client how you arrived at the price; this is also the fallback opener if the client skips rapport and wants a number right away. (06-the-soup, step 3)
- Diagnose structural issues before quoting: e.g., check for floor slope before quoting tile (cement floating required if 1-2"), check soil/engineering needs before quoting a retaining wall or a pool. (04.02-flooring; 04.04-walls-fences; 04.08-pool-jacuzzi)
- Ask when the house was built and test for asbestos before touching any acoustic/popcorn ceiling — never assume, never scrape first. (04.03-asbestos-mold; 04.05-painting)
- Pull the client's last 12 months of utility bills (or open an account with them on the spot) before sizing a solar system — systems cannot legally be oversized beyond actual usage. (04.09-solar)
- On a solar consult, determine whether a tier-lowering partial system beats a full-coverage system before pricing — cheaper sell, same urgency lever (utility rates compound 6-9%/yr). (04.09-solar)
- On windows, confirm operable-side preference (the XO blueprint system: X=fixed, O=slides) with the client before finalizing any order — get this wrong and the whole order is void. (04.10-windows)
- On roofing, count existing layers before quoting an over-layer — code caps at 2 layers max; a home already at 2 legally requires a full tear-off. (04.11-roofing)
- On a bathroom, do not treat plumbing scope beyond the immediate wall as included — state the exclusion explicitly, and confirm all client-selected finish materials are on-site before starting (mid-job material ordering costs 2-3 weeks). (04.12-bathroom)
- On any ADU pitch, check for power lines crossing the proposed footprint before quoting — a utility easement can add 6-8 months to the timeline. Also flag zoning, historical (HPOZ), HOA, and parking constraints early (the "Things to Look Out For" checklist). (04.14-ADU-room-additions)
- On an electrical job, identify the panel amperage (60-100 amp panels are a fire-hazard/upgrade opportunity) and count how many wall openings a rewire will require (100-150 holes on a standard 1,200-1,500 sq ft home) before pricing. (04.15-electrical)
- On a plumbing job, identify pipe material age/type first (galvanized water lines corrode at 15-20 years; cast iron drain lines corrode/burst around 20 years) — this is both a diagnostic and the natural opening for the urgency-based objection scripts below. (04.16-plumbing)
- On any squeaky/hollow/bouncy floor complaint, go under the house and photograph the actual failure (rotted/rusted pillars, missing T-brackets) before pricing a foundation repair — the photo IS the sales tool. (04.17-foundation)

## 5. Presenting the Price

- **Cash is quoted as a total price; finance is quoted in payments** — frame the same job differently depending on which the client is doing. (06-the-soup, step 7)
- **The anchor**: reference 2-3 similar past projects, quote THOSE at **4x-4.5x your cost** — do this about 10 minutes before giving the client's actual price. (06-the-soup, step 8 "Building the Price"; confirmed independently by Ori's notes)
- **The real price**: quote the client's actual job at **3x-3.5x your cost** — lower than the anchor, so it reads as a deal. Never give a round number (use exact figures, e.g., $42,750 not "$43k" — Ori's notes). (06-the-soup, step 9 "Giving the Price"; Ori's-notes cross-reference)
- Sequence for giving the price: tell the client what you're going to do → ask if they have questions → answer them → recap what you're going to do → THEN state the price → stop talking, let the client speak first. (06-the-soup, step 9)
- If the client says no on the spot: "Not a problem, the price is good [for] 90 days" — leaves the door open without pressure. (06-the-soup, step 9)
- Never write/imply an exact tight completion date on the eventual contract — pad heavily (see Contract section below); missing a date is a breach. (05-write-a-contract)
- Don't sell everything at once on a big-ticket, multi-part job (e.g., pool): close the core scope first, then upsell fencing/landscaping/heater as separate add-ons later — same phased logic as the Upsell section below. (04.08-pool-jacuzzi)

## 6. Objection Handling

**Wait 10 minutes after giving the price before starting any rebuttal/closing tool** — don't rush straight into pressure tactics. (06-the-soup, closing box; 07-closing)

### The Universal Rebuttal (09-top-10-excuses)

**Every excuse a client gives — more estimates, "ask my spouse/kids," "need to sleep on it," an upcoming vacation, "you're my first estimate," "next year," "no money," "money coming in soon," "it's tax time" — means one thing: the price is too high.** Don't treat the 10 excuses as 10 different problems needing 10 different scripts; treat every one as the same underlying price objection and re-sell value. This framing is reinforced independently in 10-cancellation (same "every cancellation reason is really a price objection" logic) — treat the two as the same principle taught twice. The exact verbatim isolation script was not recoverable from the source video; the principle itself is confirmed.

### The 6 Closing/Rebuttal tools (07-closing)
1. Exit/Bathroom/Phone — natural pause/reset point in a stalled close.
2. Company gift card — incentive/concession to restart momentum.
3. Front yard sign / before-after photos / video testimonial / good review — social proof, sometimes traded for a discount.
4. **Benjamin Franklin pros/cons close** — Pros: "You like me?" / "You like the company?" / "You like the product?" / "You like that we're licensed, bonded, and insured?" Cons: ONLY "Do you like the price?" — isolates price as the single objection against a wall of yeses.
5. Good cop / bad cop.
6. "Car" — [exact mechanic unverified, possibly a manager-escalation stall].

### Trade-specific objection scripts (each tagged to its module)
- **Price is $20K+ higher than 2-3 other bids** → sell personal service/communication/professionalism; contrast dedicated specialist crews vs. one generalist; brain-surgeon analogy (a great surgeon shouldn't do every kind of surgery); horror-story cheap contractors taking 6-12+ months; close with 15-25 years in business to back the warranty. (04.06-kitchen)
- **Underbid by a cheap competitor on a bathroom** (e.g., $20K vs. your $35K) → shift the conversation away from basic labor toward artistic bathroom design; show portfolio/video of finished luxury projects; pitch the result as a "piece of art," not a utility room. (04.12-bathroom)
- **"I only buy on lowest price" on a roof** → tell the temple story: $200K+ spent on high-end interior remodeling but the roof was skipped, and a winter leak then cost an extra $50K in drywall/electrical/carpet damage. The roof protects everything else in the home. (04.11-roofing)
- **Client thinks a full paint/remodel job takes 1-2 days** (based on having seen it done fast before) → explain a professional job takes 2-3 weeks / [longer per trade] due to real prep work; warn that skipping prep is exactly what let the fast/cheap job happen. (04.05-painting)
- **Client anxious about mid-project damage/mess/dust** → normalize it up front ("you will have problems, you will...") and explain the end-of-job walkthrough/cleanup process (blue tape marking, $250 cleaning service) so it doesn't blindside them. (04.05-painting; 04.06-kitchen)
- **"Let me ask my neighbor" / neighbor is splitting the cost** → stop, do NOT give a price yet; get the neighbor's contact info immediately and book both parties together same-day or next-day rather than letting the decision stall. (04.04-walls-fences; cross-reference 08-one-legger Situation 4)
- **Client wants to bypass full scope for a cheaper shortcut** (short backsplash instead of full, standard grout instead of sealed, encapsulate instead of remove) → advise against it, and if they insist, document the warning IN WRITING on the contract to protect yourself. (04.05-painting; 04.06-kitchen)
- **Client questions why solar now vs. later** → lean on the utility company's compounding 6-9%/yr rate hikes: staying on standard electricity means the bill "triples or quadruples" over time, while buying now locks in the rate. (04.09-solar)
- **Client objects to a 10-year (not lifetime) labor warranty on windows** → "if in 10 years your window is good, most likely it will last for 30 years" — compare to a car: manufacturing defects surface early, so surviving the warranty period is a strong signal of long-term durability. Always clarify separately that the lifetime warranty covers PRODUCT only, never labor. (04.10-windows)
- **Client doesn't trust giving a down payment** → if you trust them, write "$0.00" instead of leaving it blank; otherwise negotiate down to a smaller deposit ($500 / $100 / $50) rather than $0 — never leave truly blank. (05-write-a-contract)
- **Client objects to the arbitration clause** → explain arbitration is faster than court (though pricier); if they flatly refuse to initial, call your boss/owner immediately before proceeding. (05-write-a-contract)
- **Client questions a long completion-date buffer on a short job** → "Listen, you never know what could happen. Something will happen, God forbid, and we don't want to [breach]." Frame the buffer as protection, not a real estimate. (05-write-a-contract)
- **Elderly client (62+) living alone, no family present** → ask "Has anybody helped you make the decision?" If no, have them sign the standard line AND hand-write on a second line: "I am the only decision maker." Protects the contractor from later family complaints. (05-write-a-contract)
- **Emergency job needs to start before the legal 3-day cancel window** → have them sign a specific waiver of the 3-day right to cancel, on which they write WHY they're waiving it. (05-write-a-contract)
- **Insulation cost objection** → reframe as "a very, very easy thing to sell" since it costs little relative to the energy-efficiency savings; if hesitant about a higher R-value (e.g., R38), note the upgrade is only cents/sq ft more than a lower rating. (04.13-insulation)
- **ADU: "I want this as cheap as possible"** → understand it's not about being cheap personally — the client is evaluating this strictly as an investment property; frame the pitch around ROI. **"Your competitor is 25% cheaper"** → reframe around time-to-income and hassle: a 2-person crew takes months longer, causing more neighbor friction/dust/disruption. **"The bid is too high"** → use the design-first structure: bid actual construction cost before submitting to city plan check, so scope can be adjusted before permits/fees are spent. (04.14-ADU-room-additions)
- **"Electrical is too complicated/expensive"** → use the client's own fear of complexity to your advantage — position yourself as the professional who removes it for them. **"I'm older, don't use much power, don't need a panel upgrade"** → explain the real fire risk (a visiting grandchild plugging in extra devices can overload an old 60-100 amp panel) and the potential 10-15% insurance discount from a full upgrade (never guaranteed). (04.15-electrical)
- **"Why replace my old pipes, they seem fine?"** → for galvanized water lines, ask if they ever see brownish tap water (that's the rust they're bathing in) and advise immediate replacement; for cast iron drain lines, describe what happens when they burst under the house ("it just smells so bad, like as if you have a dead body underneath your property"). **"Replacing the sewer line will destroy my yard"** → sell the trenchless method (small access pits + pulling a new pipe through the old one, no destructive trench). **"Tankless is too expensive" / "tankless takes forever to get hot water"** → reframe around unlimited hot water and energy savings, and sell the $300-500 circulating-pump add-on to fix delivery lag. (04.16-plumbing)
- **"Foundation repair sounds too complicated/expensive"** → despite sounding complex, reframe it as "one of the most easiest things to sell" and highly profitable; build trust by learning and using real technical terminology (rebar, PSI, piers, T-straps) so you read as a credible expert. (04.17-foundation)

## 7. Close & Contract

- Give the client the anchor price (4-4.5x cost) then the real price (3-3.5x cost), wait 10 minutes, then deploy the 6 closing tools as needed. (06-the-soup; 07-closing)
- **Contract wording — salesman never puts personal info in the contractor-info block** ("You're not the contractor; you're the salesman") — personal name/phone only goes at the bottom. (05-write-a-contract)
- List all owners by name (husband + wife; note a co-signing adult child in parentheses). Get 2-3 phone numbers plus email — "get as much information as possible" so you can reach them later if a problem arises. (05-write-a-contract)
- **Payment milestone wording**: first milestone = "Upon start of job and delivery of some materials"; final milestone = "Upon job completion." (05-write-a-contract)
- **Down payment cap (the law)**: $1,000 or 10% of contract price, whichever is LESS. On big jobs this caps you at $1,000; on small jobs (<$10K) you can take 10%. (05-write-a-contract)
- **Final-payment sizing**: never leave a large chunk on the table for the end — e.g., on a $450K job the last payment should be only ~$20K (about 4-5%), not a big percentage. (05-write-a-contract)
- **Completion-date buffer**: 1-2 day job → promise within 1 month; 1-month job → promise within 6 months; 6-month-to-1-year job → promise within 2 years. (05-write-a-contract)
- **Paint/finish matching clause**: contract states "will match up to 85%" of paint — never promise 100%, so you always over-deliver. (04.05-painting)
- **Wood-damage contingency clause**: base price covers "up to two to three pieces of wood" — protects you to charge a change order if more rot/damage is found. Same pattern applies to roofing (2-3 pieces of plywood) and bathrooms (2-3 pieces of framing). (04.05-painting; 04.11-roofing; 04.12-bathroom)
- **Materials allowance caps** as margin protection on a "materials included" bathroom contract: tile up to $3/sq ft, tub up to $250, showerhead up to $150, standard toilet up to $150-200 — an imported/designer material choice above the cap becomes a change order, not an absorbed cost. (04.12-bathroom)
- **Dimension/spec-lock clause**: write exact dimensions into the contract (e.g., pool design-tile depth "4 inches") so any later upsize is a change order, not scope creep you eat. (04.08-pool-jacuzzi)
- **Permit self-handling liability shield**: if the client wants to skip engineering/permits themselves on a structure that requires them (e.g., a deck over 3-3.5 ft), write on the contract: "Owner will provide engineer plan and city permit plus call city inspector." (04.07-deck-patio)
- **Roof-touching disclaimer**: if installing a skylight (or any partial work) on an old roof you aren't fully replacing, get it in writing that you are not warranting the existing roof — otherwise you own any later leak. (04.10-windows)
- **Tile-color-variation disclosure**: on a roof tile repair/partial-reroof, write in writing that ~15% of old tiles will break during removal and that sun-faded originals may not perfectly color-match new tile. (04.11-roofing)
- Mark all required disclosure checkboxes (Home Improvement Contract, Arbitration Notice, 3-Day Cancellation Notice, Liability/Insurance Disclosure, Workers' Comp Insurance, License copy, Mechanic's Lien Warning) and collect all mandatory initials/signatures before leaving. (05-write-a-contract)

## 8. Job Kickoff (Opening the Job)

- **Three-business-day legal wait**: a job legally cannot open until 3 business days after signing, unless the client separately signs a Right-to-Start waiver. (11-open-a-job)
- **Day-of-sale recording**: shoot a scope video/photos immediately after closing and send it to the office, project managers, and the subcontractor so everyone shares the same scope understanding; take before photos for company records. (11-open-a-job)
- **Stagger subcontractor bids**: schedule 2-3 subs to bid the same morning in ~1-hour windows, best sub first — expect one no-show out of three, which is why you never schedule just one. Let any bid that comes in way over budget go immediately. (11-open-a-job)
- **Write sub scope in extreme detail**: every task a subcontractor owns must be written down explicitly (e.g., who pays to replace a single damaged piece of wood behind a shower) — omitted detail is exactly what causes later payment disputes. (11-open-a-job)
- **Client introductions**: personally introduce the chosen subcontractor to the client as your "foreman"/"company worker," introduce any on-site project manager, and have that PM hand over a business card/phone number directly. (11-open-a-job)
- **Protect the property day one**: floor paper + masking before any tool is used — a spotless, punctual first day buys forgiveness for problems that surface later in the job. (11-open-a-job)
- **Site-visit cadence**: the project lead must show up at least once every 2-3 days. (11-open-a-job)

> **Compliance flag — "spiking the job" (11-open-a-job):** the source video describes intentionally starting partial work during the legal 3-day cancellation window (e.g., pulling kitchen doors "for measurements," touching countertops/carpet/flooring/windows) to discourage the client from exercising their cancellation right. Josh states this himself is **"not 100% legal."** This is documented here because the training says it — it is flagged as a legal/compliance risk, not endorsed or recommended for use. The 3-day right to cancel exists specifically to protect consumers from this kind of pressure.

## 9. Upsell / Referral

**Needs vs. luxury framing**: "need" projects (active leaks, fire-hazard panels, structural/foundation issues, roofing-as-damage-prevention) sell fast with real urgency; "luxury/pleasure" projects (kitchens, bathrooms, flooring, windows) need more selling and no natural urgency unless tied to an active problem. (12-upsell-the-client)

- **The Multiplier Rule**: target an upsell that's a minimum of **2x-2.5x** the size of the original sale — go bigger on the follow-on sale, never chase progressively smaller add-ons. (12-upsell-the-client)
- **Contract Separatist Rule**: never bundle an upsell into the original contract and never rewrite the original to merge them — keep them as physically separate "Contract Number One" / "Contract Number Two" documents to avoid sticker shock (a $50K kitchen + $30K roof reading as one scary $80K contract). If a client later claims confusion about what's included, physically hold up both separate contracts side by side to end the objection without an argument. (12-upsell-the-client)
- **Cash-deposit anchor**: even on a financed original job, always collect the deposit (e.g., $1,000) in cash/check — this pulls the client out of the "financing box" mentally and primes them to pay cash on the upsell too. (12-upsell-the-client)
- **Four upsell approaches**: (1) *Day-After Preparation* — plant a seed on visit one, return 1-2 days later to formally pitch; (2) *Subcontractor Hand-off* — your sub spots/photographs a hidden problem while on the original job, and you must follow up same-day or next-day (a week is too late); (3) *On-the-Spot Presentation* — treat the referral like a brand-new lead, run a full 2-3 hour presentation and close immediately; (4) *Free Gift Upfront* — give away a relevant freebie (recessed lights, a fan, a faucet) while closing the original contract, which gets your specialized crew inside the home's utility systems where they spot the next job. (12-upsell-the-client)
- **The "Magic Wand" discovery script**: "If I had a magic wand and I could give you anything you want, what would be the next project you wish you could do?" — use this to surface the next upsell target. (12-upsell-the-client)
- **Objection — "I don't have the money" on an upsell**: read it as reluctance, not literal inability; if the client has owned the home 20+ years, assume access to at least $50K-$60K. **"They only got approved for $X in financing"**: bad credit ≠ zero money — the cash-deposit anchor above is what breaks this pattern. **"I want to wait 2 years"**: counter with immediate urgency via a nearby-neighbor name-drop ("Funny you mention the bathroom — we're doing Mrs. Weiner's right now..."), then let FOMO sit. (12-upsell-the-client)
- **The relationship principle**: remodeling is an emotional relationship — "the client is essentially married to you during construction." Keep small spontaneous gifts in your car. Summary line: **"You give them a $20 flower, they give you a $20,000 bathroom. That's how it works."** (12-upsell-the-client)
- **Subcontractor-negotiation sequencing**: always pitch and close a full-home upsell (e.g., whole-house paint) with the CLIENT before locking in a fixed price with your own subcontractor — you can always negotiate the sub down to just patchwork if the client declines, but not the reverse. (04.05-painting)
- **Referral-upsell math example**: if a sub is already charging $2,500 for patchwork, offer them an extra $3,000 ($5,500 total) to also take the full-home paint job — turns a small patch job into a much larger sale. (04.05-painting)
- **Steer clients toward the higher-margin/faster-approval option when it also serves them better**: e.g., recommend a patio enclosure over a full room addition when timeline/budget is tight — enclosure takes under a month and doesn't raise property taxes, vs. a room addition which can take nearly a year and does raise taxes. (04.07-deck-patio)
- **Avoiding price shock on a big electrical rewire**: sell the core rewiring + panel on the initial contract first. Once walls are open, walk the client through the house and pitch visual upgrades in the moment ("I think it's going to look so much nicer if you had six recessed lights..."), then close a SEPARATE second contract for decorative lighting/switches/extra outlets — same Contract Separatist logic as above. The open-wall patching process is also a natural upsell into a full interior/exterior repaint. (04.15-electrical)

## 10. Cancellation / Save Calls

**Core rule**: almost every cancellation reason (vacation, money, "ask the kids," etc.) is really the same price objection as everywhere else in this playbook — the fix is to get face-to-face fast and re-sell the value of what they already agreed to. (10-cancellation; same principle as 09-top-10-excuses)

- **The 24-hour rule**: meet a canceling client face-to-face the same day or within 24 hours. Wait 48 hours or 2-3 days and the job is completely, unrecoverably lost. (10-cancellation)
- **Re-anchoring**: a client's mental price anchor can drift down after signing (e.g., a signed $55,000 kitchen mentally resets to $30,000) — your job on a save call is to reprogram them back UP to the signed value, not negotiate down to their new lower number. (10-cancellation)
- **Cost-of-delay leverage**: warn a stalling client that waiting 5 years turns today's $55,000 kitchen into a $65,000-$75,000 kitchen — same "future cost of waiting" framing used in 04.11-roofing's $25K-now-vs-$50K-later close and 12-upsell-the-client's urgency plays. (10-cancellation)
- **Force the face-to-face**: if the client insists on canceling by phone, tell them company policy requires an in-person cancellation form AND that you must physically hand back their deposit check — this is your legitimate reason to get back in the house. Always take physical checks, even at the end, to reinforce that paper (not verbal agreement) is what's real. (10-cancellation)
- **Discounting discipline**: never discount immediately at the door. If money is the real issue, first introduce a complication or a call to a supervisor/authority figure — only then offer a calculated discount ($500-$2,000). The discount must look earned, not automatic. (10-cancellation)
- **Situational scripts**: *vacation objection* → reassure the project timeline flexes around their trip, no need to cancel, just reschedule. *"No funds" objection* → meet in person, verify it's real, loop in a manager for backup authority before discounting. *Kid says "get another estimate"* → offer to get the child on the phone directly and walk them through the premium materials/scope yourself (named example: Caesarstone, custom cabinets, dedicated electrical, plumbing replacement) to sell the price point straight to the objector. *Refuses to meet* → use the mandatory in-person deposit-return/cancellation-form requirement to force the meeting. (10-cancellation)
- **Why this matters**: successfully saving 2-3 out of every 10 cancellation attempts is worth an extra $20,000-$30,000/year to a salesperson. (10-cancellation)

## 11. Post-Sale / Legal Protection

- **Mechanic's lien timeline**: file within 90 days of completion if unpaid; then 90 days (3 months) after filing to enforce via court/arbitration. (05-write-a-contract)
- **Insurance/bond talking points for client trust**: general liability typically covers $500K-$2M in home damage; bonds typically cover $10K-$15K if the company goes bankrupt or vanishes mid-job; Workers' Comp absorbs medical liability if a worker is hurt on the property (protects the homeowner from that liability). (05-write-a-contract)
- **Asbestos/GC licensing liability**: a GC legally cannot remove asbestos themselves — must hire a certified specialist and keep a copy of the certificate of removal on file, which is what protects the contractor if a claim surfaces later. Cite the real-world cautionary example: a contractor sued for $170K (settled for $70K) on a $15K job for skipping the asbestos test. (04.03-asbestos-mold)
