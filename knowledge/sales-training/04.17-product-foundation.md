# Product Module — Foundation

Source: "Foundation.mp4" (Gemini video-analysis panel, Google Drive) + whiteboard "10_Foundation WB.jpg"

## Products & materials

Four main new-foundation types plus two repair methods:

- **Crawl space / raised foundation**: elevated off the ground — a cement belt dug into the ground with rebar and poured concrete, sticking 6 inches to 3 feet above grade; uses wooden beams, 4x4 posts, and concrete pillars/piers for support. Under-house access needs a minimum **16"x24"** opening from the exterior, or **18"x24"** minimum from an interior closet/location. Pros: easy access to plumbing/underside; "can go with the flow" in an earthquake rather than cracking instantly. Cons: if not bolted correctly, a strong earthquake can make the home literally slip off its foundation. Matches whiteboard's "Crawlspace/Raised Foundation" labeled cutaway (Cripple Wall, Crawlspace Access 18"x24" or 16"x24", Bolt).
- **Concrete slab foundation**: flat concrete poured over forms/rebar/plumbing/electrical with no crawl space. Pros: highly sturdy. Cons: earthquakes are felt "a lot harder" — a strong quake can crack the slab in two, bursting gas/plumbing lines running through it.
- **Cement basement foundation**: acts as both the home's foundation and a retaining wall for the surrounding dug-out earth; base foundation poured at bottom with rebar extending up into wooden forms; uses larger rebar and higher-density, high-PSI concrete (e.g., 4,500 PSI). Pros: extra living space/shelter under the home. Cons: most expensive foundation type to build, second only to caisson.
- **Caisson foundation**: deep holes (20-100 ft) dug via specialized truck or by hand. Pros: essential for securing heavy structures (high-rises, apartment buildings) or decks/patios/extensions built over steep 20-ft drops or cliffs. Cons: extremely expensive, especially when hand-digging is required due to limited backyard access. Matches whiteboard's "Caisson Foundation" rebar-cage sketch.
- **Bolting/re-bolting (repair)**: secures a raised house's wooden frame to its concrete foundation. Bolts placed roughly every 16 inches; T-shaped steel brackets (T-straps) secure posts/pillars. Matches whiteboard's "Bolting Foundation" detail (Bolt, Steel "T" Strap, 4x4 Post, Concrete Pier).
- **Cripple/"shiver" wall support**: on hillsides, the delicate framework between the foundation and floor level gets framing braces added to the 2x4s, bolted at the bottom to the concrete, with shear panels added for lateral bracing.
- **Underpinning (repair)**: used when a slab or raised foundation sinks from uncompacted soil, rain, or hillside sliding. Workers dig support holes beneath the existing foundation roughly every 4-5 feet. Pros: stops the home from sinking further / existing cracks from widening. Cons: expensive, highly labor-intensive, and does NOT automatically fix existing cracks. Matches whiteboard's "Underpinning Foundation" callout on the Slab Foundation sketch.

## Pricing rules

- **Underpinning, standard rate**: **$200-$400/linear ft**.
- **Floor leveling & framing repair (paired with underpinning)**: interior leveling, crack repair, framework modification, door reinstallation — **$250-$350/linear ft**.
- **Underpinning minimum job fee**: no contractor takes an underpinning job for less than **$2,000-$3,000 minimum**, even for just 5-6 linear feet.
- **Cripple wall bracing (hillside add-on)**: only **$500-$1,000 extra** if bundled into a re-bolting job already underway.
- **Caisson foundation, hand-dug backyard**: if machinery can't access the backyard and the hole must be dug entirely by hand, cost can exceed **$20,000**.
- **Delayed foundation jacking (neglect penalty)**: if a sinking house is left unaddressed and sinks 3-4 inches (up to a foot), simple underpinning is no longer sufficient — the home must be jacked up, a **2-3 month** process costing **over $150,000**. This is the core "act now" urgency lever for foundation sales.

## Process & permit rules

- **Pre-sale photo inspection**: salespeople must physically go into the crawl space and photograph the foundation, missing brackets, etc. BEFORE selling the job.
- **Permits & engineering plans**: a building permit and stamped engineering plans are mandatory for all foundation work; foundation contractors usually draft the basic plans themselves.
- **Building inspections**: city inspectors verify rebar layout before pour.
- **"The inspector loophole"**: Josh notes the practical reality that "usually the inspector won't crawl underneath the house because the city doesn't have the insurance" — flagging a real gap in enforcement rather than a rule to exploit.
- **Underpinning execution rule**: dig one section at a time — never dig out the entire foundation at once, or the house will collapse.

## What he says to do

- **Client complains of squeaky, hollow, or bouncy floors** → explain that under-house support pillars have likely rotted, rusted, or collapsed. Go underneath, photograph the fallen pillars or missing T-brackets, and use those photos to justify the repair.
- **"Foundation repair sounds too complicated/expensive"** → reframe: despite sounding complex, foundation repair is actually "one of the most easiest things to sell" and highly profitable.
- **Building trust/credibility on a technical topic** → study and use the terminology (rebar, PSI, piers, T-straps) — Josh's own framing: "the information I give you, you could take it and you could search it and build on it and be really" [knowledgeable — line cut off], suggesting reps should deepen their own technical fluency to sound like a credible expert in front of clients.
