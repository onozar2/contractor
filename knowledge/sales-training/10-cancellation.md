# Cancellation — How to Handle a Cancellation

Source: "FINAL 4k 52. How to Handle a Cancellation Export RED.mp4" (Gemini video-analysis panel, Google Drive folder "9. Cancellation"). No whiteboard exists for this module.

## The core rule

The real underlying objection for almost every cancellation is that **the price is too high** and the client is uncomfortable with it — they will offer other excuses (vacation, money, "ask the kids," etc.) but the fix is always the same: get face-to-face fast and re-sell the value of what they already agreed to.

## Pricing rules & numbers

- **The Kitchen Example / re-anchoring**: Josh uses a $55,000 kitchen contract as the running example. Even though the client signed at $55,000, their mental anchor can reset down to a lower number (e.g., $30,000) over time — your job on a save call is to "reprogram them" back to accepting the $55,000 value, not to negotiate down to their new lower anchor.
- **Cost of delaying**: warn a stalling client that waiting 5 years turns a $55,000 kitchen into a $65,000-$75,000 kitchen — inflation/cost increases as leverage against "I'll do it later."
- **Down payment as leverage**: always try to secure a down payment instead of $0 down. Example given: a $1,000 down payment on a roof job — that check becomes something you can bring back face-to-face as leverage in a save conversation.
- **Discounting rule**: never discount immediately when you first knock on the door to save the job. If money is the real issue, only offer a calculated discount ($500, $1,000, or $2,000 off) after first introducing a complication or a phone call to a supervisor/family-member "authority" — the discount has to look earned/escalated, not automatic.
- **Profit rule of thumb**: take the smaller win over losing the job entirely — "It's better off for you to make $5,000 than making nothing" [rest of the line cut off, but the point is: don't let pride about your original margin cost you the whole sale].
- **Annual value of saves**: successfully saving 2-3 out of every 10 cancellation attempts puts an extra $20,000-$30,000/year in a salesperson's pocket — this is the number he uses to justify why this skill matters.

## Process & paperwork rules

- **The 3-day legal window**: Josh acknowledges clients are legally entitled to cancel within the 3-day window ("I know it's within the three days...") — he doesn't tell you to fight a legitimate 3-day cancellation, only to try to save it while it's still in play.
- **In-person cancellation forms**: if a client insists on canceling by phone and refuses to meet, tell them company policy requires an in-person cancellation form AND that you need to physically hand back their deposit check — this becomes the legitimate reason to force a face-to-face meeting.
- **Always take physical checks**, even on what looks like a small/final amount — reinforces that paper checks (not verbal agreement) are what make the process real.
- **The 24-hour rule**: you must meet a canceling client face-to-face the same day or within 24 hours. Wait 48 hours or 2-3 days and the job is completely, unrecoverably lost.

## Situations & exact scripts

**Situation 1 — client wants to cancel because of an upcoming vacation (example: Hawaii next week):**
- Answer the phone immediately. Say: "Listen, if you need to go on a vacation to Hawaii, it's not a problem. You go on vacation for Hawaii..." [rest cut off — gist: reassure them the project timeline flexes around their trip, so canceling isn't necessary, just reschedule the start].

**Situation 2 — client says they talked to their bank and don't have the funds / don't want to spend the money:**
- Meet face-to-face immediately. Determine if it's a real money issue.
- Call your manager/a supervisor or father-figure for backup/authority before offering anything.
- Say: "Listen, they want to cancel but we ordered the materials, we put you on the calendar. It's a problem..." [rest cut off] — frame the cancellation as a mutual logistics problem, not just their decision.
- Only after that step, drop the price by $500-$2,000 (see Discounting rule above).

**Situation 3 — client says "I spoke to my son/kid, and he told me I need to get at least one more [estimate]":**
- Don't be scared of this — offer to get on the phone with the child directly.
- Say to the child: "Hey son, how you doing? What's going on? I'm sitting down with your dad, he wants to do his kitchen..." and then explicitly walk the child through the premium materials/scope (Caesarstone, custom cabinets, dedicated electrical, special lighting system, plumbing replacement) to justify the price directly to the person who's actually raising the objection.

**Situation 4 — client refuses to meet in person because they know you're just trying to save the job:**
- Force the meeting using the paperwork rule (see above). Say: "Listen, I need to go and sign you on a cancellation form. I need to give you back your deposit, you['ll need to sign for it]..." — the mandatory in-person deposit-return/cancellation-form requirement is your legitimate reason to get in front of them, which becomes your re-sell opportunity.
