# Product Module — Plumbing

Source: "35. Plumbing.mp4" (Gemini video-analysis panel, Google Drive) + whiteboard "35_Plumbing WB.jpg"

## Products & materials

**Outside the home**

- **Main line pipes (water supply)**: delivers pressurized water from the street, through the city meter, into the home. Buried 12-18 inches underground; because it's pressurized it does not need a slope.
  - Galvanized pipes: historical standard, but corrode after **15-20 years**, causing brown/rusty tap water.
  - Copper (modern standard) — expensive and a theft/vandalism target on vacant properties. Type M: thin, used for quick in-home repairs. Type L: thicker, more expensive, easily lasts **20-30 years** — the standard for main lines. Matches whiteboard's "Incoming — Galvanize to Copper" callout.
- **Sewer lines & drain pipes**: carry waste out to the city sewer; work on gravity, so they must be sloped (unlike pressurized supply lines). Trenching must dig deeper the farther the property is from the city line, **3-8 feet** deep.
  - Clay pipes: historical, crack easily (especially from tree roots).
  - Cast iron: used in older homes' indoor drains; rusts/corrodes after 20 years, leading to burst pipes and waste pooling under the house.
  - ABS: modern black/white plastic/PVC pipe — flexible, won't corrode, lasts **30-40 years**. Matches whiteboard's "Outgoing — Cast Iron to ABS."
- **Gas lines**: feed stoves, tankless water heaters, etc. If a property lacks a gas line, only the CITY (not a private plumber) can run the connection from the street to the meter; a plumber picks up from there.
- **Earthquake gas shut-off valve**: automatic safety device — an internal piston with a small ball that rolls and physically blocks the gas line during seismic activity over magnitude 4.5. Matches whiteboard's labeled "Earthquake Gas Shut Off Valve" callout exactly.
- **Septic tanks & soak-away/drainage fields**: on-site waste system for secluded, new, or hillside properties without city sewer access. Solid waste sinks in the tank; liquid rises and drains through a secondary line into a soak-away field — perforated pipes laid on a gravel bed (12 inches of gravel). Matches whiteboard's Septic Tank + Soakaway/Drainage Field diagram.
- **Water wells**: deep pipe (100-200 ft) driven into the ground for remote properties (e.g., Malibu mountain estates) to draw groundwater — zero city water bills, a strong selling point for large estates. Matches whiteboard's labeled "Well" sketch.
- **Water heaters**:
  - Traditional tank (35/45/50 gallon): heats a set volume; once empty, users wait 45 min-1 hr to reheat.
  - Tankless: wall-mounted, heats water instantly/infinitely via a high-power gas burner; saves electricity, but if placed far from fixtures, delivery to the tap can be slow.
  - Circulating pump: add-on accessory that continuously circulates hot water through the home's pipes so hot water is instant at every tap.

## Pricing rules

- **Permit fees**: **$150-$800**, varies by municipality/scale — quoting the city a lower declared project cost yields lower permit fees than a higher declared cost.
- **Exterior main line replacement**: **$2,000-$3,000** for a 20-30 ft run; **$3,000-$4,000** for a 50 ft run.
- **Exterior sewer line replacement (trenched)**: **$5,000-$8,000** for 20-30 ft; **$7,000-$10,000** for 50 ft (deeper excavation/labor).
- **Exterior sewer line (trenchless method)**: saves **$1,000-$3,000** vs. traditional trenching by skipping major excavation.
- **City gas line connection**: city charges **$800-$2,000** to run the line from city property to the home's meter.
- **Earthquake gas shut-off valve**: costs **$500-$1,000** at most to install, and can yield **$500-$1,000 in direct profit** as an easy upsell.
- **Septic tank pump-out maintenance**: **$500-$800** per service.
- **In-home "per-point" pricing formula**: every water in/out location = one point (e.g., a sink = 1 point for faucet + 1 for drain; a full bathroom with sink/toilet/shower = ~6 points).
  - Standard rate, raised foundation: **$400/point** (e.g., 6 points x $400 = $2,400).
  - Tight crawl space (2-3 ft vs. 5-6 ft): adds **20%-50%** to the price due to harder labor access.
  - Slab foundation, incoming water routed through attic: **+$50-$100/point** extra material cost.
  - Slab foundation drains (requires cutting flooring, diamond-sawing concrete, jackhammering): **$1,000-$2,500/point** — a full slab drain replacement can easily exceed **$15,000**.
- **Drywall/wall patching**: NOT included in standard plumbing bids — hiring a painter to patch/repaint costs **$1,000-$1,500**, and should be factored into the total bid presented to the client.
- **Minimum job fees**: plumbers charge a minimum of **$800** for crawl-space/permitted jobs; simple fixture hookups (e.g., connecting a pre-plumbed sink) cost **$300-$400**.
- **Water heaters**: traditional tank install **$800-$1,200** (including tank); tankless install **$3,000-$4,000**; circulating pump add-on **$300-$500**.

## Process & permit rules

- **City permits**: every plumbing job requires one. If plumbing is the ONLY work being done, the plumber (subcontractor) pulls the permit and should not charge extra labor for it (permit fees themselves are still separate). On a multi-trade remodel (plumbing + electrical + framing), the general contractor pulls the combined permit.
- **Permit contract language (critical)**: state explicitly who pays for and pulls permits. If the contractor pulls it but owner pays: "We will provide city permits, owner will pay city fees" (hand the client the city receipt for reimbursement). If the owner handles it entirely: "Owner will pull city permits."
- **No-patchwork clause**: plumbers do not patch walls after cutting into them — write into the contract: "It doesn't match 100%, it will match up to 85%" (same 85%-match pattern as painting/electrical), and factor the $1,000-$1,500 paint/patch cost into the total bid.
- **No flooring-restoration clause (slab jobs)**: standard plumbing bids don't include restoring flooring after jackhammering concrete — explain this to the client or upsell a separate flooring-restoration contract.
- **Septic maintenance rule**: pump out at least once a year; households with frequent guests or a small tank need it every 6 months.
- **Mandatory earthquake valves**: most cities legally require an earthquake gas shut-off valve on any qualifying construction/remodel project.
- **Tankless water heater install requirements**: the location needs three things — an exterior GFCI outlet, adequate gas line capacity, and [third requirement cut off].
- **Multi-unit placement**: for large estates/mansions, best practice is installing 2-3 tankless heaters positioned close to different fixture clusters rather than one central unit serving the whole house.

## What he says to do

- **"Why replace my old pipes, they seem fine?"** — galvanized water lines: ask if they ever see brownish water from the tap (that's the rust they're bathing in/drinking) and advise replacing ASAP. Cast iron sewer/drain lines: describe what happens when they burst — waste pools under the home — with the vivid line "it just smells so bad, like as if you have a dead body underneath your property" to convey urgency.
- **"Replacing the sewer line will destroy my yard/landscaping"** → sell the trenchless method: instead of a destructive trench, dig only small access pits and pull a new pipe through the old one hydraulically, saving the yard and often saving cost too.
- **"Tankless is too expensive vs. a standard tank"** → reframe around ongoing savings and unlimited hot water — a traditional tank runs out after 35-50 gallons and needs up to an hour to reheat, while tankless delivers instant, endless hot water while saving on energy.
- **"I heard tankless takes forever to reach the shower"** → validate the concern, then sell the $300-$500 circulating pump add-on — keeps hot water moving through the pipes so the tap delivers hot water instantly, eliminating the wait and the wasted water.
