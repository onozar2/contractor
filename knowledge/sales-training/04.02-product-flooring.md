# Product Module — Flooring

Source: "12. Flooring.mp4" (Gemini video-analysis panel, Google Drive, ~18 min runtime unconfirmed exact length) + whiteboard "12_Flooring WB.jpg"

## Products & materials

**Wood flooring** — installed on raised foundation over floor joists + plywood subfloor, or directly onto concrete. Tongue-and-groove installation (pieces slide snugly together).
- **Hardwood**: natural, thick. Can be sanded/refinished 10-20 times. Lasts 200-300 years. Types include cherry, walnut.
- **Engineered floor**: laminate + hardwood combo; top layer is real hardwood, refinishable 2-3 times only. Lasts 50-60 years max.
- **Laminate flooring**: cheapest wood-look type — laminated formica top over crushed wood core. Anti-scratch coating, but if it does scratch it cannot be refinished, must be replaced. Lasts 50-60 years but shows significant wear at 30-40 years.
- **Bamboo floor**: natural, solid, similar properties to hardwood — refinishable 10-20 times, lasts 200-300 years.

**Underlayment/trim**: Padding (buffer between subfloor and wood/vinyl planks) [unverified exact function detail cut off]. Molding installed with a gap near the wall for seasonal wood movement.

**Finish**: Clear coat (retains natural wood color) vs. Varnish (darkens/enriches color) — more varnish coats = darker.

**Vinyl / linoleum flooring**:
- Vinyl planks: manufactured PVC plastic flooring; modern versions look/feel like hardwood but are waterproof and highly scratch-resistant.
- Linoleum: natural-material flooring, similar look/purpose to vinyl. Comes as **Roll** (recommended — no seams) or **Tiles** (cheaper but not recommended — peel within 1-2 years).
- Filler used to patch holes/cracks in existing linoleum when it can't be fully removed.
- Whiteboard also lists "Linoleum Tile" as a distinct type alongside Vinyl Planks and Linoleum Roll.

**Carpet**: needs a tack strip (track strip) around room edges to hold it in place; installer uses a special tool to measure stretch so it isn't overstretched.
- Residential carpeting: thinner, cheaper, hides wear well in high-traffic settings.
- Industrial carpeting: ranges from basic warehouse-grade to high-end custom patterns for upscale apartments.

**Tile flooring**: installed over a concrete slab, or over cement board if on a plywood subfloor (plywood alone is never acceptable under tile — see rules below).
- Grout types: Seamless grout (tiles packed tight, minimal gap); Water-mixed grout (basic, NOT recommended — develops dark mildew); Sealer-mixed grout (liquid sealer added — resists moisture/mildew, easy to clean).
- Tile types named on whiteboard: Ceramic, Porcelain, Glass, Cement, Marble, Mosaic, Limestone, Granite, Travertine, Quarry, Metal, Resin, Flagstone, Saltillo, Terrazzo. Gemini flagged Ceramic/Porcelain as the primary options for standard home floors/walls, Glass/Mosaic typically used for kitchen backsplash texture, Flagstone as an exterior option.
- Coving: running vinyl/linoleum continuously up the base of the wall (diagrammed on whiteboard with a cabinet toe-kick).

## Pricing rules

- Hardwood materials: $7-$15/sq ft (also referenced elsewhere in the video as $7-$25/sq ft — [unverified] which range is the "real" one; note the discrepancy).
- Bamboo materials: ~$5-$7/sq ft.
- Refinishing labor: $2.00-$3.00 (or up to $3.50) per sq ft depending on region and whether varnish is used.
- Removing old linoleum layers: +$300-$500 depending on number of layers.
- Vinyl coving labor: +$1-$2 per linear foot.
- Vinyl plank materials: ~$3/sq ft (good quality).
- Vinyl plank labor: $1-$3/sq ft depending on area/project size.
- Carpet installation: $2-$5/sq ft.
- Industrial carpet (cheap): $1-$3/sq ft.
- Industrial carpet (expensive/custom): $5-$7+/sq ft.
- Flagstone materials: $7-$30/sq ft.
- Labor-scale rule: larger carpet project areas lower the per-sq-ft labor cost (economies of scale).

## Process & contract rules

- **Wood gap rule**: never install wood flooring completely flush to the wall — leave a gap for expansion (wet winters)/shrinkage (dry summers). Skipping this causes squeaky floors.
- **Refinishing machine rule**: stop the sander 6-7 inches from molding; pop out molding and hand-sand those edges plus areas around railings.
- **Dust clearance rule**: fully clean sanding dust off the floor before applying any stain.
- **Stain drying rule**: wait 5-6 hours between every coat of stain/varnish before applying the next.
- **Varnish change-order rule**: if a client picks varnish (darker) over clear coat, that's a change order — charge more (more coats needed).
- **Linoleum roll rule**: after gluing down a roll, run a heavy roller over it to spread glue fully, eliminate bubbles, ensure permanent stick.
- **Carpet timeline**: a full-house carpet install should take max 1-2 days.
- **Cement floating rule**: if the floor has a 1-2 inch slope, float (level) it with cement before laying flooring — and charge extra for this labor since it's significant work.
- **Tile subfloor barrier rule**: never put tile directly on plywood — plywood absorbs moisture and tiles pop out. Cement board is required as the moisture barrier/sealer.
- **Client-sourced materials rule**: if a client buys their own tile, instruct them to also buy the [liquid sealer/grout additive — sentence cut off in source, unverified exact wording].

## What he says to do

- Client wants darker wood finish → apply varnish coats; tell them more darkness = more coats = charge more.
- Existing linoleum damaged/multi-layered → fully remove and charge +$300-$500; if it can't be removed, fill holes/cracks with filler before covering.
- Industrial/commercial high-moisture area (e.g., a 7-Eleven-style bathroom) → cove the vinyl up the wall base for easy cleaning and wall protection; charge +$1-$2/linear ft for it.
- Client has dogs/small kids worried about floor damage → recommend vinyl planks (hardwood look, waterproof, scratch-resistant).
- Client wants tile but floor is sloped → diagnose the slope; if it's a major 1-2 inch slope, explain cement floating is required and charge extra.
- Client worried about ugly blackened grout lines (bathroom/backsplash) → steer away from water-mixed grout (attracts mildew); recommend sealer-mixed grout and pass the material upcharge to the client.
