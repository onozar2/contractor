# Product Module — Electrical

Source: "34. Electrical.mp4" (Gemini video-analysis panel, Google Drive) + whiteboard "34_Electrical WB.jpg"

## Products & materials

- **Panel boxes (amperage)**:
  - 60-100 amp: standard "back in the day" when homes used little electricity. Older/retired clients with few gadgets can still function on an 80-100 amp panel, but it's dangerous — a visiting grandchild plugging in extra devices can overload it and cause a fire.
  - 200 amp: the modern average-home standard; needed to run multiple high-demand appliances (HVAC, EV charger, etc.) simultaneously. Matches the whiteboard's "Electrical (main) Box/Panel — 200 AMP/400 AMP" callout.
  - 400 amp: historically commercial-only; cities now allow residential 400-amp panels under certain conditions.
- **60 amp disconnect switch**: dedicated box/switch to shut off an AC condenser or a steam-shower steamer; requires its own dedicated line to the main panel. Matches whiteboard's "60 AMP Disconnect Switch" label next to the AC condenser.
- **Sub panel**: feeds off the existing main panel to give a separate part of the house (e.g., a family member's living area) its own dedicated panel. Matches whiteboard "Subpanel" callout.
- **Solar inverter**: solar panels generate DC power, which runs to the inverter and converts to AC before feeding the home; excess power on sunny days pushes backward through the meter into the grid. Matches whiteboard's DC→Inverter→Battery AC→Meter flow diagram.
- **Wiring & outlets**:
  - Three-wire system: hot (usually black, carries incoming electricity), neutral, and ground.
  - Grounding: homes 40-50 years old often have no ground wire or a very thin one — modern grounding uses a copper rod (matches whiteboard's "Ground" + "Copper Rod" detail).
  - Interior GFCI: reset button, resets locally if water touches it (vs. older outlets that don't).
  - Exterior GFCI: same as interior but with a weatherproof cover.
  - 220-volt outlets: large sockets for high-power machines (stoves, ovens, washers).
  - Metal conduit: required to run wiring safely from the home to a detached garage.
- **EV charging station**: runs on a 240-volt line; a standard outlet CAN charge a car but takes 10-12 hours. Matches whiteboard's "Charging Station 240V, Level 1/2/3, cost $1,500-$3,000."
- **Tankless water heater**: exterior-mounted, requires an exterior GFCI outlet nearby.

## Pricing rules

- 200-amp panel install (with a nearby power pole): **$2,000-$3,000**.
- 400-amp panel install: **$3,000-$4,500** (more in some areas).

- Sub panel install: **$1,000-$1,500**.

- Standard home rewire, without panel: **$6,300-$13,000**.
- Patchwork & paint matching after a rewire: **$1,500-$2,500** (note: electrician pricing does NOT include wall patching — that's a separate line).
- GFCI outlet install: **$350-$450**.
- EV charging station with install: **$1,500-$3,000**.
- Tankless water heater: **$2,500-$3,500**.
- Solar panel detach & reinstall (for a roof replacement underneath): **$1,500-$2,500**.
- **Insurance discount lever**: home insurers often offer a **10-15% discount** once a home's entire electrical system is upgraded — never guarantee this, but mention that most insurers offer it.

## Process & permit rules

- **Double permits for a 400-amp upgrade**: requires one permit from the city building department AND one from the utility company.
- **Panel relocation & site inspection**: the utility company sends an inspector within **5-10 business days** to assess the panel's proximity to the power lines. If the panel is on the wrong side of the house relative to the lines, they place an orange sticker flagging relocation. Meeting the inspector on-site with your electrician often lets you negotiate the exact placement.
- **Pre-wiring walkthrough**: before starting a rewire, walk the property with the client and mark exactly where they want new switches/outlets/fixtures.
- **Wall openings**: expect to open **100-150 small holes** across a standard 1,200-1,500 sq ft home to run new wiring. For a delicate kitchen backsplash, run wires by cutting stucco holes from the exterior side instead of through the tile.
- **Stucco patching sequencing**: the inspector must sign off on rough wiring/patch prep BEFORE the final stucco coat goes on.
- **Final inspection prep**: the inspector checks that everything is patched and all light switches/outlets are functional — if not, the job can fail and trigger a reinspection fee.
- **Paint matching contract rule**: always write "paint matching up to 85%" in the contract — never promise 100%, so you can over-deliver (same rule as 04.05-painting).
- **City electrical code minimums**: standard bedroom needs at least 3 outlets (nearly one per wall); kitchen remodel needs dedicated lines run to the panel for major appliances; kitchen countertops need an outlet every 3 linear feet (matches 04.06-kitchen); an attic HVAC install needs a normal outlet and light fixture nearby.

## What he says to do

- **"Electrical is too complicated/expensive"** → use the client's own fear of complexity to your advantage — position yourself as the knowledgeable professional who removes that complexity for them; electrical work is highly profitable [rest of framing cut off].
- **"I'm older / don't use much electricity, don't need to upgrade my old 80-100 amp panel"** → explain the real fire hazard (a visiting grandchild plugging in extra devices can overload it); pitch the financial upside of a full upgrade (potential 10-15% insurance discount, never guaranteed); and note that many insurers reduce or deny claims after a fire traced to outdated, un-upgraded electrical.
- **"This is going to create too much dust/dirt"** → be upfront that opening 100+ holes will be dusty, then offer a professional cleaning service once the job completes (same pattern as painting/kitchen/bathroom cleanup lines).
- **"Why are there corrections/failed items on my inspection?"** → normalize it from the start of the sales process: "Electrical work is very complicated, and the codes change every year" — this keeps trust intact when a minor correction shows up.
- **Avoiding price shock on a big rewire**: don't hit the client with one giant bundled price. Sell the core rewiring + panel on the initial contract first. Once walls are open, walk the client through the house and pitch visual upgrades in the moment ("I think it's going to look so much nicer if you had six recessed lights in your living room...") — close a SECOND contract for decorative lighting/switches/extra outlets. The open-wall patching process is also a natural upsell moment into a full interior/exterior repaint.
