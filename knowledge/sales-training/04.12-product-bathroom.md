# Product Module — Bathroom

Source: "29. Bathroom Export RED.mp4" (Gemini video-analysis panel, Google Drive) + whiteboard "29_BATHROOM WB.jpg"

## Products & materials

**Structure/rough-in**:
- 2x4s: construct the shower pan framework.
- Drain pipes: older = cast iron; modern = ABS or PVC.
- Copper pipes: required by code to replace old galvanized water lines before walls are closed up.
- Shower valve: old two-valve (separate hot/cold) setups are a burn hazard; modern code requires a single-valve system transitioning sequentially cold→hot. New controllers/handles must match the valve.
- Recessed/can lights: must be rated waterproof inside a shower area.
- GFCI outlets: required by code anywhere water is present — reset locally without killing power to the whole house. Explicitly required if a towel warmer is installed.
- Exhaust vents: required by code in every bathroom; omitting one causes ceiling mold and grout mildew. Requires routing a pipe to the roof or side of the house.

**Waterproofing (two methods)**:
- Black paper + chicken wire: the traditional method — paper over the frame, chicken wire cover, inspection, then a mortar scratch coat. Speaker says this seals the wall "100% without any gaps." Matches the whiteboard's labeled Black Paper/Chicken Wire/Frame Work diagram exactly.
- Green board / Hardy Backer (cement board): pre-made waterproof concrete board that screws into framing — faster/easier, favored by most contractors, though it always leaves small seam gaps needing extra sealing.
- Hot mop: sealing material poured over the sloped concrete shower pan base — matches whiteboard's "Shower Pan (Hot Mop)" label.
- Metal flashing + silicone/cold mop: applied around exterior roof vent penetrations for a watertight seal.

**Tile & finishes**:
- Tile/grout: must be perfectly aligned (no sticking-out edges). Modern trend is "groutless" (minimal spacing) to reduce re-grouting/mold. If grout is used, mix with a sealer (not water) to resist mold.
- Mosaic/glass tile: for accent areas like a "waterfall" design feature — matches the whiteboard's walk-in shower waterfall design liner.
- Shower floor tile/pebbles: small tiles or pebbles for better grip and drainage on the pan floor.
- Bench tile: should NOT use small tiles — larger format for comfort/stability.
- Tile molding/trim: traditional = curved ceramic; modern = clean-edged metal.

**Fixtures**:
- Jacuzzi tubs: must have an integrated side-access panel for servicing the water pumps.
- Linear/hidden drains: modern alternative to square/circular drains — hidden drains let tile run to the edge with a subtle 1" gap for runoff.
- Security bars: safety bars for stability, especially recommended for elderly clients — whiteboard callout on the walk-in shower.
- Frameless glass shower doors: high-end enclosures; for steam showers, glass must seal floor-to-ceiling (whiteboard: "Frameless Shower Door").
- Vanities — three types: Custom (100% bespoke, expensive), Semi-Custom (pre-sized boxes assembled like Lego from a warehouse catalog), Online/Pre-set (factory-finished, complete with countertop/sink/sometimes faucet).
- Toilets: standard up to luxury smart models (e.g., TOTO) with self-cleaning systems.
- Medicine cabinets: sized at 16" to fit standard 16"-on-center wall studs; larger 32" units require cutting/widening the structural framing. Don't install on top of drywall or use as the sole vanity mirror.
- Blue painter's tape: used at final walkthrough to mark cosmetic touch-up spots (same technique as 04.06-kitchen).

## Pricing rules

- **Wood replacement allowance**: 2-3 pieces of rotted framing wood found during demo are covered in the base price; extensive rot/termite damage/black mold triggers extra charges (same pattern as roofing and painting).
- Shower pan curb height: exactly **4 inches** above floor level.
- Shower head height: raise **~1 foot** when updating older plumbing (older installs are commonly too low).
- Water test: hot-mop shower pans filled with **2 inches of water**, left overnight to test for leaks.
- **Overall project pricing**: basic remodels **$15,000-$20,000**; high-end artistic bathrooms **$30,000-$35,000**.
- Frameless shower doors: standard glass **$1,000-$2,000**; full steam-shower enclosure up to **$5,000**.
- Online prefab vanities: **$500-$5,000** (double-sink at the top end).
- Toilets: standard **$150-$250**; smart luxury toilets up to **$10,000**.
- Wall stud spacing: **exactly 16 inches** apart by code.
- **Materials allowance caps** (for "all-included" contracts):
  - Tile: up to **$3.00/sq ft**.
  - Bathtub: up to **$250**.
  - Shower head: up to **$150**.
  - Standard toilet: up to **$150-$200**.
- **Labor-only pricing strategy**: sample structure prices labor + rough materials at **$29,000**, leaving finished materials to be selected/paid separately by the client.
- **Imported materials risk**: a client choosing specialized imported tile (e.g., from Italy) at $25+/sq ft can blow past the tile allowance — the allowance cap in the contract is what protects you.

## Process & permit rules

- **Demolition protections**: crews mask/protect adjacent areas to limit dust; demo proceeds carefully down to structural framing without hitting active water lines.
- **Plumbing code & scope limit**: upgrades are legally confined to the immediate wall work area; the contract should explicitly state that plumbing underneath the home or in distant walls is excluded. Old galvanized lines must be replaced with copper by code before walls are sealed.
- **Sequential inspections** (5 stages):
  1. In-wall plumbing/electrical — before insulation/wallboard closes up.
  2. Waterproofing — inspector reviews the lath layer (black paper + chicken wire) before tile.
  3. Tub/Jacuzzi mechanical — confirms drain/pump hookups before surfaces are finished.
  4. Shower pan water test — 2" water fill, inspector marks the line, returns 24 hours later to confirm no leak.
  5. Final inspection — full code compliance once the remodel is complete.
- **Material logistics rule**: construction should ideally not start until 100% of the client's selected finished materials are physically on-site — letting a client order materials piecemeal mid-job routinely delays the project by "two to three weeks."
- **Contractual writing rule**: every allowance, restriction, exclusion, and spec must be documented in writing. Direct quote: "Put all the thinking aside, put everything down in writing is super, super [important]."

## What he says to do

- Property gets damaged during demolition → reassure upfront: "Don't worry, we'll fix it, we'll take care of it for you" — commit to restoring any accidental damage to beautiful condition.
- Unexpected rotted framing/termites/mold found in open walls → cover 2-3 pieces of wood in the base price, but protect yourself with an explicit contract clause triggering a change order beyond that.
- Upgrading old dangerous two-handle valves to single-handle → explain the safety/code requirement and charge extra for the mandatory upgrade.
- Bathroom lacks an exhaust vent → tell the client it's mandatory by code, explain the labor involved (routing a pipe through roof/siding, weatherproof sealing), and advise them to run the vent continuously to prevent mildew/mold.
- Meticulous/picky client on shower drain choice → have the client pick and purchase the drain directly (or go with them) — avoids absorbing the cost of a $200-$300 designer drain and protects you from a mismatch dispute.
- Vanity/toilet selection disputes → have the client source and purchase their own toilet/vanity; if a toilet is promised in the contract, cap it in writing (e.g., "up to $150 or $200").
- Underbid by a cheap competitor (e.g., $20,000 vs. your $35,000) → shift the conversation away from basic labor and toward artistic bathroom design — show video portfolios and photo lookbooks of finished luxury projects, and pitch the final result as a "piece of art," not a utility room.
