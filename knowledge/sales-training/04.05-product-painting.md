# Product Module — Painting

Source: "29. Painting.mp4" (Gemini video-analysis panel, Google Drive) + whiteboard "30_Painting WB.jpg"

## Products & materials

**Exterior / stucco assembly** (matches whiteboard "Exterior Paint" diagram exactly):
- Wood framing (2x4s), spaced ~16 inches apart, forms the wall structure.
- Plywood installed over the framing.
- Black paper stapled to the plywood (used under both stucco and wood siding installs).
- Chicken wire stapled on top of the black paper as the base for stucco.
- **3 stucco coats** (whiteboard: "3 Coats of Stucco"):
  1. Scratch coat — 3/8" thick, 5-7 day cure time.
  2. Brown coat — 3/8" thick, 5-7 day dry time.
  3. Finish/color coat — 1/8" thick, mixed with the home's final color; can be Spanish or smooth finish.
- Special textile filler: a silicone-fiberglass-based flexible plaster (NOT concrete-based) used to open up and fill cracks before recoating.
- Wood shake siding, eaves, fascia board, wood shutters — all labeled on the whiteboard as exterior trim/siding elements.
- "Trim paint" is defined on the whiteboard as: painting all wood work around the home (shutters, fascia, eaves, framing) — distinct from stucco/siding paint.

**Prep and finish materials**:
- Primer: blocks old color and helps paint adhere. Skipping it drops exterior paint lifespan to 10-15 years; a good primer extends it [exact extended lifespan number cut off in source].
- Wood sealer: applied after sanding/replacing wood, seals and protects from rain and the elements.

**Interior**:
- Venetian plaster: an actual plaster (not paint), applied in 2-3 coats with 4-5 hour dry time between coats; ranges from rough to glossy finish.
- Drywall + drywall tape: tape covers seams/screw holes for a smooth surface.
- Level 5 / skim coat: a smooth-plastering process where plaster is applied to the whole wall 2-3 times (used when a client wants perfectly smooth walls instead of visible texture).
- Acoustic/popcorn ceiling: flagged on the whiteboard as requiring asbestos inspection before touching (cross-reference: see 04.03 Asbestos-Mold module).
- Electrical panel/box: patched around when doing interior work (whiteboard shows this explicitly next to the drywall diagram).

## Pricing rules

- Smooth stucco finish (vs. Spanish/textured): +$50 per 100 sq ft ("big square").
- Perimeter trench for stucco painting: must be dug 3" wide x 6" deep (matches whiteboard's "3x6 Trench" label).
- Trim painting (shutters, framing, fascia, eaves): base cost **$2,000-$3,000**.
- Extensive fascia/eaves wood replacement: +$1,000 on top of trim painting cost.
- Wood siding installation: **$250-$700 per big square** (100 sq ft), depending on materials.
- Scaffolding rental: **$1,500-$2,000/month** for a single wall (incl. setup); **$3,000-$4,000** to outfit an entire house.
- Venetian plaster: **$1,000-$2,000** for one wall; **$3,000-$5,000** for a whole room.
- Patchwork after other trades:
  - Plumbing patchwork: **$1,000-$1,500** (fewer holes).
  - Electrical rewire patchwork: **$1,500-$2,500** (80-90% more holes than plumbing).
- **Referral paint upsell formula**: if a painter is charging $2,500 for rewire patchwork, offer them an extra $3,000 ($5,500 total) to also paint the whole home — [exact mechanism/rationale for the upsell cut off, but the core math is a $3,000 upsell-for-referral trade].
- Bedroom painting baseline: **$500-$800** per room.
- Wallpaper removal: +$300-$500 on top of the bedroom baseline.
- Door refinishing: **$500-$1,500** depending on door size/condition.
- Asbestos testing: **$50-$400** depending on area (consistent with the 04.03 Asbestos-Mold module's $50-$100 "per sample" figure — this may reflect a wider per-project range vs. per-sample cost).
- Asbestos removal (local/limited): **$500-$1,500**.
- Full acoustic ceiling removal + reinstall (sheetrock, insulation, new drywall): **$10,000+**.

## Process & contract rules

- **Stucco cure timing**: never rush the 5-7 day drying windows between scratch and brown coats.
- **Sanding contract language**: never write "sand wood" in a contract — write "sand wood as needed" to avoid a fixed-scope trap.
- **Wood damage limit rule**: always write into the contract that the base price covers "up to two to three pieces of wood" — if more rot/termite/mildew damage is found under old paint, this protects the contractor to charge a change order.
- **Trim replacement spec**: contracts must specify the exact linear footage of wood being replaced on fascia boards, etc. — not a vague "replace as needed."
- **Job volume rule**: the bigger the wood siding job (whole house vs. just the front), the cheaper the per-square-foot rate (economy of scale, same principle as the flooring module's carpet pricing).
- **Interior damage liability rule**: always include a clause that the contractor isn't responsible for breaking delicate items [exact wording cut off — general liability disclaimer for items near the work area].
- **Scope exclusion rule**: explicitly tell the client (and note in the scope) that painting crown molding, door frames, [and other specific trim items — list cut off] is excluded unless priced separately.
- **Paint matching rule**: write into the contract "will match up to 85%" of the paint — never promise 100% matching, so you always over-deliver rather than under-deliver.
- **Subcontractor negotiation rule**: always pitch and close the full-home painting upsell to the client BEFORE locking in a fixed price with your painter/subcontractor — this gives you room to negotiate the sub down to just patchwork pricing if the client declines the full-home upsell.
- **Wallpaper sourcing rule**: never take responsibility for choosing/providing wallpaper samples — require the client to purchase and order their own wallpaper (online or delivered to their home); the contractor only installs it.
- **Acoustic ceiling asbestos rule**: before touching an acoustic ceiling, ask the client when the house was built and run an asbestos test — do not just start scraping.

## What he says to do

- Client thinks a whole-house paint job takes 1-2 days (because they've seen it done fast before) → explain a professional job takes 2-3 weeks due to proper prep (trenching, high-pressure water blasting to clean surfaces, etc.); warn that skipping this prep is what let the "1-2 day" jobs happen and that it produces a worse result.
- Trees are touching the home's exterior walls → tell the client upfront: "We'll cut some of the trees so you can paint" — get this agreed before starting, not after.
- Client wants to convert a garage into living space with drywall → screw drywall to framing, tape the seams/screw holes; if the client wants perfectly smooth walls (no visible texture), explain that requires a Level 5 skim coat (extra cost/process).
- Client wants to refinish a door that's already painted → explain this is not possible — you cannot properly refinish (strip/restain) a painted door. (Note: the reverse works — a refinished/stained door CAN be painted later, just not the other way around.)
- Client wants an acoustic ceiling removed but wants to avoid the cost/disruption of full removal → if the ceiling has a concrete base, offer to leave it intact and encapsulate/skim-coat over it instead, which avoids the asbestos testing/removal cost and the scraping mess.
