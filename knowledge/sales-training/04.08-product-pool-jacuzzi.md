# Product Module — Pool & Jacuzzi

Source: "22. Pool-Jacuzzi.mp4" (Gemini video-analysis panel, Google Drive) + whiteboard "22_Pool & Jacuzzi WB.jpg"

## Products & materials

**Structure/build**:
- Concrete/cement mix: must match the minimum spec on the engineering plans — ranges from 2500 PSI to 4500 PSI. Must be delivered by cement truck, never mixed by hand in the backyard.
- Rebar: installed on pool floors and walls before concrete is poured.
- Wooden forms: hold poured concrete in place while curing, removed afterward.
- Plumbing pipes: run before concrete is poured — drain lines and return plumbing for jets. Blue lines/pipes = water exiting the pool toward equipment; red lines/pipes = water returning to the pool (whiteboard convention).

**Finishes** (whiteboard "Pool Plaster" list matches Gemini exactly):
- Normal plaster: feels like smooth stucco, applied ~1/2" thick, customizable colors, lasts **6-8 years**.
- Pebble Tech (PebbleTec): epoxy-like textured finish, much more durable than plaster, lasts **10-15 years**.
- Full tiling finish: tiles the entire pool interior instead of plastering — best longevity (30-40+ years) but highly labor-intensive and expensive.
- Whiteboard's third option is labeled "Tile/Mosaic," matching the full-tiling-finish description.

**Coping, tile, and edges**:
- Swimming pool coping: heavy stone/tile perimeter edge, pieces ~2-3" thick, 1-2 feet long, ~1 foot wide.
- Design tile: installed just below coping around the upper perimeter — usually glass-look tile in blue, green, other colors. Typically covers 4-6" of depth from the top, though some clients request up to 10"+. Mosaic tile flagged as potentially very expensive.

**Equipment**:
- Skimmer: acts as an integrated vacuum for the upper water layer, catching leaves/bees/dead insects while the pump runs.
- Filter: receives water from the pump to remove dust/debris; needs changing every few months.
- Heater: diverts and warms water before it returns via the return jets — essential for jacuzzis, not always standard on regular pools.
- Vacuum port: dedicated underwater connection for pool-cleaning robots; draws water through pump/filter to clean below the surface.
- Shutter valves: near the pump/motor, direct water flow — can block water from the filter and open a path to pump water out (e.g., for draining).
- Equipment enclosures: wood or cinder block structures to hide/muffle motors, heaters, pumps. Wood is cheaper; cinder block is a pricier, more finished option.

**Lighting & electrical (code items)**:
- Pool lights: at least one required by code; modern designs use multiple lights around interior walls.
- Junction box (J-Box): required code component wired to the pool lights, runs via conduit to an exterior GFCI outlet/breaker. Must sit **exactly 18 inches from the water line to the top of the box** — whiteboard marks this "1/8" ↔ J-Box" callout consistent with the 18" spec.

**Layout features**:
- Baja shelf/Baja: shallow lounging area (1-2 feet deep) for a tanning bed or small kids to play — labeled on the whiteboard as its own zone.
- Stairs & railings: railings are optional, need a separate installation specialist.
- Diving board: heavily restricted or illegal under modern safety codes in most cities.
- Handicap crane: required for commercial pools, not required residential.
- Pool fences (required to pass inspection):
  - Glass railings: high-end, aluminum/metal base holding glass panels, available at 4-foot height.
  - Removable metal fences: cheaper, small metal posts drilled into the coping/deck.
  - Wood fences: an option, but not personally recommended by the speaker.
- Surrounding landscaping flanking the coping: flagstone, grass, pavers, standard concrete.
- Gravel & sand: used when backfilling/demolishing an unwanted pool — gravel allows water to channel downward for drainage.

## Process & permit rules

- **Soil engineering phase**: before excavating, a city permit must be pulled requiring an engineering plan and a soil engineer. Takes **4-5 weeks** (sometimes months) — involves driving rods into the backyard to draw soil samples for lab testing, confirming the walls can withstand surrounding dirt pressure without shifting.
- **Excavation & rebar setup**: soil is dug and hauled away (pre-calculate haul-away cost); rebar is laid on floors/walls along with wood forms and all plumbing (drain lines and return jets).
- **City inspection sign-off**: before pouring concrete, a city inspector must inspect and sign off — verifying rebar layout and checking electrical grounding/wiring for lights and the J-box.
- **Backfilling & compacting**: once concrete forms are removed, the gap between the new wall and the dirt bank must be filled and compacted properly so future surrounding concrete pads don't settle or crack.
- **Contract specifications formula**: the contract must specify exact dimensions and materials to protect margins — explicitly write measurements like tile depth into the contract, and explicitly state what's included/excluded (plumbing modifications, water heater, landscaping, etc.).
- **Safety code/enclosure approval**: a pool will not pass final inspection — and inspectors will prohibit filling it with water — without a compliant fence/enclosure installed (ties directly to the Pool Fences requirement above).
- **Pool removal/cancellation procedure**: drill deep relief holes straight through the plaster and concrete base down into the raw earth (lets groundwater pass through and prevents the empty shell from popping out of the ground), then backfill with gravel/sand.

## What he says to do

- Client faces a long, arduous soil engineering process → check city files for a next-door neighbor who recently completed a certified soil engineering report; some city planning departments will let you adopt/pull an existing neighbor report directly, saving weeks.
- Client wants a customized design-tile band depth → close the initial deal with "4 inches" explicitly written into the contract; if the client later wants to deepen the tile line to 6" or 10", use that contract line as the basis for a change order/additional charge.
- Choosing tile for a remodel or new build → never bundle the retail material cost of tiles directly into a flat project bid — premium tile choices swing widely in price and can eat your margin if pre-bundled.
- Client only wants to replace damaged pool coping → warn them that removing old coping will almost certainly shatter or damage the adjacent waterline tile, so budget/expect that as part of the job, not a simple standalone swap.
- An existing diving board is found on a remodel site → inform the homeowner that modern safety codes outlaw diving boards in most municipalities; it likely can't be reinstalled to code.
- Client is overwhelmed by a high all-inclusive initial price → don't sell everything at once — focus the initial close on the pool itself, then upsell fencing/landscaping/heater as separate add-ons later (same phased-upsell logic as 12-upsell-the-client.md).
