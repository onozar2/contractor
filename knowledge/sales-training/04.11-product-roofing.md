# Product Module — Roofing

Source: "28. Roofing Export RED.mp4" (Gemini video-analysis panel, Google Drive) + whiteboard "28_Roofing WB.jpg"

## Products & materials

- **Plywood/sheathing**: used on pitched and flat roofs — flush placement prevents leaks over a 10-year period.
- **Metal flashing**: seals cut-out holes around roof penetrations (pipes, vents, chimneys, skylights); must be replaced whenever doing an over-layer.
- **Roof vents**: required by city code; inspectors may mandate adding more vents than currently exist if under the code minimum.
- **Underlayment (black paper)** — whiteboard's "30LB *Synthetic *Black Paper" label matches Gemini exactly:
  - 15-lb black paper: an option.
  - 30-lb black paper: what's recommended to clients — thicker, longer-lasting, justifies a higher project cost.
  - 60-lb underlayment (double-layer 30-lb): required by code for tile roof re-lays, and recommended for metal roofs.
- **Synthetic underlayment**: premium, flexible alternative to black paper — won't dry out, crack, or crumble even after 30 years. Sold as an upsell.
- **Drip edge**: metal flashing tightly around roof edges where water flow rots the fascia board. Mandatory to pass inspection; must be replaced during over-layers.
- **Composition shingles**: half black paper, half shingle material. Must ALWAYS be nailed and overlapped from the bottom of the roof to the top — installing top-to-bottom causes immediate, persistent leaking into the attic and living-room drywall.
  - 20-year shingle: very flat appearance.
  - 30-year shingle: dimensional thickness — the standard choice for most projects.
  - 40-year shingle: very thick, lasts 40-50 years, recommended for cold/snowy/high-rain climates.
- **Ridge cap**: installed along the ridge line to connect both roof slopes.
- **Tile roofing**: one of the most expensive materials, 50-60 year lifespan. Leak repairs: lift tiles, set aside, replace underlayment, reinstall the same tiles.
- **Metal shingles/roofs**: very expensive, 50-60 year lifespan.
- **Wooden shingles**: highly leak-resistant but a high fire hazard. Traditional wood-shingle roofs skip plywood, using spaced 1x4 wood slats instead.
- **Gravel/rock roof**: used on very low-pitch roofs where standard shingles are code-prohibited.
- **Flat roof methods**: Hot Tar/Nail-Down (obsolete — nails rust and pop, causing leaks within years) vs. Torch Down (modern — black paper melted directly onto plywood with a torch for a seamless membrane).
- **Capping/wall coping**: aluminum/metal or Spanish-tile cap on a flat roof's perimeter wall — whiteboard shows both "Aluminum/Metal Wall Coping" and "Clay Wall Coping" as options.

## Pricing rules

- Adding a roof vent: **$250-$500/vent**.
- **Plywood allowance**: every standard contract includes **2-3 pieces of plywood** in the baseline price — anything beyond that is a change order (same pattern as the wood-damage clause in 04.05-painting).
- **40-year shingle upcharge**: **+$50-$100 per "big square"** vs. 30-year shingles.
- **Future Value Close / Value Formula**: a standard roof might cost **$25,000 today**. A cheap roof will fail, and a replacement in 15 years could cost **$50,000**. So spending an extra **$15,000-$20,000 upfront** for an upgraded 40-year shingle or tile roof is the better long-term value — direct parallel to the same "cost of delay" framing used in 10-cancellation.md and 12-upsell-the-client.md.

## Process & permit rules

- **Three ways to address a pitched roof**: Re-decking, Tear-off, or Over-layer — whiteboard confirms this exact numbered list.
- **Re-deck process**: demo old shingles/paper/damaged wood → dumpster/haul-away → new plywood/underlayment/shingles.
- **Over-layer restrictions**: up until ~2015, codes allowed up to 3 roofing layers. Modern codes cap it at **2 layers maximum** — if a home already has 2 layers, a complete tear-off is legally required. Over-layers are also forbidden if there are active leaks or mold.
- **Tile/metal underlayment code**: 60-lb underlayment thickness mandated for tile roof replacement.
- **Wooden shingle code**: no US city permits a brand-new wood shingle roof where one doesn't already exist. Replacing an existing wood shingle roof requires removing it entirely and addressing fire-code concerns.
- **Flat roof construction**: must have a slight built-in elevation — **~2-3 inches of pitch in the middle** — so gravity carries water to the edges into a mandatory gutter system. If there's a perimeter wall, the torch-down membrane must run seamlessly up the wall to the coping cap.
- **Contract clauses**: top sales pros explicitly write specific waivers into the contract — driveway/vehicle liability during nail cleanup, and tile color-variation disclosure (see objections below).
- **Permits & inspections by method**:
  - Re-deck (2 inspections): #1 after plywood install (alignment/flushness), #2 final after full completion (signs the permit card).
  - Tear-off (2 inspections): #1 immediately after removing old material (checks existing plywood integrity/dry rot), #2 final sign-off (drip edges, ridge caps, shingle orientation).
  - Over-layer (2 inspections): #1 verifies only one existing shingle layer, #2 final completion sign-off.
  - Flat roof (1 inspection): city permit required, but inspector typically only does a single final inspection at the end.

## What he says to do

- "We're scared of getting flat tires from stray nails" → reassure the client the crew sweeps the area with a specialized magnet tool. Still instruct clearly: "Do not park your car in the driveway," even after cleanup — if they park there anyway and get a flat, they're fully responsible.
- Client has an old tile roof with a minor leak but doesn't want a full new roof → propose lifting the existing tiles, setting them aside, tearing off the failed underlayment, laying new underlayment, then reinstalling the same tiles — a repair path, not a full replacement.
- Explaining why replacement tiles will look different → warn upfront that ~15% of old tiles will break during removal and need replacing, and that 10-30 years of sun exposure means even a color-matched new tile may look different. Put this warning IN WRITING on the contract.
- Walking a completely flat (no-pitch) roof → tell the homeowner you must build a specialized wood framing system underneath to create the required 2-3" pitch.
- "I want to go with a cheaper competitor / I only buy on lowest price" → shift from price to home-protection consequences. Tell the story of a temple that spent over $200,000 on high-end remodeling (paint, carpets, woodwork, lighting) but skipped the roof — a winter leak then destroyed drywall, electrical, and carpets, costing an additional $50,000 to fix. The roof protects everything inside the home; saving money upfront on the roof risks a much bigger bill later.
