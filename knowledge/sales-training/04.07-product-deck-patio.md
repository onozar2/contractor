# Product Module — Deck & Patio

Source: "32. Deck & Patio Export RED.mp4" (Gemini video-analysis panel, Google Drive) + whiteboard "32_Deck & Patio WB.jpg"

## Products & materials

**Deck structure**:
- Metal bracing/braces: set into the footing concrete while curing; the wood post sits snugged between two pieces of metal bracing and is bolted in (matches whiteboard footing/pillar sketches).
- Large beam: sits on the pillars to hold up the structure — 4x8 or 4x6.
- Wood board (ledger board against the home): bolted to the home's framework with one or two bolts depending on desired strength — approximately 2x6 or 2x8 (whiteboard shows a labeled "4x4" post size elsewhere on the rail structure).
- Joist hangers: sit on top of the ledger board, letting the joists rest on top.
- Special hybrid screws/nails: forged from rust-proof material, a screw/nail hybrid designed specifically so deck boards won't pop out over time.
- Railing types (whiteboard "Railing Type": Wood/Composite, Metal, Glass, Cable — matches Gemini):
  - Glass railing: "a very very expensive option."
  - Cable, wooden, iron railing: other available options.

**Wood types** (whiteboard "Wood Type": Redwood, Cedarwood, Douglas Fir, IPE/Brazilian, TRX/manufactured — matches Gemini exactly):
- Redwood/cedar/Douglas fir: traditional deck woods; Douglas fir is the cheapest.
- Ipe/Brazilian wood: very strong, resists water absorption, termites won't eat it; needs restaining every 2-3 years; expensive — can cost as much as Trex.
- Trex (manufactured wood): comes with lifetime, 30-year, or 20-year warranties; anti-scratch, no staining/painting needed; costs more and needs a different installation process (special hidden-fastener system, detail cut off); usually not used for patios.

**Finishes**:
- Paint: NOT recommended for deck floors — sits on top of wood and peels quickly.
- Solid stain: soaks into the wood (vs. sitting on top like paint) — needs resealing every 5-7 years.
- Clear coat stain: shows off the wood grain but needs much more upkeep — reseal every 2-3 years.

**Elevated/structural**:
- Metal I-beams and caissons: used instead of wood supports when a deck is built ~30 feet above ground (e.g., steep hillside lots).

**Patio types** (whiteboard "Patio Type": Wood, Aluminum, Vinyl — matches Gemini):
- Aluminum patios: lightweight, cheaper than wood, foam-insulated between panels, baked-on enamel finish (like car paint) needs no upkeep, grain looks like real wood; downside — if a piece cracks you often can't spot-swap it, may require replacing the whole patio.
- Vinyl patios: similarly priced to aluminum but eventually cracks and discolors over time.
- Pergola/lattice patio (whiteboard label matches exactly): open-top style, balances shade/sun, lets rain through; built from straight 1x1 wood pieces or in a lattice pattern.
- Solid patio: solid roof, blocks sun and rain fully (whiteboard labels this "Solid" on the same roof sketch as "Pergola/Lattice").
- Patio enclosure: insulated flat roof attached to the house, requires a concrete pad (not built on dirt); does NOT count toward the home's taxable square footage.
- Sunroom: fully framed room sharing the home's roofline; DOES increase property taxes in most areas (opposite of patio enclosure).
- Gazebo: freestanding backyard structure with a vaulted top, finished in composite or wood shingles.
- Torch-down / hot tar / special glue: materials used when building a deck on top of an existing flat-roof patio, to keep it secured and waterproof.

## Pricing rules

- Glass railing: $250-$400/linear ft.
- Bench addition: $300-$400 (standard); a very large bench $500-$600.
- Deck: priced per square foot.
- Railing: priced per linear foot.
- Stairs: priced strictly per step, $150-$300/step.
- Caisson: can cost over $20,000 per single caisson if hand-dug in tight-access areas (example given: Hollywood Hills).
- Patio electrical add-ons (fans, lights, switches): +$1,000-$1,500.
- Wood patio: $40-$60/sq ft.
- Aluminum patio: $16-$25/sq ft depending on area.
- Patio enclosure wall rule: 65-75% of the walls must be windows (this is a design/code constraint, priced accordingly).
- Patio enclosure pricing formula: square footage of the patio area plus total linear footage of [the window/wall framing — exact second variable cut off in source].
- Gazebo: $10,000-$20,000+ depending on wood used.
- Deck-on-patio roof slope: the flat roof under a deck-on-patio build must slope ~1-3 inches (depending on patio size) for drainage.

## Process & contract rules

- **Permit/engineering rule**: a deck over 3-3.5 ft high requires a city permit and an engineering plan for the structural support. If the client wants to bypass engineering or handle it themselves, you MUST write on the contract: "Owner will provide engineer plan and city permit plus call city inspector" — this protects the contractor from liability if an inspector stops by.
- **Foundation guideline**: dig ~1 ft into the ground, pour concrete, and set metal braces into the concrete while it cures.
- **Siding removal rule**: if the home has wood siding, it must be fully removed to bolt the deck's ledger board directly to the home's framing (not bolted through siding).
- **Overhang limit rule**: when extending joists from the house to the main beam, keep the structure 2-4 ft away [from what — likely the beam-to-house span]; for a 4-ft deck, the beam sits ~3 ft out; for a 5-ft deck, ~4 ft out. Maximum overhang past the beam is ~1 ft, to prevent the deck getting wobbly over time.
- **Decking direction rule**: deck boards can run vertically or horizontally, but never lay them in the exact same direction as [the joists — likely rule to avoid, sentence cut off].
- **Safety/height rules**: a 1-ft deck likely needs no railing; a 2-ft deck can use just a step; a deck 3-4 ft [and above needs a full railing — sentence cut off, but implication is railing requirement scales with height].
- **Hand-digging regulation**: deep caisson holes (8, 10, or 20 ft) dug by hand require Cal/OSHA (Department of Safety) involvement.
- **Patio inspection process**: the city inspector checks two milestones: (1) footing and rebar installation, and (2) [final framing/completion — second milestone cut off].
- **Existing patio exemption**: when replacing an existing patio, some cities will skip re-checking the footing if it was previously approved/permitted.
- **Deck-on-patio fastening rule**: never secure deck wood to a flat patio roof with nails or screws (causes leaks) — instead glue with special glue/hot tar, or hook the ends to [a structural anchor point — detail cut off].

## What he says to do

- Tight backyard access for heavy machinery on a hillside lot → caisson holes must be dug entirely by hand, which drastically increases labor — charge accordingly (ties to the $20,000+/caisson figure above).
- Client wants electrical additions (fans, lights, switches) on a standard patio → explain standard patio pricing doesn't include electrical; it's not a problem to add, but charge an extra $1,000-$1,500.
- Client wants a finished floor (carpet/tile) inside a patio enclosure → since it requires the concrete pad anyway, charge extra for the flooring finish on top of that.
- Client wants a full room addition but has a tight budget/timeline → steer them toward a patio enclosure instead: a room addition can take almost a year and raises property taxes, while an enclosure takes under a month, doesn't count toward taxable square footage, and [remaining benefit cut off].
- Client wants a walkable deck built on top of a patio → the whole structure must be built out of wood (aluminum can't support deck weight); explain the complex multi-step roofing process required (sloping the roof 1-3", laying the [waterproofing membrane — sentence cut off], etc.) so they understand why it costs more.
