# One Legger — Selling With Only One Decision-Maker Present

Source: "FINAL 1080p 36. One Legger.mp4" (Gemini video-analysis panel, Google Drive folder "7. One Legger"). No whiteboard exists for this module.

"One-legger" is Josh's term for any sales call where only one of the decision-making partners (spouse, co-owner, etc.) is present or engaged. The whole module is a set of situational rules for what to do — and specifically what NOT to do (give a price) — in each flavor of that situation.

## The core rule

**Never give a price to a one-legger.** If only one decision-maker is present or engaged, connect with them, understand what they want, take measurements/notes — but do not quote a price. Giving a price to only one partner and leaving lets them shop it around independently without you in the room to defend it, which the video frames as a lost sale to whoever eventually gets both decision-makers together at once.

Both partners must be present to sign a contract. Josh states 95%-99% of the time couples will not make a structural financial decision without both present.

## Situations & what he says to do

**Situation 1 — exterior job, client won't let you inside (one spouse talks to you outside, the other stays in the house):**
- Do not try to force the indoor spouse out immediately. Engage and build rapport with the outside spouse first.
- After 30-40 minutes, the indoor spouse will usually come out naturally out of curiosity. When they do, greet them warmly and compliment them by name (example given: "Oh my gosh, so you're Rose! Oh, he spoke about you so many good things") and connect with both.
- If 30-45 minutes pass and nobody comes out, take the lead and firmly say "Okay, let's go in the house" — do NOT ask permission (don't say "Jacob, can we walk into the house?") because they'll likely say no. State it, open the door, walk in with confidence; they will follow.
- Common exterior jobs cited for this situation: roofing, exterior painting, windows, landscaping.

**Situation 2 — one spouse is home but hiding in the bedroom/office ("Don't worry, I'm making all the decisions"):**
- Josh calls this claim "BS."
- Don't try to pull them out right away — spend the first 30-40 minutes building rapport with the spouse who's present.
- Then ask the present spouse to call their partner out. If they hesitate, walk toward the bedroom yourself and say something like "Hey Michael, can you come out really quick? I want to show you some stuff." Lean on self-confidence to break the awkwardness.
- Exception noted but not detailed: there are extreme circumstances where a single spouse truly does make the decision alone [exact circumstances Gemini cut off].

**Situation 3 — only one spouse is physically home (the other is at work/away):**
- Stay calm, stay in the house, build rapport for 30-40 minutes to understand their project goals.
- Do not give them a price.
- Immediately schedule a second meeting within 24 hours for when the other partner returns (example: "Not a problem, I will be here at 7:00 PM, 6:30 PM, or tomorrow morning to meet with them"). This lets the first spouse pre-sell you to their partner before you return.
- When you come back for the second meeting, rebuild rapport from scratch with the second partner — don't assume the first spouse's rapport transfers.

**Situation 4 — a neighbor is jointly financially involved (e.g., splitting a shared fence) but isn't present:**
- Do not give a price.
- Reschedule to a time when you can meet the homeowners AND the neighbor together.
- Example project cited: fences, where neighbors commonly split cost.

**Situation 5 — one partner tries to leave the meeting right as you reach pricing:**
- Josh's read: they leave because they're scared and sense you're about to close them; leaving early sets up the stall objection ("I need to ask my husband... we'll talk it overnight and call you tomorrow").
- Stop them immediately, don't let them leave.
- Be assertive: "Hey Mike, sorry, it's going to take me another 10-15 minutes to finish. I need you here in order to [finish/decide]."
- If needed, be more direct: "Hey listen, I just spent with you guys two and a half hours. Please give me another 15 minutes of your time." Take charge, don't be scared of the client, maintain control of the room.

## Process rules

- **The 24-hour follow-up rule**: when dealing with a single decision-maker, schedule the second meeting for the same day or "not longer than 24 hours" later — otherwise "most likely you'll never walk into that house again."
- **The "run the numbers" protocol**: when scheduling the follow-up, tell the client you need to go back to the office to run the numbers, then return so everyone can sit down together and go over them. This gives you a legitimate reason to delay pricing until both partners are present.
- **Contract signing prerequisite**: both partners must physically be present to sign.

## What products/projects get mentioned as examples

No product specs or pricing numbers are covered in this module — it's pure process/objection-handling. Projects mentioned only as situational examples: roofing, exterior painting, windows, landscaping (exterior "won't let you in" scenario), kitchens (interior remodel example, and a topic that comes up during measurements), fences (neighbor cost-share scenario).
