## Pricing rules

- **Down payment limit (the law)**: cannot exceed **$1,000 or 10% of the contract price, whichever is less**.
  - Example: $450,000 ADU project → max legal down payment is $1,000 (not 10%, since 10% would be far more — the "whichever is less" rule caps it at $1,000).
  - Example: $8,000 job → max legal down payment is 10% = $800.
- **Deposit minimum (practical, not legal)**: if a client won't give the full $1,000, still secure at least $500, $100, or $50 — never leave it at $0.00 unless absolutely necessary, to make the contract "solid" and prevent easy cancellation.
- **Final payment withholding rule**: never leave a large chunk of money on the final milestone (clients frequently [find reasons to withhold/delay — sentence cut off]).
  - $450,000 project → final payment should be only ~$20,000.
  - $50,000 project → client should hold no more than $4,000-$5,000 for the end.
  - $10,000 project → client should hold no more than $1,500.
- **Mechanic's lien timeline**: contractor must file a mechanic's lien within **90 days** of job completion if the homeowner doesn't pay; then has **3 months (90 days)** after filing to enforce it through court/arbitration.
- **Insurance coverage figures** (used when explaining protections to the client): general liability policies typically cover home damage (example given: an HVAC gas-leak fire) up to $500,000, $1,000,000, or $2,000,000. Contractor bonds generally protect up to $10,000-$15,000 depending on the state.
- **Completion date buffer formula** (see Process rules below for the full table) — this is really a timeline/risk-buffer rule, not a price, but it's numeric: 1-2 day job → 1 month buffer; 1-month job → 6 month buffer; 6-month-to-1-year job → 2 year buffer.

## Process & contract rules

### Contract sections and exact wording
- **Contractor info section**: write the formal business name (e.g., "ABS Construction Inc.") and license number — this section is usually pre-filled by the company. **Salesman restriction**: never put personal name/phone/email here — "You should never put your personal information on it. You're not the contractor; you're the salesman." The only place for the salesman's name is at the bottom of the contract.
- **Property owner info**: write the owner's name(s) at top — if husband and wife, list both (e.g., "Jacob and Ann"). If a son is involved, note him in parentheses as "(Sun)" ["Son" — likely a transcription/spelling artifact from the source, flagged not corrected]. Get home address, home phone, AND cell phone — "get as much information as possible" (two or three phone numbers if available) so the contractor can reach them if a problem arises later. Get an email if they have one.
- **Project location section**: write the work address; if same as home address, write "Same as above." If no construction lender is used, write "N/A" for "not applicable."
- **Description of project**: a brief job-scope name (e.g., "Bathroom Remodeling," "Complete Home Remodeling"). Leaving it blank doesn't cancel the contract, but it helps clarity. If space is limited, write "See additional description" and reference a numbered sheet (e.g., sheet "3").
- **Finance charge line**: write "N/A" — even when using an external financing company, the customer signs those financing documents directly with the lender, separate from this contract.
- **Payment schedule wording**: if space is limited on a large project, write "See additional description" to lay out milestone stages on a separate page.
  - First milestone clause (important wording): **"Upon start of job and delivery of some materials."**
  - Final payment clause: **"Upon job completion."**
- **Paperwork handout checkboxes**: the salesman must mark X's on the contract confirming the homeowner received copies of: Home Improvement Contract, Notice of Arbitration/Arbitration of Disputes, Notice of Cancellation/Three Days Right to Cancel, Disclosure of Commercial General Liability and Insurance, Workers' Compensation Insurance, Contractor License copy, and Mechanic's Lien Warning notice.

### The 3-day rule and completion-date buffers
- **3-day rule**: by law in most states, the client must get 3 days to cancel before any work starts — so the scheduled start date should typically be at least 3-4 days out from the contract signing date.
- **Completion date buffer table** — never write a tight/exact completion date (missing it = breach of contract); always pad generously:
  - 1-2 day job (e.g., concrete driveway) → give yourself **1 month**.
  - 1-month job (e.g., bathroom or kitchen) → give yourself **6 months**.
  - 6-month-to-1-year job (e.g., room addition or ADU) → give yourself **2 years**.

## What he says to do

- **Client has an emergency** (plumbing leak, crumbling foundation, severe electrical problem) and wants work to start immediately, before the 3-day window → have them sign a specific "waiving of the three days right to cancel" document, on which they must explicitly write WHY they're waiving it.
- **Client questions the 2-year completion buffer on an 8-month project** → say: "Listen, you never know what could happen. Something will happen, God forbid, and we don't want to [be in breach — sentence cut off]." Frame the buffer as protective security, not a real estimate.
- **Client doesn't trust giving a down payment** → if you trust the client, you can write "$0.00" instead of leaving it blank; but still try to negotiate some smaller deposit to secure the deal and reduce cancellation risk.
- **Client objects to the arbitration clause** → educate them that arbitration resolves disputes faster than court (though more expensive); if they flatly refuse to initial it, call your boss/company owner immediately to check if proceeding without it is okay.
- **Explaining the Right to Cancel and all the initials** → don't hide the 3-day right to cancel — tell them directly. Frame every signature/initial as being "to protect the client" — this honesty builds trust and comfort rather than looking like the contractor is hiding something.
- **Selling to elderly clients (62+) living alone with no family present** → ask them: "Has anybody helped you make the decision?" If they say no, have them sign the standard line, then on a SECOND line have them physically hand-write: "I am the only decision maker." This protects the contractor if an adult child later complains about a parent being signed up alone — the contractor can show the written proof of independent decision-making.
- **Explaining worker liability and liens during paperwork review** → build trust by walking through what each protection does: the Mechanic's Lien warning protects the equity flow but [also warns them of a real risk if they don't pay — sentence cut off]; Workers' Comp Insurance absorbs medical liability if a worker is injured on their property (which would otherwise fall on the homeowner); the Bond protects up to $10,000-$15,000 if the company goes bankrupt or disappears mid-job.
