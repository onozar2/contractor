# Open a Job — Project Kickoff Process

Source: "FINAL 1080p 50. How to Open a Job.mp4" (Gemini video-analysis panel, Google Drive folder "8. Open a Job"). No whiteboard exists for this module.

This module covers what happens between signing the contract and the crew actually starting work — subcontractor selection, client introductions, and the legal/practical mechanics of "opening" a job.

## Products, materials, tools, and documents mentioned

- **Construction contract**: signing it starts the legal clock — see 3-day rule below.
- **Right to Start / Waiver document**: a separate document a client can sign to legally let a contractor bypass the standard 3-business-day waiting period.
- **Kickoff video/photos**: right after selling the job, the speaker records a video explaining exactly what needs to be done, and sends it to the office, the project managers, and the subcontractor so everyone has the same scope understanding. Before-and-after photos are also taken for company records.
- **Subcontractor contract/written agreement**: once a subcontractor is picked, every task they're responsible for must be written down in extreme detail — the speaker specifically warns that omitting details (e.g., who pays to replace a single piece of damaged wood behind a shower) causes disputes later.
- **Floor paper & masking materials**: subs are required to lay paper on floors and mask all vulnerable areas before any tool is used — keeps the job clean and makes the client feel secure.
- **Business cards**: the on-site project manager or runner must hand the client their card/phone number for direct communication.
- **Trade materials named in the "spiking" context** (see below): kitchen doors, countertops, carpets, flooring, windows.

## Pricing & scheduling numbers

- **Three business days**: the legal waiting period after contract signing before a job can be opened.
- **Subcontractor bidding window**: schedule 2-3 contractors to bid on the same morning, staggered in roughly 1-hour windows — bring your best contractor first so they get the first shot at winning it.
- **Next-day arrival window**: when telling a client when workers arrive the next morning, never give an exact time — always give a range [exact window size not specified by the source].
- **Site visit frequency**: the project lead must show up at the job site at least once every 2-3 days to maintain oversight.

## Process & checklist (in order)

1. **Record the site (day of sale)**: comprehensive video/photos explaining scope, sent to office + project managers + subcontractor immediately after the sale closes.
2. **Pre-call the subcontractor**: call the day before kickoff and ask directly: "If we close a price, can you start right away?"
3. **Stagger site bids**: meet 2-3 contractors at the property sequentially in ~1-hour windows; your best contractor goes first.
4. **Confirm price and scope**: review pricing with the chosen subcontractor and write down every explicit task in extreme detail to prevent later financial disputes.
5. **Perform client introductions**: introduce the selected subcontractor to the customer as your "foreman" or "company worker." Introduce any on-site project manager/runner too. Make this first introduction yourself, personally, so the client knows you're the one running the project.
6. **Hand over contact info**: the on-site project manager gives their phone number and business card directly to the client.
7. **Protect the property**: subcontractor lays floor paper and masks all vulnerable areas before any work begins.

## Objections & situations

**Client cancels within the legal 3-day window**: legally, if you started work within those 3 days and the client cancels, they generally don't have to pay for the work already done. Josh's countermeasure is "**spiking the job**" — intentionally starting some work early (e.g., pulling out a few kitchen doors "for measurements," or similarly touching countertops/carpets/flooring/windows) so the client feels the job is already underway and is less inclined to exercise their cancellation right. **Josh explicitly states this is "not 100% legal"** but presents it as common industry practice.

> Flagging for whoever uses this module: this is a legally questionable tactic straight from the source video, not something to present to trainees as a clean best practice. It's included here because the training says it, not as an endorsement — treat it as a compliance risk if implemented literally, and note that the 3-day cancellation right exists precisely to protect consumers from this kind of pressure.

**A scheduled subcontractor doesn't show up for a bid**: expected — out of three scheduled contractors, one will almost always no-show. That's why you always schedule 2-3, not 1.

**A contractor's bid comes in way over budget**: let them go immediately, wait for the next scheduled contractor.

**A subcontractor demands extra money later for unforeseen work** (e.g., damaged wood behind a shower): risk is they involve the client directly, creating a mess. Prevention is the same as the general rule — write every task down in extreme detail in the subcontractor contract up front so there's no room for a later dispute.

**Something goes wrong later in the project timeline**: the stated mitigation is to front-load your effort into making the first day perfect (clean masking, punctual arrival, professionalism) — a client who's happy on day one is more forgiving of problems that surface afterward.
