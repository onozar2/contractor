## Products & materials

**Old vs. new windows** — old windows have a single, thin glass pane that lets outside noise through; new windows are strongly sealed, give significant noise reduction, and have strong locking systems (no more broomstick-in-the-track security). Newer screens use specialized materials that won't dry out and break.

**Glass technology**:
- Double/triple pane: newer windows use two (sometimes three) glass layers, each thicker than old single-pane glass. A double-pane window will shatter if hit hard but is much harder to break through than single pane.
- U-Factor: energy-efficiency rating — the LOWER the U-factor, the more efficient the window (less heat transfer).
- Low-E (Low Emissivity): the HIGHER the Low-E number, the more efficient. "Low-E 1" is very inefficient; the speaker's company sells **Low-E 3** — 3+ layers of silver coating or tin oxide. Whiteboard confirms exactly: "U-Factor (lower is better, marked with a minus), Low-E (1-2-3, marked with a plus)."
- Silver coating / tin oxide: deflects sun away from the window, applied directly to glass, gives a rich color/slight tint, typically lasts **10-15 years** before fading.
- Argon gas: injected between the two panes for insulation — speaker compares it to the gas deep-sea divers use in dry suits to withstand cold.
- French grids: placed inside the glass (except on wooden windows), easier to keep clean; can also go on French doors or picture windows.

**Frame materials**:
- Vinyl: doesn't conduct heat, very energy efficient; flexible, can warp over 20-30 years, thick moldings/rough edges. Two sub-types — recycled vinyl (turns yellow after 5-6 years) vs. virgin vinyl (stays white; the speaker's manufacturers only use virgin vinyl).
- Aluminum: stronger and pricier than vinyl, best for huge window openings; shares most vinyl benefits but has a higher U-factor (less efficient).
- Wood: 100% custom-made — cut, refinish, glass install, sealing. One of the most expensive types; arrives unstained/unpainted, needs a painter to finish.
- Fiberglass: the "cream of the crop" — expensive, modern look, matches vinyl's efficiency, won't warp, thinner molding, but must be repainted every few years.

**Window/door types**: picture windows (fixed, no opening), normal/standard windows (open up/down or sideways), garden windows (kitchen bump-outs, project ~1 foot, standard sizes 4x4/4x5/4x3), sliding doors (single-side-move or both-sides-move), French doors (swing in/out, solid glass or grids), skylights (normal swap-out vs. electrical requiring a dedicated line), hurricane-proof windows (Florida-market, bolted to structure, rated for 100-180 mph winds).

## Pricing rules

- Windows make up **~20-25%** of a home's exterior surface (used in the insulation-objection script below).
- Built-in tint upgrade: **+$50-$60/window**.
- French grids: **+$50-$100/window**, depending on size.
- Fiberglass or wood window: **~$2,500/window**.
- Standard vinyl window: **$250-$700**.
- Garden window: **$1,400-$2,000/window**.
- Sliding vs. French doors: a both-sides-moving slider is pricier than a single-side slider — but surprisingly, a **French door costs LESS than a sliding door**.
- Skylight costs: unit alone **$500-$1,500**; structural framing/roof cutting/sealing **$2,500-$3,500**; total installed cost **~$5,000**.

## Process & permit rules

- **Lead times**: vinyl/aluminum windows 2-3 weeks; large aluminum mansion orders 2-3 months; wood windows (fully custom) up to 2 months; garden windows 4-6 weeks.
- **Wood window painting**: always add painting/staining cost separately when estimating — factor in the painter's "minimum job" fee if it's only one window.
- **New skylight structural work**: requires cutting open the roof, building structural framing, installing metal flashing, and sealing the full perimeter.
- **Window blueprint labels — the XO system** (whiteboard confirms exactly, showing 3-panel Open/Close/Open and 2-panel O-O / O-X diagrams):
  - X = closed/solid (non-movable).
  - O = open (movable/sliding).
  - OX = one side slides, other is stationary — most residential windows use this layout.
  - OXO = a central fixed picture window bordered by two inward-sliding windows.
  - Both sides/top-bottom moving = a separate layout the tech must confirm with the client.
  - **Measurement protocol**: the technician must ask the client exactly which side they prefer operable before finalizing the order.
- **Permits vary by city** — many cities require no permit for window replacement; **Florida requires a mandatory permit** because windows must be bolted directly to the structure (hurricane code).
- **Warranty structure**: windows come with a **lifetime manufacturer warranty**, but this only covers the PRODUCT (e.g., glass seal failure, hardware) — never labor. The manufacturer replaces the unit only; labor/installation warranty must come separately from the contractor's own company. Always explain this distinction to the client.

## What he says to do

- Selling against old single-pane windows → "Your glass in your window right now is thinner than the glass that we have in our windows now." Point out that single-pane lets in outside noise (neighbors, traffic).
- Client hesitant about windows vs. whole-house insulation spend → remind them windows are ~25% of the home's exterior exposure — you can insulate roof/walls/attic/crawlspace, but windows remain the most exposed weak point; uninsulated windows let drafts and radiant heat straight through.
- Client wants a skylight on an old roof → do NOT warranty the roof. Say explicitly: "Listen, I can put you the skylight but I am not warranting your roof unless I'm redoing your entire [roof]." Get this in writing — if you touch an old roof without the disclaimer and a leak happens later, the client will blame you for it.
- Client objects that the warranty is only 10 years, not lifetime → reassure using an industry rule of thumb: "Listen, if in 10 years your window is good, most likely it will last for 30 years." Compare to a car — manufacturing defects usually surface early/under warranty, so holding up through the warranty period is a strong signal it'll hold up well beyond it.
