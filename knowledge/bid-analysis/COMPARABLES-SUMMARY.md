## Market ranges observed vs. costbook.json

**Bathroom remodels (bids #02, #04, #07 — single/primary-bath-scale jobs):** observed totals range **$23,500 – $26,580** for full-scope bathroom work (demo through paint, with tile/plumbing/electrical/waterproofing), and $25,000 for a smaller bath portion bundled with bedroom/closet/hallway work. Summing costbook.json's discrete `Bathroom Renovations` line items (bath-demo $900-2,400 + bath-pan $1,200-3,200 + bath-tile $2,200-6,500 + bath-plumb $2,000-6,000 + bath-elec $800-2,500 + bath-floor $600-1,800 + bath-paint $300-900, **excluding** vanity/glass/toilet which are frequently client-supplied per these bids) gives a costbook composite range of roughly **$8,000 – $23,300** for the same trade set. All 3 real bathroom bids price **at or above the top of that composite costbook range** ($23,500-$26,580 vs. costbook's $23,300 ceiling) — **Joon's costbook bathroom ranges read low relative to real-world signed contracts**, especially once framing/structural work (new walls, shower bench, niche — as in bid #04) or premium finish labor (lacquer paint, marble, heated towel rack — as in bid #07) is included, since costbook has no explicit "framing" or "premium paint finish" line for bathrooms.

**ADU garage conversions (bids #05, #06):** observed totals **$110,000 and $129,000**, both for a garage-conversion ADU with kitchen+bath and heavy client-supplied finishes (client provides doors, windows, flooring, HVAC unit, water heater, cabinets, countertops, all fixtures, vanities). Applying an **assumed** 350-600 sf footprint (per costbook.json `adu-garage` typical range — not stated in either bid), these totals fall at roughly **$183-$369/sf (#05)** and **$215-$434/sf (#06)**, which sits within to above costbook's `adu-garage` benchmark of **$200-$400/sf** — notable because costbook's benchmark is explicitly a **full-turnkey** figure (materials+labor), while these two real bids **exclude** the bulk of finish-material costs. That means the true "rough construction + labor only" cost per sf implied by these bids is likely being masked by the unknown sf assumption — **flag this as the single biggest gap in the comparable set: neither ADU bid states square footage**, so Joon cannot validate its $/sf ADU assumptions with real confidence from these two documents alone. Directionally, though, $110K-$129K for a garage-conversion ADU shell+MEP+finish-labor (no major finish materials) is consistent with costbook's ADU range being reasonable, possibly even conservative once a full turnkey (with finishes) price is built up from these bids.

**General/multi-trade remodels (bids #01, #03):** not directly comparable to a single costbook category since they span many trades in one lump sum; both priced consistently with summed costbook per-trade ranges for the scopes described (e.g., bid #01's $46,000 kitchen-with-wall-opening line sits above simple kitchen costbook items because it bundles structural header-beam work, which costbook does not price as a discrete kitchen line item).

## What these teach us about pricing

1. **The standard CA remodeling coverage split, observed 5 of 7 times nearly verbatim:** contractor covers demolition, rough+finish MEP, framing/structural, waterproofing, insulation, drywall, and *installation* labor for tile/fixtures/cabinetry; client supplies (or pays for, allowance-style) all **finish/selection materials** — tile, countertop stone, cabinets/vanities, doors, windows, fixtures, appliances, light fixtures, flooring material, hardware. **Permits, city fees, inspection fees, engineering fees, HERS testing, and structural observation are client-paid in all 7 bids without exception.** This is the coverage split codified in the Joon bid template.

2. **CSLB down-payment compliance is consistently followed:** every bid with a stated payment schedule (5 of 7) opens with a $1,000 deposit — matching the CA rule that a home-improvement contract down payment cannot exceed the lesser of $1,000 or 10% of the contract price (BPC §7159.5). The Joon template should hard-code this.

3. **Milestone-based progress payments scale with project complexity:** small bathroom-scale jobs (bid #07) use a clean 5-stage schedule; large ADU jobs (bids #05, #06) use an 11-stage schedule tied to construction phases (foundation → framing → rough MEP → insulation → tile → drywall → final inspection). Two of seven bids (#02, #03) had **no payment schedule at all**, and one (#04) had none despite being the most professionally organized bid in the set — a real-world gap the Joon template closes by making a milestone schedule mandatory.

4. **Same-contractor, same-week, same-scope-boilerplate price variance can be large:** bids #05 and #06 (Guideline Builders, one week apart, nearly identical scope language) came in $19,000 (17%) apart with no explanation in the documents — a reminder to Joon that identical-looking scope templates still need site-specific pricing, not a fixed formula.

5. **"Client contracts a specialty vendor directly" is a recurring, legitimate carve-out** (kitchen cabinet maker in bids #03 and #05/#06; carpet company and vanity-cabinet vendor in bid #07) — useful as an optional Joon template clause when a client has a preferred vendor Joon doesn't want to mark up or warranty.

6. **Boilerplate reuse carries risk:** bid #01 references being reimbursed via "Ground Up Builders" — a different company name than the quoting contractor, an apparent copy-paste artifact — and two Guideline Builders ADU bids skip payment-line "#11" in numbering. Both are reminders to proofread any Joon template output per-project rather than trusting a saved boilerplate blindly.
