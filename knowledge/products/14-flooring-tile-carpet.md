# Flooring: Tile & Carpet

## The ladder

**Tile:**
- *Good — ceramic tile, PEI I-II.* Fired ceramic body, glazed surface, standard 12x12 or 12x24 format. Suited to walls, backsplashes, and low-traffic areas — not durable enough for main floor traffic. Installed: roughly **$8-$12 per sq ft**.
- *Better — porcelain tile, PEI III-IV.* Denser, less porous, fired at higher temperature than ceramic; PEI IV rating handles any residential floor including entries and kitchens, plus light commercial. Standard sizes through large-format (12x24, 24x24). Installed: **$12-$18 per sq ft**.
- *Best — large-format porcelain (24x48+) or natural stone, PEI IV-V.* Large-format porcelain gives a seamless, high-end look with fewer grout lines but requires a dead-flat substrate and more skilled (slower, costlier) installation — often large-format tile setters charge a premium labor rate specifically for this. Natural stone (marble, travertine, slate) is a separate premium category requiring sealing and more maintenance. Installed: **$18-$30+ per sq ft**.

**Carpet:**
- *Good — polyester (PET), 20-30 oz face weight.* Soft, inherently stain-resistant, budget-friendly, but the least durable fiber under heavy traffic — best for bedrooms, low-traffic rooms. Installed: roughly **$3-$5 per sq ft**.
- *Better — nylon, 35-45 oz face weight.* The industry's strength/resilience benchmark — bounces back from furniture and foot traffic better than any other fiber, treated for stain resistance. Installed: **$5-$8 per sq ft**.
- *Best — triexta (e.g., Mohawk SmartStrand), 45+ oz face weight.* Stain resistance is built into the fiber itself (not just a surface treatment) so spills release more easily, and it holds up to nylon-level durability with a softer hand-feel — popular with pet owners. Installed: **$7-$10+ per sq ft**.

## How to choose

- Kitchens, entries, and high-traffic main living areas → porcelain, PEI IV minimum; never ceramic on a floor that gets daily heavy foot traffic.
- Shower walls, backsplashes, accent walls → PEI I-II ceramic is fine and cheaper — durability rating matters far less on a vertical, low-abrasion surface.
- Client wants the minimalist large-format look → confirm subfloor flatness first (large-format tile telegraphs any dip or hump) — this can add a self-leveling-compound line item.
- Bedrooms and low-traffic rooms where softness matters more than durability → polyester carpet is a legitimate budget-tier choice, not just "cheap."
- Family rooms, stairs, homes with pets → nylon or triexta at 40+ oz face weight; this is where carpet quality actually shows up over time.

## Upsell notes

"PEI is basically a hardness test — a IV-rated porcelain can take a kitchen's daily abuse for decades, a II-rated ceramic will show wear in the entry within a few years." "Big-format tile means fewer grout lines to clean and re-seal — it reads more expensive because it basically is a cleaner look." "SmartStrand's stain resistance is baked into the fiber, not sprayed on top like most stain-guard treatments — that's why it still resists spills even after years of vacuuming and cleaning." "Face weight is the real quality number on carpet — two carpets can have the same fiber name and feel completely different underfoot."

## Watch out

Large-format tile (24x48 and up) requires a flatter substrate tolerance than standard tile and a trained large-format setter — budget more labor time and confirm the sub has done it before. Natural stone needs periodic sealing and is more prone to etching/staining (especially in kitchens) — set that maintenance expectation up front or it becomes a callback. Polished porcelain and natural stone can be slippery when wet — steer clients away from high-polish finishes in bathrooms and entries, especially with elderly residents in the home; consider a matte or textured finish instead. Carpet face weight and PEI ratings vary by specific product line and change with manufacturer runs — verify current spec sheet numbers before quoting a named product rather than relying on memory.
