# Stucco

## The ladder

**Good — One-coat (fiber-reinforced) stucco system:** ~$5-9.75/sf installed (LA market runs toward the top of this range with labor costs). A single fiberglass-reinforced base coat goes over the weather barrier/lath, then a finish coat — no multi-day cure between scratch and brown coats. Faster install, lower labor hours, and adequate performance for most standard single-story additions, patch repairs, and budget-driven jobs.

**Better — Traditional 3-coat cement plaster (scratch/brown/finish):** ~$7-9/sf installed for new application (full house re-stucco on a ~2,000 sf exterior wall area runs $14,000-$28,000). Scratch coat is troweled and scored onto the lath for mechanical bond; brown coat levels and builds thickness; finish coat carries color and texture. This is the traditional Southern California standard — most 1950s-2000s LA stucco homes were built this way, and it's what most stucco repair/patch work needs to match.

**Best — Acrylic finish system (EIFS-adjacent, synthetic stucco):** ~$10-18/sf installed (full-house EIFS runs $18,000-$36,000 on a comparable home). Rigid foam insulation board bonded to the wall, fiberglass mesh embedded, then a polymer-modified acrylic finish coat. Adds real R-value and flexibility (better crack resistance over foam substrates), plus the widest color/texture palette since acrylic finishes are factory-tinted and don't need separate painting.

## How to choose

- Small patch/repair matching existing stucco → match the existing system (almost always traditional 3-coat on pre-2000s LA housing stock) rather than mixing systems, which telegraphs a visible seam and can trap moisture differently.
- Standard remodel/addition on a budget, new garage or ADU wall → one-coat system; adequate durability, faster schedule, lower cost.
- Full re-stucco after stucco failure (cracking, moisture intrusion) on an older home → traditional 3-coat, the code-familiar, resale-safe choice for LA appraisers and buyers.
- New construction, ADU, or a client prioritizing insulation/energy efficiency and a smoother modern look → acrylic/EIFS-adjacent finish system.
- Any job near the coast or in a high-crack-risk area (expansive soil, older foundation) → discuss acrylic's flexibility advantage — it resists hairline cracking better than pure cement finishes.

## Upsell notes

"The one-coat system holds up fine, but the traditional three-coat is what's on basically every home in this neighborhood — it's the safe, sellable look, and it's proven over decades in our climate." For the acrylic step-up: "This finish flexes instead of cracking as the house settles, and it comes pre-colored, so you're not repainting the exterior again in five years."

## Watch out

**Finish textures** — smooth, sand float, and lace/dash are texture choices layered onto any of the three systems above, not separate systems; confirm which texture matches the existing house (LA tract homes vary block by block, and a mismatched texture patch is highly visible).
- Matching an existing 1960s-80s LA stucco house exactly (color + texture) on a patch job is difficult — weathering changes the original color, and lace/dash texture is hard to replicate by hand; set expectations that patches may be visible up close.
- EIFS/acrylic systems require correct flashing and drainage details (drainable EIFS, not "barrier" EIFS) — undetailed synthetic stucco has a well-documented history of trapping moisture and causing rot in wood-framed walls; always spec drainable EIFS in LA's occasional wind-driven rain.
- Stucco over wood-frame construction needs weep screeds at the foundation line and proper weather-resistive barrier per code — missing these is a common inspection fail.
- New stucco needs proper cure time (traditional 3-coat: about 7 days minimum between brown and finish coat, plus a broader cure period before painting) — rushing the schedule causes cracking.
