# Fencing & Gates

## The ladder

**By material, installed per linear foot (LA market, standard 6-ft residential fence unless noted):**
- **Good — vinyl**: ~$35-$65/lf installed. Zero-maintenance, won't rot/warp/need paint, wide range of styles (privacy, picket, semi-privacy). The practical "set it and forget it" option, especially for clients who don't want a recurring maintenance conversation.
- **Good/mid — pressure-treated pine**: ~$25-$40/lf installed. Cheapest wood option, needs re-staining/sealing every 2-3 years or it grays and warps faster than cedar/redwood.
- **Better — cedar**: ~$45-$70/lf installed in LA (higher than the ~$23-$50/lf national range — LA labor and hillside-access premiums push it up). Naturally rot- and insect-resistant, takes stain well, the standard "good wood fence" recommendation.
- **Better/best — redwood**, Construction Heart grade: ~$70-$120/lf installed. California-native material, best natural weathering and rot resistance of the wood options, premium look and feel — priced accordingly.
- **Best — wrought iron / powder-coated steel**: ~$45-$130/lf installed depending on pattern complexity, picket spacing, and finish. Doesn't block light/airflow or sightlines (a real selling point on view lots or pool enclosures), effectively permanent with minimal upkeep beyond occasional touch-up paint.
- **Best/hybrid — block wall (CMU) with wood or wrought-iron inlay panels**: priced per project, typically $80-$150+/lf installed depending on block height, stucco finish, and inlay material — this is the top-tier combo for street-facing frontage where the client wants masonry permanence with a softer visual break.

**Automatic gate operator upsell (add to any driveway gate):**
- Basic swing or slide operator, single residential driveway: ~$2,500-$5,000 installed complete (operator + wiring + safety sensors).
- Higher-end system with smart/app access, keypad, or long electrical run from the panel: ~$4,000-$8,000+ installed.

## How to choose

- Client wants low-maintenance and privacy, doesn't care about a "natural" look → vinyl privacy fence.
- Client wants a classic wood look and is budget-sensitive but not rock-bottom → cedar; it's the default "good wood fence" recommendation for most LA backyards.
- Client specifically wants premium/high-end wood, or wants a material that ages best against direct sun exposure (a real factor in LA's UV load) → redwood.
- Pool enclosure, hillside/view lot, or anywhere the client wants security without blocking sightlines → wrought iron/steel.
- Street-facing front yard where the client wants a substantial, permanent-feeling boundary (and is willing to pay for it) → block wall with wood or iron inlay — also the highest-margin combo job on this list.
- Gated driveway, especially on a property with any privacy/security concern or a long driveway from the street → automatic operator is the natural upsell the moment a swing or slide gate is already being quoted.

## Upsell notes

"Pressure-treated pine is the cheap option, but you're re-staining it every couple years or it starts graying and cupping. Cedar costs more up front but it fights rot and bugs on its own — you're not fighting the wood every summer." For redwood: "This is the wood that's actually built for this climate — it's what you see holding up on decks and fences that have been in the sun for twenty years without warping." For wrought iron: "A wood or vinyl fence back there blocks your view of the yard — iron gives you the security without losing the sightline, and there's basically nothing to maintain." For the gate operator: "Since we're already putting in the gate, automating it now is a fraction of the cost of coming back and retrofitting an opener later — plus you're not getting out of the car to open it every time."

## Watch out

- **LA city front-yard fence height**: 42 inches (3.5 ft) max without a permit; anything taller in the front yard needs a permit, and a fence over ~7 ft in front typically triggers plan check.
- **LA city side/rear yard height**: non-masonry fences (wood, vinyl, wrought iron, chain-link) up to 8 ft don't need a permit; masonry/block walls need a permit above 42 inches in **any** location (front, side, or rear) due to seismic/structural requirements — always flag block wall jobs for permit regardless of height.
- Verify current height/permit thresholds against the specific property's zoning before quoting — LA County unincorporated areas, hillside overlay zones, and HOA/HPOZ (historic preservation) districts can carry different or stricter limits than the base City of Los Angeles code.
- Post spacing/gauge: wood fence posts (4x4 or 6x6, set in concrete) typically run 6-8 ft on-center; wrought iron/steel panel systems follow the manufacturer's panel width (commonly 6-8 ft sections); block walls need engineered rebar/pilaster spacing per structural code, especially on any wall over 42 inches or on a slope — don't eyeball this, pull it from the structural plan on permitted work.
- Property line disputes are common in LA's older, informally-fenced neighborhoods — confirm the client has a survey or clear agreement with the neighbor before installing on/near a shared line; replacing a fence "in the same spot" doesn't avoid a dispute if the original placement was never verified.
- Hillside/sloped lots need stepped or racked (raked) panel construction, which adds labor and material waste — get eyes on the actual grade before quoting a flat per-linear-foot price on a hillside property.
- Automatic gate operators require a Underwriters Laboratories UL 325-compliant safety package (photo eyes, edge sensors, entrapment protection) — this isn't optional add-on equipment, it's required by code on any powered vehicular gate; make sure it's priced into every operator quote, not treated as an upsell within the upsell.
