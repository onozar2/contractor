# Patio Covers & Pergolas

## The ladder

- **Good — Alumawood (aluminum wood-look), lattice/open pattern:** $18-22/sf installed. Engineered kit systems (pre-designed, permit-ready packages sold through licensed dealers) that go up fast, never rot, warp, or need repainting. Lattice pattern lets in dappled light — no permit shortcut, but it's the cheapest way to define an outdoor living space.
- **Better — Alumawood, solid roof pattern:** $20-35/sf installed (up to $55/sf for larger/more custom layouts); insulated Alumawood roof systems run $36-50/sf. Solid roof blocks sun and rain fully, so the space becomes usable year-round instead of just shade. Comes in wood-grain finishes (the "wood-look" appeal) with zero maintenance.
- **Better — real wood (custom-built):** $27-75/sf installed depending on wood grade and design complexity; solid-roof custom wood can run $60-120/sf for high-end lumber and detailing. Real cedar or Douglas fir reads as more architectural/custom than Alumawood, matches houses with existing wood trim or exposed-beam interiors, but requires re-sealing/re-staining every 3-6 years (~$6/sf per treatment) to avoid weather damage.
- **Best — louvered/adjustable-louver systems (Equinox, StruXure — verify current brand/dealer lineup, these systems are typically sold through certified dealer networks and pricing varies by dealer):** Equinox-class systems run roughly $65-100/sf installed; StruXure-class premium systems run $150-200/sf installed (a 12x20 StruXure-class system can land $36,000-48,000 total). Motorized aluminum louvers that rotate open for full sun/airflow or close flat for full rain/shade coverage, often paired with integrated LED lighting, heaters, drop screens, and app/smart-home control. This is the "outdoor room you use 12 months a year" tier.

## How to choose

- Budget-conscious homeowner who wants defined outdoor shade fast, or a rental/investment property → Alumawood lattice.
- Homeowner who wants a true all-weather covered patio (rain protection, more usable square footage) without stepping up to motorized louvers → Alumawood solid roof.
- Homes with a strong existing wood architectural language (craftsman, farmhouse, exposed-beam interiors) where a metal system would look out of place → real wood, custom-built.
- Homeowner who entertains often, wants the space to flex between full sun and full shade/rain-cover on demand, or is building a genuine backyard-living investment → louvered system.
- Anyone mentioning they want to add a fireplace, TV, heaters, or full outdoor kitchen under the cover → steer toward louvered or solid-roof tiers; lattice can't support the added structural/electrical load as cleanly.

## Upsell notes

"Alumawood looks like real wood but it'll never crack, warp, or need to be restained every few years — with real wood you're back up on a ladder resealing it every 3-5 years or it starts graying and splitting." For the louvered step-up: "Right now your patio cover is an all-or-nothing bet — full shade or full sun, no in-between, and if it rains you're stuck inside. A louvered roof lets you dial it from wide open to fully closed in under a minute, so it's usable rain or shine instead of three seasons a year."

## Watch out

- LA patio covers under 120 sf in roof area and one-story/non-habitable are generally permit-exempt; anything over 120 sf, over 12 ft in height, or attached with enclosed walls typically requires a building permit — verify current threshold with LADBS for the specific jurisdiction (city of LA vs. LA County vs. other incorporated cities each have their own building department).
- LA sits in Seismic Design Category D — all patio cover foundations, post connections, and ledger attachments to the house must be engineered for seismic load, not just wind/snow; kit systems need their engineering package pulled for permit, not just installed off the shelf.
- HOA design review is common in planned LA communities — many HOAs restrict patio cover height, roof material/color, or require review before any attached structure goes up; confirm before quoting, especially for solid-roof or louvered systems that change the roofline silhouette.
- Attached patio covers close to property lines can trigger setback requirements that vary by zoning district — don't assume the house's existing setback carries over to a new attached structure.
- Louvered systems are heavier and carry real wind/rain load when closed — engineering and footing requirements are more involved than a standard patio cover kit; budget for a structural engineer's stamp on anything beyond a standard dealer-engineered package.
- Electrical (lighting, heaters, ceiling fans, motorized louver controls) run under an attached structure typically needs its own permit and inspection — don't bundle it into the structural permit scope without accounting for it.
