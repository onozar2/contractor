# Bathroom Wet Areas: Tub/Shower Units, Waterproofing Systems & Glass

## The ladder

**Tub/shower enclosure:**
- *Good — one-piece or multi-piece acrylic/fiberglass unit.* Molded surround (Sterling, MAAX, Delta) dropped into a gut-remodel or a straight tub/surround swap. No grout lines to fail, fastest install (1-2 days). Installed: roughly **$3,500-$6,500** for a standard tub/shower combo unit, less for shower-only.
- *Better — tile-over-cement-board shower, standard finish tile.* Cement board (Durock/HardieBacker) substrate, field tile 12x24 porcelain or similar, standard hardware. Installed: roughly **$8,000-$14,000** for an average 5x3 shower, materials-dependent.
- *Best — tile-over-bonded-waterproofing system, large-format or natural stone, curbless/linear drain.* Schluter-KERDI or wedi panel system (see below) with a curbless entry and linear drain, premium tile/stone. Installed: **$15,000-$28,000+**, driven mostly by tile selection and curbless framing/drain work.

**Shower pan / waterproofing system:**
- *Traditional hot-mop pan.* Tar-paper-and-hot-asphalt pan built by a roofer-trade sub, then a mud-set mortar bed on top for slope, tiled over. Time-tested (30+ years in SoCal) but labor-intensive, needs the 48-hour water test before tile (see Playbook 07), and is a dying trade — fewer subs still do it well. Roughly **$1,200-$2,000** as a stand-alone pan system within a larger job.
- *Bonded sheet/panel membrane — Schluter-KERDI or wedi.* KERDI is a fabric membrane bonded directly to a pre-sloped foam tray (KERDI-SHOWER-T) or mortar bed with thin-set, paired with a KERDI-DRAIN; wedi uses waterproof foam panels with integrated sealing tape/collars. Faster install (no 48-hour cure/test wait), consistent slope, works with curbless designs. Roughly **$1,000-$1,800** as a stand-alone system — often *cheaper in total labor* than hot-mop despite higher material cost, because it skips a trade and a multi-day wait.

**Shower glass:**
- *Framed* (aluminum frame all the way around): **$400-$1,100** installed. Budget-friendly, most leak-resistant seal, dated look.
- *Semi-frameless* (metal only at hinges/some edges): **$500-$1,550** installed. The current mid-market default.
- *Frameless, 3/8" glass*: **$600-$1,900** installed depending on size/hardware finish. The standard premium look.
- *Frameless, 1/2" glass*: adds roughly 30-50% to glass cost over 3/8" — used on larger openings, taller panels, or when the client wants maximum "heft."

## How to choose

- Rental/flip/budget remodel, tight timeline → acrylic one-piece unit or tile-over-cement-board with standard tile; framed or semi-frameless glass.
- Primary bath in an owner-occupied home, mid-budget remodel → tile shower with a KERDI or wedi pan (faster, no lost days waiting on a water test) and semi-frameless or 3/8" frameless glass.
- Aging-in-place or accessibility-focused client → curbless entry only works with a bonded membrane system (KERDI/wedi), not hot-mop — flag this early since it changes the framing scope.
- High-end primary suite, large-format porcelain or natural stone, linear drain → bonded system, 1/2" frameless glass.
- Client specifically wants "the old-school way" or has a sub who only hot-mops → traditional pan is still code-legal and durable; just set expectations on the 48-hour test delay.

## Upsell notes

"A one-piece unit gets you in and out fast, but every seam is a future caulk line you'll be re-caulking in a few years — tile has none of that." "The membrane system we use lets your shower floor sit flush with the bathroom floor, no curb to trip over — that's the same waterproofing hospitals use in walk-in showers." "Half-inch glass doesn't wobble when you close the door — it feels like a piece of furniture, not a shower stall."

## Watch out

Acrylic/fiberglass units have size limits — verify they fit through doorways/hallways to the bathroom before selling one on a remodel with no wall demo. Hot-mop pans require the full 48-hour water test before tile per company process (Playbook 07) — do not let a sub skip this. Curbless showers need floor framing coordination (recessed subfloor or linear drain trenching) decided *before* rough plumbing, not after. Natural stone tile is porous and needs sealing — flag ongoing maintenance to the client. Frameless glass on an out-of-plumb or out-of-level wall (common in older SoCal homes) needs field verification before ordering — glass doesn't flex to fit a bad wall the way a framed unit's trim can.
