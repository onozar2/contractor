# HVAC: Efficiency Tiers, Heat Pumps & Rebates

## The ladder

- *Good — 14.3 SEER2 single-stage AC or gas furnace/AC split system.* Minimum code-legal efficiency in the Southwest region (14.3 SEER2 for systems under 45,000 BTU). Single-stage compressor runs at full blast or off — cheapest equipment, least comfort control, shortest equipment life under constant full-power cycling. Installed: roughly **$7,000-$11,000** for a 3-4 ton split system.
- *Better — 16-18 SEER2 two-stage system, or entry heat pump.* Two-stage compressors run at a low and high setting, cutting noise and short-cycling while holding temperature more evenly — this is the practical sweet spot most homeowners land on. A heat pump at this tier (14.3-16 SEER2 minimum for rebate eligibility) replaces both the AC and the furnace with one electric system. Installed: **$11,000-$17,000** for AC/furnace; heat pump systems run similar or slightly more once electrical panel work is included.
- *Best — 18+ SEER2 variable-speed system, or high-efficiency heat pump (20.5+ SEER2 / 9.1+ HSPF2).* Variable-speed compressors modulate output continuously, which is the quietest, most even, most efficient tier and the one that qualifies for the top rebate bracket. Installed: **$16,000-$26,000+** depending on ductwork condition and zoning.
- *Ductless mini-split (any efficiency tier, for additions/ADUs).* Wall or ceiling cassette units with no ductwork required — the default answer whenever a room or ADU isn't tied into the main system's ducts. Installed: **$4,000-$9,000 per zone** (single head); multi-zone systems scale from there.

## How to choose

- Cash-tight rehab/flip or a system swap with no other work triggering upgrades → 14.3-16 SEER2 single- or two-stage, gas furnace/AC — meets code, keeps the bid competitive.
- Owner-occupied home where the client will live with the system daily → two-stage 16-18 SEER2 is the value sweet spot: real comfort/noise improvement over single-stage without variable-speed pricing.
- Client asking about rebates, going electric, or replacing an aging gas furnace → heat pump conversion — check current LADWP/SoCalGas/SCE rebate status before quoting a number (see Watch Out).
- ADU, garage conversion, or room addition not on the main ductwork → ductless mini-split, full stop; running new ducts to an isolated space is almost always more expensive and less efficient than a dedicated mini-split head.
- Noise-sensitive household (light sleepers, home office, upstairs bedrooms over living space) → variable-speed, regardless of budget tier — this is the single biggest reason to step up.

## Upsell notes

"Single-stage is either full blast or off — that's the unit you hear kick on and blast cold air, then shut off completely. Two-stage runs at a lower gear most of the time, so it's quieter and holds the temperature steadier instead of swinging." "A heat pump does both jobs — heating and cooling — off one system, and with the SoCal climate it's rarely working hard in either direction, so it's efficient nearly year-round." "A mini-split means we're not tearing into your walls and ceiling to run new ductwork to the addition — one small line set, and it's its own independently controlled zone."

## Watch out

**Rebates are volatile — verify current status before quoting a dollar figure to a client.** As of mid-2026: the federal 25C tax credit (up to $2,000 for qualifying heat pumps) expired December 31, 2025, and is not available for 2026 installs. TECH Clean California and HEEHRA state/federal rebate programs are reported fully reserved for single-family homes as of early 2026 (waitlist only). LADWP's own heat pump rebate (roughly $2,500/ton, requiring minimum 15.2 SEER2/7.7 HSPF2) is separately funded and was reported active as of mid-2026 — this is the rebate most likely to still apply to an LA job, but confirm current terms on ladwp.com before promising a number. **Verify current rebate program status before every quote** — these programs open, close, and change eligibility tiers with little notice.

Title 24 (2025 standards, effective January 1, 2026) requires duct insulation at R-6 or R-8 depending on LA's climate zone, and tightened maximum duct leakage to 5% for single-family homes — any duct replacement or new HVAC install on a permitted job needs to hit this or fail inspection. Heat pumps lose capacity in cold weather, which is rarely a real issue in coastal/basin LA but can matter in higher-elevation or inland-valley microclimates — size for the site, not a generic assumption. Variable-speed and heat pump systems require a compatible, often proprietary thermostat — don't mix brands without checking compatibility first.
