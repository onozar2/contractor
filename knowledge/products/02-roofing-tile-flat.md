# Roofing: Tile & Flat Roofing

## The ladder

**Good — Concrete tile.** Eagle Roofing (Capistrano high-S profile, Malibu low-profile), US Tile by Boral (clay look in concrete, lighter than real clay), or Westile/Boral concrete lines. Installed roughly **$900–$1,800/square** ($9–$18/sq ft) in SoCal, common 50-yr material warranties. Volume choice for Spanish/Mediterranean tract and custom homes across the LA basin.

**Better/Best — Clay tile.** Fired-clay barrel or flat profiles (Eagle, Boral clay lines — verify current SKU availability; some colors have had extended backorders since the 2022 Westlake acquisition). Color is fired through the body, not surface-coated, so it won't fade like a paint/slurry coat. Installed roughly **$1,500–$3,500/square** ($15–$35/sq ft) — a genuine step above concrete. Tier for high-end Mediterranean/Spanish Colonial restorations and hillside estates.

**Flat/low-slope systems** (garage roofs, additions, modern flat-roof homes):
- **Torch-down modified bitumen (APP):** heat-welded cap sheet, proven low-slope workhorse. **$400–$700/square** ($4–$7/sq ft); complex applications up to $900.
- **SBS modified bitumen:** torched, hot-mopped, or cold-adhered — more installer flexibility. **$350–$650/square**.
- **TPO membrane:** single-ply, heat-welded seams, reflective white cap sheet (helps Title 24 cool-roof compliance). **$400–$1,000/square** depending on mil thickness (45 vs 60) and insulation.

## How to choose

- Spanish/Mediterranean tract home, budget-conscious but wants the tile look → concrete (Eagle Malibu or Capistrano).
- Hillside estate, historic restoration, or client insisting on "real clay" or fade-proof color → clay tile; set expectations on lead time given recent backorders.
- Garage roof, addition, or modern flat-roof design → torch-down or TPO; TPO wins when cool-roof reflectance is in play.
- Roof with heavy penetrations/HVAC or complex flashing → SBS or torch-down (easier detail work) over TPO's heat-welded seams.
- Re-roof where the structure already carries tile → stay in tile; don't suggest a concrete-to-clay switch without an engineer's sign-off on point loading.

## Upsell notes

- Clay vs. concrete: "Color is baked all the way through — it won't chalk or fade the way a concrete color coat eventually does."
- Tile vs. shingle: "This roof will likely outlast the house — tile commonly runs 50+ years versus 20–30 for shingle."
- TPO vs. torch-down: "The white reflective membrane keeps that flat section cooler in summer, especially if it's over a bedroom or living space."

## Watch out

- Tile is heavy — concrete roughly 900–1,100 lbs/square, clay varies by profile. Any re-roof changing tile type, or adding tile to a structure built for shingle, needs a structural check before quoting.
- Broken tile during related work (solar, chimney flashing) is a common change order — matching discontinued/faded tile is often the hard part, not labor.
- Torch-down (open flame) carries fire-watch and permit implications in the high-fire-hazard-severity zones common across LA hillside communities — cold-applied SBS or TPO sidesteps the open-flame issue; confirm current fire-zone rules before committing to torch-down on a hillside job.
- TPO and mod-bit systems aren't interchangeable mid-repair — TPO seams are hot-air welded and membrane-specific; patching TPO with asphaltic materials (or vice versa) will fail adhesion.
- Title 24 cool-roof reflectance applies to low-slope re-roofs too, with its own thresholds — confirm CRRC listing on the specific cap-sheet product and color before promising code compliance.
