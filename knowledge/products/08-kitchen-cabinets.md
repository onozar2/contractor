# Kitchen Cabinets

## The ladder

**Good — Stock cabinets, particleboard box, refacing option:** Stock cabinets run ~$80-120/linear ft installed (big-box lines like Home Depot's in-house brands or IKEA SEKTION). Boxes are typically particleboard or MDF with a laminate/thermofoil skin; framed construction is common at this tier. If the existing boxes are structurally sound, **refacing** (new doors/drawer fronts + veneer over the existing box) runs ~$150-450/linear ft or roughly $5,000-13,000 for an average kitchen — 50-70% cheaper than full replacement, and the fastest path to a visual refresh.

**Better — Semi-custom, plywood box, frameless or framed:** ~$150-300/linear ft installed. This is where named brands live: **Fabuwood** (Allure series — all-plywood boxes, Blum hardware standard, 5-business-day lead times, now with a SoCal distribution center that cuts freight) and **Waypoint Living Spaces** (American-made, more wood species and finish options, positioned slightly above Fabuwood). Both offer soft-close as standard or a one-line upgrade, and enough size/finish modularity to fit most LA kitchen layouts without a full custom shop's price tag.

**Best — Full custom, local cabinet shop:** ~$500-800/linear ft installed. Built to the exact kitchen (no filler panels, exact dimensions, unlimited wood species/finish/door-style choices). Worth it for non-standard layouts, high-end finish work, or integrated built-ins (banquettes, matching furniture pieces) that off-the-shelf lines can't do.

## How to choose

- Boxes are square, doors are just dated, and the layout works → reface, not replace; biggest visual impact per dollar.
- Standard kitchen footprint, mid-range remodel budget, wants name-brand plywood construction → Fabuwood/Waypoint-tier semi-custom.
- Rental/investment property or a fast flip → stock/particleboard tier; it won't be lived with long enough to matter.
- Non-standard layout (odd angles, tall ceilings needing stacked upper cabinets, integrated appliance panels) or a design-forward client → custom shop; stock/semi-custom modularity runs out fast on unusual kitchens.
- High-use household (kids, frequent cooking, resale in 5-10 years) → semi-custom or better, specifically for the plywood box and soft-close hardware — particleboard swells at water exposure points (under the sink, dishwasher) over time.

## Upsell notes

"Particleboard is fine until it gets wet — under the sink, near the dishwasher, that seam swells and the door stops closing right. Plywood just doesn't do that." On frameless vs framed: "Frameless gives you a bigger opening on every cabinet, so pots and pull-out shelves actually use the full space instead of fighting a face frame." On soft-close: "Every door and drawer closes itself, quietly — no slamming, no wear on the box over ten years of daily use."

## Watch out

- **Framed vs frameless (European) construction** is a structural choice, not just style — frameless needs precise leveling and shimming during install (less tolerance for an out-of-square wall), while framed is more forgiving on older LA homes with settled/uneven walls.
- **Soft-close hinges/slides are a real quality signal, not just a feature checkbox** — name-brand hardware (Blum, and Grass as a close second) is cycle-tested past 200,000 opens; unbranded soft-close hardware commonly fails at 20,000-30,000 cycles (roughly 3 years in an active kitchen) and fails hard — verify current spec / brand on any "soft-close included" line before quoting it as a selling point.
- Refacing only works if the boxes are structurally sound and square — water damage, warping, or particleboard swelling under the sink usually means refacing isn't viable and it's a replacement conversation instead.
- Semi-custom lead times (Fabuwood's SoCal distribution center helps) are still weeks, not days — set expectations against the overall project schedule, especially if cabinets gate countertop templating.
- IKEA-tier stock cabinets use a different mounting/hardware system than North American cabinet lines — factor this in if a client already owns IKEA boxes and wants a partial addition or repair.
