# Driveways & Hardscape

## The ladder

**Base prep (applies at every tier — this is what actually prevents cracking/heaving in LA's expansive clay soils):** excavate to design depth, compact native subgrade, then place and compact a Class II road base (crushed aggregate) — 4" minimum under a standard residential driveway, 6-8" in known clay-heavy areas (much of the Valley, parts of the Westside) or anywhere a vehicle will regularly load the slab. Compact in 2" lifts to 90-95% relative compaction. Skimping the base is the #1 cause of callback cracks — sell the base spec, not just the surface.

- **Good — broom-finish concrete:** $8-15/sf installed for a standard reinforced (rebar or wire mesh) slab in the LA market; add control joints every 8-10 ft to manage cracking. This is the baseline — clean, durable, and the cheapest way to close a driveway or walkway.
- **Better — stamped/decorative concrete:** $8-26/sf installed; a realistic mid-project number for LA is $15-20/sf for a stamped pattern (ashlar slate, cobblestone, wood-plank) with integral or acid-stain color. Same structural base as broom-finish, dressed up with a pattern and color coat.
- **Better/Best — 60mm concrete pavers (Belgard, Angelus):** material runs $4-8/sf for Angelus lines, $6-14/sf for Belgard lines; with base prep, edge restraint, and labor, installed pricing lands around $16-28/sf for a driveway-rated (60mm minimum thickness, vehicular-load-rated) install. Interlocking pavers over a proper aggregate base flex instead of cracking, and individual pavers can be lifted and replaced (utility trenching, oil stains) without patching a slab. Verify current SKU/thickness — some decorative paver lines are 40mm and NOT rated for vehicle loads; only spec 60mm+ for driveways.
- **Best — travertine pavers:** installed pricing in the LA market runs roughly $18-30+/sf depending on pattern (French pattern vs. uniform), finish (tumbled vs. honed), and base complexity. This is the premium natural-stone tier — cooler underfoot, doesn't hold heat like concrete, and reads as high-end from the curb, but it's a softer stone that needs sealing and is pricier to repair if stained or cracked.

**Artificial turf (related line item, not a driveway product but frequently sold alongside hardscape jobs — parkways, side yards, pet runs):** $8-14/sf installed for a mid-grade residential product; premium/pet-turf blends run $12-18/sf. Look for face weight in the 50-70 oz/yd² range for a full, realistic residential lawn (verify current spec — face weight is sometimes marketed per sf, but the industry-standard unit is oz per square yard, so confirm units on any product a supplier quotes). Backing should be a polyurethane or dual-layer latex/poly backing with hole-punch or flow-through drainage holes rated for adequate drainage — this matters in LA's occasional heavy-rain events. Class 6-8" of decomposed granite or crushed rock base under turf, same logic as hardscape base prep.

## How to choose

- Rental/investment property, tight budget, or a driveway that's purely functional → broom-finish concrete.
- Homeowner who wants curb appeal on a budget, especially a flip or a listing prep → stamped/stained concrete.
- Homeowner planning to stay long-term, wants a repairable surface, or has underground utilities/irrigation likely to need future access → 60mm pavers.
- High-end remodel, Mediterranean/Spanish-style or luxury modern homes where the driveway is part of the front-of-house design statement → travertine.
- Any client complaining about mowing, water bills, or a shady lawn that won't grow → turf, pitched as a hardscape add-on alongside the driveway/patio scope.

## Upsell notes

"Poured concrete is going to crack eventually — it's just a question of when, and once it does you're either living with the crack or repouring the whole slab. Pavers are individual pieces sitting on a compacted base, so they flex with ground movement instead of fracturing, and if one ever gets stained or damaged we pull that one piece and drop in a new one — no patch, no color mismatch." For travertine: "This is the same stone you see on high-end hotel entries — it doesn't absorb heat the way concrete does, so it's actually cooler to walk on barefoot in summer."

## Watch out

- LA has expansive clay soils in large swaths of the Valley and inland areas — under-spec'd base depth is the single biggest source of cracking complaints; don't quote the minimum base on a site with known clay without a soils conversation.
- Driveway aprons (the section between the sidewalk and street) fall under LADBS/public-works jurisdiction, not just private property — replacing or widening an apron typically requires a separate permit and inspection.
- Pavers marketed for patios/walkways are often 40mm thick and not vehicle-rated — always confirm 60mm+ before quoting a driveway in pavers; verify current spec/thickness with the specific Belgard or Angelus line before ordering.
- Grading/drainage: any driveway or hardscape project that changes impervious surface coverage or redirects runoff toward a neighboring property can trigger stormwater/drainage review — flag this on larger regrades.
- HOA approval is common in LA's planned communities for anything that changes the driveway material or front-yard turf — confirm HOA design-review requirements before ordering material, especially for turf (some HOAs restrict or ban synthetic turf in front yards).
- Turf drainage: a poorly prepped or non-perforated base under turf will pond water after LA's infrequent but heavy winter rains — always spec a permeable base and drainage-rated backing.
