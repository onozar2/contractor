# Insulation

## The ladder

**By material, installed (per square foot of coverage area, LA market):**
- **Good — fiberglass batt** (Owens Corning / Johns Manville standard batts): ~$1.50-$3.00/sq ft installed. Fast, cheap, works well in open unobstructed attics/walls with standard framing bays. Loses R-value fast if compressed or gapped around wiring/plumbing.
- **Better — blown-in / loose-fill** (fiberglass or cellulose, machine-blown): ~$2.00-$4.00/sq ft installed. Fills irregular attic framing and gets into gaps batts miss; the standard retrofit choice for topping up an existing attic without opening walls.
- **Best — spray foam**, open-cell (~$1.50-$3.00/sq ft, expands to fill cavity, some vapor permeability, good sound-deadening) or closed-cell (~$3.00-$7.00/sq ft, higher R-per-inch, adds structural rigidity and a vapor/air barrier in one step, better in below-grade or moisture-prone assemblies). Best air-sealing performance of any option, which is most of what actually drives comfort and utility savings on an older LA house with leaky top plates and can lights.

**R-value by assembly (spec to hit these, tier follows from which material gets you there in the available cavity depth):**
- Attic: R-30 (older/shallow-truss retrofit floor), R-38 (standard 2025 Title 24 baseline for most LA climate zones), R-49 (deep-truss new construction or "go big" upsell in a hot-attic house).
- Walls: R-13 (older 2x4 cavity, batt-only), R-15+R-5 continuous rigid or R-21 in a 2x6 cavity (current Title 24 mandatory minimum framing), R-21+ with closed-cell foam where the cavity allows.
- Floors (over garage, crawlspace, or vented space): R-19 standard, R-30 upgrade in a cold-floor complaint house.

## How to choose

- Older LA house (pre-1978) with an accessible, open attic and no rewiring planned → blown-in fiberglass/cellulose is the fast, cost-effective topper to get to R-38/R-49.
- New construction, addition, or any wall opened up for other work (rewire, replumb, window replacement) → this is the only cheap window to hit wall R-values (R-21) that are otherwise nearly impossible to retrofit into a closed 2x4 wall — always flag it as a now-or-never upsell.
- Client complaint is "one room is always hot/cold" or there's an attic with visible daylight/gaps around can lights and top plates → spray foam (open-cell) for the air-sealing, not just R-value — batts alone won't fix a drafty room.
- Garage conversion (ADU) or below-grade/crawlspace work where moisture or vapor control matters → closed-cell spray foam, it's doing double duty as insulation and vapor barrier.
- Budget-constrained rental/flip where nobody's chasing peak efficiency → standard fiberglass batt to code minimum, cheapest path to pass inspection.

## Upsell notes

"Batts only work if they sit perfectly flat in the cavity — any gap around a wire or a light fixture and you lose the R-value right there. Blown-in fills around all of that instead of just draping over it." For spray foam: "Insulation slows heat moving through the material — it doesn't stop air just leaking straight through gaps. Foam does both in one pass, which is why houses with foam actually feel different, not just test different." For the wall-opening moment: "This is the one time it's cheap to insulate this wall — once we close it back up, getting insulation in there again means cutting holes in your finished wall."

## Watch out

- California Title 24 (2025 Energy Code) sets a mandatory minimum wall cavity insulation of R-15 (2x4 framing) or R-21 (2x6 framing) regardless of climate zone — this is a floor, not a target, and applies to any permitted wall work. Prescriptive attic and whole-assembly U-factor targets are climate-zone- and building-type-specific (pulled from Title 24 Table 140.3-B) — verify current spec against the CEC's address/ZIP lookup tool before finalizing a permitted job; most of LA falls in Climate Zone 8 (coastal/Long Beach basin) or Climate Zone 9 (Hollywood/Burbank/San Fernando Valley), and the two zones can carry different prescriptive attic R-value targets.
- Knob-and-tube wiring (still present in some pre-1960s LA housing stock) generally cannot be buried in insulation per fire code — flag for an electrician before insulating over it, don't just blow foam or fill over an unknown older attic.
- Closed-cell spray foam on an unvented ("hot") roof assembly can trigger code requirements for an ignition barrier or thermal barrier over the foam (drywall or approved coating) — don't leave it exposed in habitable or attic-storage space.
- Recessed can lights: only IC-rated (insulation contact rated) fixtures can be directly covered by insulation; non-IC cans need a clearance box or need to be swapped before insulating over them.
- Existing knob-and-tube aside, always check for vermiculite (potential asbestos) in older attic insulation before disturbing it — do not blow new insulation on top without addressing/testing suspect material first.
