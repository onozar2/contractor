# Rain Gutters

## The ladder

**Good — 5" K-style aluminum, .027 gauge.** Standard seamless aluminum, formed on-site from coil stock (no seams except at corners/joints). .027" is common contractor-grade thickness for moderate roof loads. Installed roughly **$6–$9/linear foot**.

**Better — 6" K-style aluminum, .027–.032 gauge.** Wider trough for bigger roof areas or higher-intensity rain; adds **$1–$5/linear foot** over 5". Bumping to .032" gauge is a heavier-duty upgrade for large roof planes or homes wanting a sturdier feel (less denting) — call it "premium gauge," not just a size change. Installed roughly **$8–$14/linear foot**.

**Best — Copper (sectional or seamless).** A real architectural upgrade, not just capacity. Sectional runs **$18–$30/linear foot**; seamless **$30–$40/linear foot**. Develops natural patina (or can be left bright/sealed). Lifespan commonly cited at 50–100 years vs. 20–25 for aluminum.

**Add-on — Leaf/gutter guards.** Basic screen/mesh inserts: **$0.40–$5.70/linear foot** material. Branded reverse-curve systems (LeafGuard-style, integrated hood replaces the gutter itself): **$15–$45/linear foot** installed. Mid-tier brands (Master Shield, similar): roughly **$15–$21/linear foot**.

## How to choose

- Standard single-story tract home, modest roof planes → 5", .027 aluminum; no need to upsell size.
- Two-story homes, steep pitches, or large connected roof planes feeding one run → 6", and check downspout sizing alongside gutter width, not instead of it.
- Homes near heavy tree canopy (older LA neighborhoods like Hancock Park, Los Feliz, Pasadena foothills) → lead with a guard-system conversation; clogged gutters are the #1 callback driver.
- Architecturally significant or high-end custom homes (Spanish/Mediterranean, Craftsman) where copper reads as "correct" → copper, framed as design/longevity, not just drainage.
- Documented overflow, fascia rot, or foundation-adjacent ponding → a downspout/capacity problem first, not a "nicer gutters" problem — fix sizing before upselling material.

## Upsell notes

- 6" vs 5": "More roof, more rain, same gutter — that's what's causing the overflow you're seeing at the corners in a real storm."
- .032 gauge: "Thicker aluminum won't dent from ladders or debris the way the thinner stuff does — it holds its shape for the life of the house."
- Copper: "Same material used on landmark buildings — it'll outlast two or three aluminum replacements and ages into a look people pay extra to fake."
- Leaf guards: "You're probably up on that roof twice a year cleaning these out — this is the last time you clean them."

## Watch out

- Downspout sizing is usually the real bottleneck, not gutter width: roughly one 2x3 downspout per 600 sq ft of roof area per inch of rainfall/hour, or one 3x4 per 1,200 sq ft; space downspouts every 25–35 linear feet, never more than 40 feet apart. A 6" gutter feeding an undersized 2x3 downspout won't stop overflow — verify current spec/local rainfall-intensity numbers for a formal calc.
- Copper cannot directly contact untreated aluminum, galvanized steel, or other dissimilar metals (flashing, fasteners) without isolation — galvanic corrosion eats the lesser metal. Flag copper quotes for a flashing-material check.
- Copper against stucco/masonry can cause staining (verdigris runoff) over time — mention proactively so it isn't a surprise in year three.
- Reverse-curve guard systems generally require full gutter replacement, not a retrofit onto existing gutters — price as a system swap, not an add-on.
- Some LA-area HOA guidelines restrict gutter color/visibility or copper use on street-facing elevations — check CC&Rs before quoting copper or visible guards in HOA neighborhoods.
