# Solar & Battery

## The ladder

**Panels, installed per watt (whole-system price, LA market, 2025-2026):**
- **Good — mainstream/value tier**: Qcells, Canadian Solar, or similar N-type TOPCon panels (~20-21.5% efficiency, US-assembled Qcells lines are common on this tier now): roughly **$2.40-$3.00/watt** installed before incentives. A typical 7-8 kW residential system runs ~$18,000-$24,000 before any rebate.
- **Better — REC Alpha Pure-RX**: ~22%+ efficiency, strong reputation for durability and a stronger-than-average warranty: roughly **$2.80-$3.25/watt** installed.
- **Best — premium/high-efficiency tier**: Maxeon 7 (formerly SunPower's panel line) at ~24% efficiency with a 40-year warranty, or Panasonic-class premium modules: roughly **$3.50-$4.50/watt** installed. Worth it mainly on roofs with limited usable area where every watt/sq ft matters, or for clients who want the longest warranty on the market.

**Inverter architecture (add to any panel tier):**
- **String inverter** (SolarEdge/Enphase-competing central inverter with power optimizers): lower cost, fine for simple roofs with one orientation and no shading.
- **Microinverters (Enphase IQ8-series)**: inverter per panel, panel-level monitoring, and the system keeps producing even if one panel is shaded or fails — the standard recommendation for LA roofs with dormers, chimneys, or multiple roof faces. Modest cost premium, usually bundled into the per-watt price above rather than quoted separately.

**Battery, installed (per unit, add to any solar system or as a standalone backup retrofit):**
- **Good/entry — Enphase IQ Battery 5P**: 5.0 kWh usable per unit, stacks (most installs use 2 units = 10 kWh, 3 units = 15 kWh); ~$8,000-$12,000 installed for a 2-unit (10 kWh) setup.
- **Better — Tesla Powerwall 3**: 13.5 kWh usable, 100% depth of discharge, 11.5 kW continuous output (strong for running AC/well pumps during an outage); ~$12,000-$16,000 installed for one unit, 10-year warranty.
- **Best — FranklinWH aPower 2**: 15.0 kWh usable per cabinet (most storage per unit of the three), 10 kW continuous / 15 kW surge, 15-year warranty; ~$14,000-$18,000 installed. The pick when the client wants maximum whole-home backup from the fewest cabinets, or has EV charging load to cover overnight.

## How to choose

- Budget-conscious client, simple south-facing roof, standard usage → mainstream TOPCon panel tier (Qcells-class) with a string inverter or basic microinverters.
- Roof with shading from trees, multiple orientations, or a dormer/chimney breaking up the array → microinverters (Enphase IQ8) regardless of panel tier — shading losses on a string system tank the whole string's output, not just the shaded panel.
- Small or steep roof where usable area is the constraint → premium high-efficiency tier (Maxeon/Panasonic-class) to squeeze more kW into less roof.
- Any client asking "will this actually pay for itself" under current rules → battery is not optional messaging anymore, it's the deciding factor in the payback math (see Watch Out) — quote solar+battery as the default, solar-only as the stripped-down option.
- Client wants outage backup for medical equipment, WFH setup, or an EV → size the battery (or stack multiple units) to real backup load, not just the cheapest single-unit option; walk through what actually needs to stay on during an outage.

## Upsell notes

"Under the current rules, the utility barely pays you anything for power you send back to the grid during the day — it's the power you use yourself, especially in the evening, that actually saves you money. A battery is what lets you store the cheap daytime solar and use it instead of buying it back from the utility at night." On panel tier: "The efficiency difference matters most when your roof is the limit — a smaller, better panel gets you the same output as a bigger cheap one, if you don't have roof space to spare." On backup: "This isn't just a green upgrade anymore — if the power goes out, this is what keeps your fridge and internet running instead of everything going dark."

## Watch out

- **NEM 3.0 changed the economics materially.** Under the current net-billing tariff, the utility pays for exported solar at an "avoided cost" rate that varies by hour/day/month — averaging roughly 75% lower than the old NEM 2.0 retail-rate credit (often landing around $0.05-$0.08/kWh vs. the old ~$0.25-$0.30/kWh). Do not quote payback numbers assuming full retail credit for exported power — verify current avoided-cost schedule before running numbers for a client. Solar-only payback under NEM 3.0 is commonly running 9-13 years; solar+battery (using stored power instead of exporting it) commonly runs 7-9 years — battery attach rates have risen sharply since NEM 3.0 took effect specifically because of this math.
- If a client already has an existing NEM 2.0 (or earlier) system, adding a battery does **not** forfeit their grandfathered NEM 2.0 status — this is a real, common client question, and getting it wrong costs them real money. New standalone systems, however, can only enroll under NEM 3.0 today.
- The federal residential solar tax credit (Section 25D, the 30% credit) expired December 31, 2025 — do not promise a federal tax credit on any system placed in service in 2026 or later; verify current federal/state incentive status before quoting.
- Battery sizing should be driven by actual backup-load and time-of-use goals, not just "biggest number" — oversizing wastes budget, undersizing means the battery drains before the discount evening/backup window is over.
- Confirm HOA/historic-district roof restrictions and LADWP (or relevant municipal utility) interconnection timeline before promising an installation date — permitting/utility approval, not the physical install, is usually the long pole.
